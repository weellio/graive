## CONCEPT: Three Types of Fake (0:50–3:00)

**[HOST, graphics]**

"Before AI, fake news was mostly written by humans trying to deceive you. That was already a problem. But now, anyone can generate convincing, professional-sounding text in seconds.

There are three main types of AI-assisted misinformation to know about:

**1. Fabricated facts** — AI invents something that sounds true. Statistics, quotes, events, studies. This is called hallucination when AI does it by accident — but it can also be done *on purpose*.

**2. Fake quotes** — Real person, made-up words. 'According to [famous person]...' — but they never said that. AI can generate these instantly, and they spread fast because people recognise the name.

**3. Deepfakes** — AI-generated images, audio, or video. Photos of events that never happened. Voices of people saying things they never said. This is the most advanced and the most unsettling.

None of this means everything online is fake. But it means **you need a process** before you believe or share something."

---

## THE VERIFICATION FRAMEWORK (3:00–5:30)

**[HOST, animated checklist]**

"Here's my four-question checklist — I call it STOP:

**S — Source: Who published this?**
Not just the website name — dig into it. Is it a known news organisation? Does the site have an 'About' page? When was it created? A site created three weeks ago posting viral political content is a red flag.

**T — Tone: Does it feel designed to make me angry or scared?**
Emotional manipulation is a feature of misinformation, not a bug. If something makes you immediately furious or terrified, that's a signal to slow down, not react. Ask: why does this want me to feel this way?

**O — Other sources: Can I find this reported elsewhere?**
Real news gets covered in multiple places. If a dramatic claim only appears on one site — especially a new or unknown one — it probably hasn't passed any editorial check.

**P — Proof: Is there verifiable evidence?**
Can you find the original study, video, document, or event it's claiming? Or is it just assertions?

**[ANIMATED: STOP graphic]**

Run anything suspicious through STOP before sharing it.

And here's a key habit: **Don't share while angry.** Outrage spreads misinformation faster than anything. If something made you furious and you immediately want to share it — wait ten minutes. Run STOP. Then decide."

## USING AI TO VERIFY (5:30–6:30)

**[HOST, screen share]**

"Here's the twist — you can actually use AI to *help* you verify information. The same tools that can create misinformation can help you detect it.

If you see something suspicious, try this prompt:

> 'I saw this claim: [paste claim]. Help me check it. What would I need to verify this? What are the red flags? What do reputable sources say about this topic?'

The AI won't always know if something specific is true or false — especially recent events. But it can help you think through what to look for and what questions to ask.

It's like having a research partner who knows a lot about how to check sources — even if it doesn't know the specific answer."

**[Live demo: paste a suspicious-sounding claim, show the AI's verification prompts and reasoning]**

## CHALLENGE (6:30–7:15)

**[HOST]**

"Your challenge: Find something online this week that you're not sure about. Could be a news story, a statistic in a post, a claim someone made in a video.

Run it through STOP. Then run it through an AI verification prompt.

Document:
- What was the claim?
- What did STOP tell you?
- What did the AI suggest checking?
- What did you actually find?

Was it true, false, or somewhere in between?"

"AI has made it cheaper and faster to create convincing-sounding content — true or false.

Your defence: **STOP**. Source. Tone. Other sources. Proof.

And use AI as a verification partner, not just a content creator.

See you there."
