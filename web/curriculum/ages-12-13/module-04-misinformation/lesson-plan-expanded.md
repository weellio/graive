# Lesson Plan: Misinformation in the AI Age
### Builder Level (Ages 12–13) | Module 04 — Expanded

---

## About This Lesson

Misinformation is not new. False stories, manipulated images, and propaganda have existed for centuries. But AI has changed misinformation in three important ways that make it a fundamentally different challenge for your generation than for any previous one.

First, the volume. AI tools can produce convincing, varied, plausible-sounding text at near-zero cost and enormous speed. A single person or small group can now flood information spaces with false content that would previously have required large teams and significant resources to produce. This changes the economics of misinformation in a way that will continue to have major effects.

Second, the believability. AI-generated images, audio, and video (called deepfakes) can now reach a level of realism that makes them difficult to distinguish from genuine media even for trained professionals. A realistic-looking photo of a political leader "doing" something they never did, generated in seconds, can spread to millions of people before any fact-check can catch up. This is not a distant future problem — it is happening right now, in elections, in conflicts, and in public discourse.

Third, the two-way problem. Not only can AI be used to create misinformation, but AI language models can also amplify it. If AI training data contained false information — and it did, because the internet contains plenty of false information — then the AI may reproduce those falsehoods confidently. This means the tools we might reach for to check facts can themselves be a source of misinformation. Navigating this requires being thoughtful about which tool you use for what purpose.

The good news is that the same critical thinking skills that help you detect human-written misinformation also apply to AI-generated content, plus a few new layers. The STOP framework — Source, Tone, Other sources, Proof — gives you a systematic method for evaluating any claim regardless of where or how it was generated. These are habits, not tricks. They need to be built over time until they become automatic.

The bigger picture here is about agency. Most people who spread misinformation are not malicious — they are caught off guard by content designed to bypass their critical thinking (usually by triggering emotion before reason kicks in). The people who study misinformation most carefully all say the same thing: slowing down by a few seconds before sharing anything is the single most impactful habit you can build. This lesson gives you the tools to make that pause productive.

## Key Concepts

- **Misinformation:** False or inaccurate information, regardless of intent — it includes honest mistakes as well as deliberate deception.
- **Disinformation:** False information that is deliberately created and spread to deceive people — misinformation with malicious intent.
- **Deepfake:** AI-generated audio, video, or images that realistically portray a person doing or saying something they never did.
- **The STOP framework:** A four-step fact-checking method — Source, Tone, Other sources, Proof — that can be applied to any suspicious claim.
- **Lateral reading:** A fact-checking technique where instead of reading the suspicious source deeply, you open new tabs and look for what other reliable sources say about the original source.
- **Emotional bypass:** The mechanism by which misinformation works — triggering a strong emotion (outrage, fear, excitement) that causes people to share before thinking critically.

---

## How It Works

The most effective misinformation is designed to bypass your critical thinking by triggering a strong emotional response first. This is not a conspiracy — it is basic human psychology. When you feel a surge of outrage, fear, or excitement, your brain is flooded with chemicals that activate fast, reactive thinking and suppress the slower, more careful reasoning you would normally apply. Misinformation that makes you feel something strongly is misinformation you are far more likely to share before checking.

AI amplifies this in two ways. First, AI tools can help creators A/B test emotional triggers at scale — generating thousands of variations of a false story and testing which version gets the most engagement before pushing that version to mass audiences. Second, AI-generated images and video reach a level of realism that activates a sense of "I can see it, so it must be real" — a deeply human bias that previous generations never had to consciously override.

The STOP framework works precisely because it inserts a structured pause between your emotional reaction and your decision to share. Each step is a deliberate interruption of the emotional bypass. The habit is not complicated — the difficulty is remembering to do it when you are already mid-emotional-response.

## Real World Examples

**1. The AI-generated viral image of Pope Francis in a puffer jacket (2023)**
In early 2023, an AI-generated image showing Pope Francis wearing a designer white puffer jacket went viral on social media, with millions of people initially believing it was real. It was created using the AI image tool Midjourney. This was one of the first widely documented cases of an AI image being broadly mistaken for a real photograph — and it showed that even a benign, implausible-seeming image could fool a large population.

**2. Deepfake audio in political campaigns**
During multiple election cycles in 2023–2024, deepfake audio clips imitating the voices of political candidates were circulated on social media, portraying them as saying things they never said. In some cases, hundreds of thousands of people heard the audio before fact-checkers were able to identify it as fake. AI voice cloning tools can now create convincing imitations from just a few seconds of real audio.

**3. AI-generated "news sites"**
Researchers have documented hundreds of websites that appear to be local news outlets but are actually automated AI content farms producing fake or misleading stories at scale. These sites often have realistic-looking names, About pages, and articles, but their content is fabricated or heavily slanted. They are designed to appear in search results and to look credible at a glance — precisely the kind of source STOP's Source check is designed to catch.

## Try It — Fact Check a Real Claim

### Step 1: Find a Claim to Check (10 mins)

Go to a social media platform, news aggregator, or Google News. Find ONE claim that seems interesting or surprising. It could be:

- A statistic ("Did you know 90% of people...")
- A news headline about something dramatic or unexpected
- A quote attributed to a famous person
- A health/science claim ("Study finds...")

Copy the claim into your notebook exactly as you found it. Also note where you found it (which website/platform).

**Do not choose something obviously fake or satirical.** Pick something that genuinely made you think "hm, is that true?"

---

### Step 2: Run the STOP Check (15 mins)

Go through each letter:

**S — Source**
- Who published this originally?
- Is this a known publication with a track record?
- When was the site/account created?
- Does it have an About page? Who runs it?

Notes: _______________________

**T — Tone**
- Does it make you feel angry, scared, or outraged?
- Does it use emotionally charged language?
- Is it trying to persuade you strongly rather than inform you?
- How is that making you feel right now?

Notes: _______________________

**O — Other Sources**
- Can you find this story or claim on at least 2 other sources?
- Are those sources independent (different organisations, not just re-posting each other)?
- What do the most trusted news sources you know say about this topic?

Notes: _______________________

**P — Proof**
- Is there a direct link to the original study, video, document, or event?
- If it's a quote — can you find the original video or transcript?
- If it's a statistic — where did that number come from?

Notes: _______________________

**Your STOP verdict:** TRUE / PROBABLY TRUE / UNCERTAIN / PROBABLY FALSE / FALSE

---

### Step 3: Use AI to Verify (10 mins)

Now use an AI tool to help you dig deeper. Use this prompt:

```
I'm fact-checking a claim. The claim is:
"[PASTE YOUR CLAIM HERE]"

Please help me:
1. Identify what I would need to verify this claim
2. List any red flags in how the claim is worded
3. Tell me what reliable sources would say about this topic (general knowledge — you may not know this specific story)
4. Suggest 2–3 search queries I could use to investigate further
```

Write down what the AI tells you. Then try the suggested search queries.

**Did the AI help you reach a better conclusion? Where was it useful? Where did it fall short?**

---

### Step 4: Write Your Verdict (5 mins)

Write a short fact-check summary (4–6 sentences):
- What the claim was
- Where it came from
- What you found when you checked it
- Your conclusion (true, false, misleading, unverifiable)
- What someone should know before sharing this

---

### Step 5: The Emotional Trigger Audit (10 mins) — NEW

Now go back to the original claim you checked. This time, do not focus on whether it is true — focus on how it was designed to make you feel.

Answer these questions about the content:

1. **What emotion did this content aim to trigger first?** (Options: anger, fear, excitement, pride, disgust, sadness, hope — there may be more than one)
2. **On a scale of 1–10, how strong was that emotional hit?** A "10" means you felt it before you had time to think.
3. **Did the emotional response make you more or less likely to check before sharing?** Be honest.
4. **If the same information were presented in a completely neutral, factual tone, how much less viral would it be?** Why?

Then find one more piece of content in your feed — not to fact-check, just to do the emotional trigger audit. Is it designed to make you feel something before you think something?

This skill — identifying emotional engineering in content — is one of the most valuable things you can develop as a consumer of digital media.

---

### Step 6: Deepfake Detection Practice (10 mins) — NEW

This activity does not require you to create anything. It requires you to look carefully.

Search online for "deepfake detection tips" or "how to spot AI-generated images." Read at least two sources and compile a list of the most useful visual tells. Common examples include:
- Unnatural blurring at edges of hair or skin
- Inconsistent lighting (shadows not matching the light source)
- Hands with incorrect numbers of fingers or impossible joint angles
- Background elements that do not make spatial sense
- Teeth or eyes that look slightly "off"

Then find three images online (from social media, news sites, or an image search) and apply your detection checklist to each one. For each image write:
- What you noticed
- Your confidence that it is real or AI-generated
- What you would do before using or sharing this image

Remember: even trained experts get fooled. The goal is not perfect detection — it is developing the habit of pausing and checking before assuming an image is real.

## Level Up — Spot the Fake

This is a creative challenge. Use an AI tool to generate three short news-style paragraphs about made-up events. Make them *plausible-sounding* (not obviously ridiculous).

Then share them with a family member or friend and ask them to guess which is real.

After the experiment, discuss: what made the fake ones seem believable? What gave them away?

**Reflection: What did building fake content teach you about detecting it?**

Note: The point of this exercise is understanding *how* misinformation works so you can spot it — not to deceive anyone for real.

**Extend it:** After the reveal, ask your family member or friend: which fake felt most believable? Try to redesign that fake to be even more convincing — then explain, in writing, every technique you used to make it work. What does that teach you about what makes misinformation effective?

## Reflect

1. Before this module, did you ever share something online without checking if it was true? No judgement — just honest reflection.

2. The STOP framework takes maybe 5 minutes. What stops people from using it before sharing? What would make it easier?

3. Is it worse to accidentally share something false, or to never share anything in case it's wrong? Where's the line?

4. If AI can generate realistic fake images and audio cheaply and instantly, what does that do to trust in visual media? Is there any image you would trust completely now?

5. Who should be responsible for the spread of AI-generated misinformation — the person who created it, the platform that hosted it, the algorithm that spread it, or the person who shared it? Can it be more than one?
