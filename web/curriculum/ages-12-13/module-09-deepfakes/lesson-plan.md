## Key Concepts

### What Is a Deepfake?
A deepfake is a piece of media — video, audio, image, or text — where AI has been used to make it look or sound like someone did or said something they never actually did.

The name comes from "deep learning" (the AI technique) + "fake."

Examples:
- A video of a politician saying something they never said
- An audio clip of a celebrity endorsing a product they never promoted
- A realistic photo of a person that never existed
- A news article in someone's style containing false information

> "Deepfakes aren't just fake news. They're fake evidence. And they're getting harder to detect every year."

### How Are Deepfakes Made?
Deepfake video tools work roughly like this:
1. The AI is trained on many real videos/photos of the target person
2. It learns the person's facial features, voice patterns, expressions
3. It can then generate new media with the person's face/voice saying or doing anything

More advanced deepfakes require more training data. This is why public figures — celebrities, politicians, athletes — are most commonly targeted.

### Why Are They Dangerous?
- **Political manipulation** — making leaders appear to say inflammatory things before elections
- **Fraud** — fake video/audio of executives authorising payments ("business email compromise" with AI)
- **Harassment** — targeting individuals with fake compromising content
- **Misinformation** — spreading false "evidence" of events that never happened
- **Erosion of trust** — making people doubt ALL video/audio evidence

### The STOP Framework
Before sharing any piece of media that could be a deepfake:

**S — Stop** before sharing. Pause and think.

**T — Think** about whether the content matches what you know about this person. Would they really say this? Does this fit their known views, speech patterns, history?

**O — Origins** — where did this come from? Is it from a verifiable, trustworthy source? Can you trace it to an original?

**P — Prove it** — can you find this content on the subject's own official channels? Can you find multiple credible sources reporting it? If not, be very cautious.

## Try It — Deepfake Detective Activities

### Activity 1: Spot the Signs (15 mins)

Even without technical tools, there are visual and contextual clues that suggest deepfakes. Learn to look for:

**Visual tells (in video/images):**
- Blurry or soft edges around the face
- Unnatural blinking or eye movement
- Hair that behaves strangely
- Odd lighting on the face vs the background
- Teeth that look slightly off
- Facial expressions that don't quite match the emotional content
- Audio that doesn't perfectly sync with lip movements

**Contextual tells:**
- The content seems too perfectly timed (before an election, during a scandal)
- The person is doing or saying something very out of character
- You can only find it on unknown or fringe websites
- It appeared suddenly and is spreading fast

## The Bigger Picture: Truth in an AI World

Here's a deeply unsettling reality: we are entering an era where **seeing is no longer believing.**

Until recently, video was considered strong evidence. Courtrooms used it. Historians relied on it. News used it to show the world what was happening.

AI is changing this fundamentally. In a few years, creating a convincing deepfake may be as easy as typing a description.

> "The danger isn't just fake videos. It's that real videos can now be dismissed as 'probably a deepfake.' AI threatens both what we believe — and what we can prove."

This creates a paradox: if fake videos can look real, AND real videos can be called fake — what do we do?

Think about: what would a world with truly reliable media authentication look like? Who would run it?
