## Key Concepts

### The Spectrum of AI Writing Use
AI writing assistance isn't simply "allowed" or "not allowed." It exists on a spectrum:

```
FULLY LEGITIMATE                                    CLEARLY PROBLEMATIC
       |                                                      |
Using AI     Using AI to    Using AI to     Using AI to    Copying AI
to brainstorm  understand    improve your   write a draft  output word
ideas before   a concept     own draft      you then edit  for word
writing        you'll write  you wrote      heavily
               about
```

Where the line sits depends on:
- What your teacher or school says
- What the assignment is testing
- Whether you're still doing the learning

### The Learning Test
The most important question: **Are YOU still doing the thinking?**

| AI Use | Still learning? | Usually acceptable? |
|---|---|---|
| Ask AI to explain a concept | Yes — you're building understanding | Yes |
| Ask AI to brainstorm topic ideas | Yes — you choose and develop | Usually |
| Ask AI for feedback on your draft | Yes — you decide what to change | Usually |
| Ask AI to improve specific phrases | Borderline — are you understanding the edits? | Depends |
| Ask AI to write your draft | Not really — AI did the composition | Usually not for school |
| Copy AI output without reading it | No — zero learning | No |

> "The test isn't whether AI helped. The test is whether YOU still did the thinking, making, and deciding."

### AI as a Writing Collaborator
The best use of AI for writing is as a **thinking partner**:
- "I want to write about X. What angles could I take?"
- "Here's my thesis. Is there a flaw in this argument?"
- "This paragraph is clunky — what's wrong with it structurally?"
- "What counterarguments would someone make against my position?"
- "I've written this conclusion — does it actually follow from my argument?"

Notice: in all of these, YOU wrote something first, and AI is helping you improve YOUR work.

## Try It — Writing Collaboration Workshop

### Activity 1: Brainstorm Partner (10 mins)

Pick a writing topic — it can be for school or made up:

My topic: ___

Ask the AI: "I need to write about [topic]. I want to find an interesting angle that not everyone would take. What are 5 different angles or perspectives I could write from?"

Read the suggestions. Now here's the important step: **pick one and add your own twist.**

The angle I chose: ___
My twist on it: ___
Why this feels authentic to what I think: ___

---

### Activity 2: Write First, Then Get Feedback (20 mins)

**Step 1:** Write a paragraph (4–7 sentences) on your topic completely on your own. No AI. Put real effort in — it doesn't have to be perfect.

**Step 2:** Paste your paragraph into AI and ask:
- "What's working well in this paragraph?"
- "What's the weakest part?"
- "Give me one specific suggestion for how to make the argument clearer."

**Step 3:** Read the feedback. Decide what to accept and what to ignore.

**Step 4:** Revise the paragraph yourself — don't ask AI to rewrite it.

**Compare your before and after versions:**

Before (paste or summarise): ___
After (paste or summarise): ___
The change I made based on AI feedback: ___
Why I kept my own version of [something]: ___

---

### Activity 3: The Ethics Debate (10 mins)

Read this scenario and answer the questions:

> *Alex is 13 and has a book report due. Alex read the book but found it hard to write about. Alex describes the book's plot to AI and asks it to write a 500-word analysis. Alex reads the AI output, changes a few words, and submits it.*

Questions:

1. Did Alex read the book? Yes. Did Alex do the writing? No. Which matters more for what's being assessed?

2. If the assignment was testing Alex's ability to analyse a book — what did Alex miss out on learning?

3. If you were Alex's teacher and you found out — what would be fair?

4. If Alex had instead asked AI "What are some questions I should ask myself when analysing a book?" and then used those questions to write their own analysis — is that different? How?

My view: ___

## The Disclosure Question
Some schools and publishers are now asking: if you used AI in your work, should you say so?

Arguments for disclosure:
- Honesty builds trust
- People reading your work should know what's human-generated
- Sets healthy norms for AI use

Arguments against mandatory disclosure:
- We don't disclose using a spell-checker
- It might discourage legitimate helpful use
- Hard to enforce consistently

What's your view? Should AI use in writing be disclosed?

## Challenge
**The Revision Challenge:**

Find a piece of writing you did in the past — a school essay, a story, anything.

Use AI as a collaborator to improve it through at least three rounds:
1. Ask AI to identify the three biggest weaknesses
2. Fix those weaknesses yourself (don't ask AI to fix them)
3. Ask AI what's still not working
4. Fix that yourself too
5. Final check: "What's the one most important improvement you'd still make?"

At the end, rate:
- How much better is your final version? ___/10 improvement
- How much of the improvement came from your own decisions vs AI guidance: ___% me / ___% AI
- What did you learn about your own writing from this process: ___
