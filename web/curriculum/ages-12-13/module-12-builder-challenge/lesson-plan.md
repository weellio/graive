## The Challenge — Build Your Subject Toolkit

You are going to create an "AI Toolkit" for one school subject of your choice. The toolkit is a document that a student your age could use to get genuinely better results in that subject with the help of AI.

**Pick your subject now:**
- English / Language Arts
- Maths
- Science (Biology, Chemistry, Physics, or general)
- History
- Geography
- A language you are learning (French, Spanish, etc.)
- Computing / ICT
- Art and Design
- Any other subject you are studying

Your toolkit will have four parts. Work through each one in order.

---

### Part 1: The Prompt Collection (20 mins)

Create at least **six tested prompts** specifically for your chosen subject. Each prompt must:
- Follow the Role + Task + Context + Format + Constraint structure (from Module 08)
- Have been tested — actually run it and confirm the output is useful
- Include a short note explaining when a student would use it

Here is a structure to follow for each prompt:

```
PROMPT NAME: [Give it a memorable title]
WHEN TO USE IT: [Describe the situation — e.g. "When you have a history essay due and need an outline"]
THE PROMPT:
[Full prompt text here, with [BRACKETS] for the parts that change each time]
SAMPLE OUTPUT QUALITY: [Describe briefly — does it work well? Any watch-outs?]
```

To get you started, here are suggested prompt types for different subjects:

**For English:** Essay outline generator, quote analyser, "explain this literary technique", vocabulary expander, feedback on a paragraph I wrote

**For Science:** Concept explainer at different levels, experiment design helper, "explain why this happens", key term definition in plain language, exam question predictor

**For History:** Cause and effect mapper, "explain the significance of [event]", compare two historical figures, timeline builder, essay argument checker

**For Maths:** "Walk me through this type of problem step by step", error finder ("here is my working — find my mistake"), "give me 5 practice questions on [topic]", concept explainer

**For a Language:** Translation and explanation, grammar rule explainer with examples, "write a dialogue using [vocabulary list]", pronunciation description, common mistake checker

---

### Part 2: The Study Cheat Sheet (15 mins)

Create a one-page "cheat sheet" for your subject using AI to help you compile it.

Your cheat sheet should cover the most important concepts, formulas, rules, or facts that a student needs to know. Use AI to help you draft it, then:
- Verify that everything on it is accurate (check against your textbook or a trusted source)
- Add anything important that AI left out
- Organise it clearly with headers and short bullet points

At the top of the cheat sheet, add a note: "AI-assisted — verified by [your name]. Always check against your textbook."

This honesty note matters. It tells anyone using your cheat sheet that AI helped create it and that it has been checked — which is the responsible way to share AI-assisted work.

---

### Part 3: The AI Limitations Warning Card (10 mins)

Your toolkit should also be honest about where AI fails for this specific subject.

Write a short "Warning Card" (one page, or a clear box in your document) that tells users:

1. **Two things AI is particularly good at** for this subject — with a brief explanation of why
2. **Two things AI is unreliable for** in this subject — with an example or explanation
3. **One thing you should NEVER use AI for** in this subject (e.g. final calculations without checking, translating without understanding, science claims without verification)
4. **One golden rule** for using AI safely in this subject

This section shows that you understand AI well enough to know its limits — not just its strengths. That is the mark of a genuine Builder.

---

### Part 4: The Beginner's Guide (15 mins)

Imagine a classmate who has never used AI for school before. Write them a short guide (200–300 words) that explains:

- What AI can help you with in this subject (in plain, friendly language)
- How to write a good prompt (give one worked example)
- What to watch out for (keep it brief — 2–3 warnings)
- How to check if AI got something right

Write this in a conversational, encouraging tone — like you are explaining it to a friend at lunch, not writing an instruction manual.

---

## Design It!

Create a **cover page** for your Subject AI Toolkit.

Include:
- The title: "[Your subject] AI Toolkit — Builder Level"
- Your name as the author
- The date completed
- A brief one-paragraph description of what is inside and who it is for
- A small visual — draw a symbol, logo, or scene that represents your subject combined with AI (e.g. a test tube with a circuit board pattern, a book with a glowing cursor, a maths symbol intertwined with a brain)

This is the final touch that turns your document from a list of prompts into a real toolkit someone would want to pick up and use.

## Final Self-Assessment

Score your completed toolkit:

| Criteria | 1 point | 2 points | 3 points |
|----------|---------|---------|---------|
| Prompt Collection — are prompts specific and tested? | Vague and untested | Some are tested | All tested and specific |
| Cheat Sheet — is it accurate and verified? | Unverified | Partially checked | Fully verified |
| Warning Card — does it show real understanding? | Surface level | Mostly thoughtful | Genuinely insightful |
| Beginner's Guide — could a real beginner use it? | Too technical | Mostly clear | Clear and friendly |
| Overall quality — would you actually use this? | Probably not | Maybe | Definitely yes |

**Total: ___ / 15**

| Score | Builder Rank |
|-------|-------------|
| 13–15 | Master Builder — you are ready for Thinker level |
| 10–12 | Skilled Builder — excellent work |
| 7–9 | Developing Builder — strong effort, polish it further |
| Under 7 | Emerging Builder — the process matters, keep going |

## Reflect
Answer these questions out loud or write them down:

1. You have now spent 12 modules building your AI skills. What is the single most useful thing you have learned, and why?

## Share (Optional)
This one is worth doing: Share your toolkit — or at least one part of it — with a real person. Give it to a classmate, show a teacher, email it to a parent. Ask them: "Is this useful? What is missing? What would you change?" Real feedback from a real person is worth more than any self-assessment rubric.

If you are proud of it, consider asking a parent to help you share it with your class or year group. You made something genuinely useful — that is worth celebrating.
