# Lesson Plan: AI in the News
### Builder Level (Ages 12–13) | Module 11

---

## Objective
By the end of this lesson, you can read an AI-related news headline or article critically — separating genuine developments from hype, identifying who benefits from each narrative, and forming your own informed opinion rather than borrowing someone else's.

---

## What You'll Need
- A device with internet access
- A free AI tool (ChatGPT free or Claude free)
- Access to Google News or a news website
- Paper and coloured pens OR a digital document
- About 40 minutes

---

## Watch First
Watch **Module 11: AI in the News** with a parent or on your own.

Remember: Almost every AI news story has an angle — a company wanting investment, a journalist wanting clicks, a politician wanting votes, or a researcher wanting funding. Reading critically means asking: who wrote this, and what do they gain from you believing it?

## Try It — Read Like a Journalist

### Activity 1: The Hype-O-Meter (15 mins)

AI news tends to cluster around a few repeating story types. Learning to recognise these types helps you instantly calibrate how seriously to take them.

Here are the five most common AI story types:

**Type 1 — The Breakthrough Announcement**
"AI beats human experts at X for the first time!"
*What to ask:* What is the actual test it passed? Is that test a fair measure of real-world ability? Has the result been independently verified?

**Type 2 — The Job Fear Story**
"AI will replace 40% of jobs within 10 years"
*What to ask:* Who produced this prediction — a tech company, a consultancy, a researcher? What is their track record on previous predictions? What does "replace" mean — fully, or change the role?

**Type 3 — The Capability Exaggeration**
"AI develops feelings / becomes conscious / understands humans"
*What to ask:* Is this peer-reviewed science or a press release? What does "understands" actually mean in technical terms? Who gains from this claim?

**Type 4 — The Doom Story**
"AI will destroy democracy / cause mass unemployment / end civilisation"
*What to ask:* Is this a specific, near-term risk with evidence, or a distant hypothetical? Who is making the claim — what is their background and agenda?

**Type 5 — The Miracle Solution**
"AI will cure cancer / solve climate change / end poverty"
*What to ask:* What specific problem is it solving, and what stage is the research at? Is this peer-reviewed? Has it been tested in the real world?

**Your task:** Go to Google News and search "artificial intelligence" or "AI". Find five recent headlines. For each one:
1. Write the headline
2. Identify which of the five story types it is (or create a new type if none fit)
3. Write one question you would want answered before you believed the story

---

### Activity 2: Anatomy of an AI Article (15 mins)

Now let us go deeper on one article. Pick the most interesting headline from your list in Activity 1 and open the full article. Read it carefully.

Then fill in this analysis framework:

**Source Analysis:**
- Who published this? (Newspaper, tech blog, company PR, academic journal)
- When was it published?
- Who wrote it — is the author named? Do they have expertise in AI?

**Claim Analysis:**
- What is the central claim of the article? (One sentence)
- What evidence is given to support it?
- Is the evidence from a primary source (original research) or secondary (someone reporting on someone else's claim)?

**Language Analysis:**
Look for these "hype words" and count how many appear:
- Revolutionary / game-changing / breakthrough
- Could / might / may / potentially (hedge words that sound dramatic but commit to nothing)
- Experts say / scientists claim (vague attribution — which experts?)
- According to [company name] (the company is not an independent source)

**Counter-Analysis:**
- What is the strongest argument against the article's claim?
- Is anyone quoted in the article who disagrees or adds caution? (If not, that is itself suspicious.)
- What would this story look like if the opposite were true?

After completing the framework, rate the article:
- **Red:** This is hype, fear-mongering, or PR dressed as news
- **Amber:** Some substance, but significant parts are exaggerated or one-sided
- **Green:** Solid reporting with real evidence and multiple perspectives

---

### Activity 3: Write the Balanced Version (10 mins)

Pick one of the articles you have analysed (or the headline from Activity 2 if you only did one deep-read).

Your task: Write a **balanced, 150-word summary** of what the article is actually saying — stripping out the hype and adding the caveats the journalist left out.

Your summary must include:
- The central claim (what happened or what was found)
- The actual evidence (what was tested, by whom, at what scale)
- At least one limitation or reason to be cautious
- One question that remains unanswered

You are not writing a negative or positive spin — you are writing a neutral, accurate summary. Read it back: could a thoughtful adult trust this as a fair account of the story?

Bonus: Use your AI tool to help. Ask it:
> "Here is a news article about AI. [Paste the article or a summary.] What are the limitations or caveats that this article downplays or leaves out? What questions should a critical reader ask?"

Compare what the AI identifies with what you found yourself. Did it spot anything you missed?

## Design It!

Create a one-page **"AI News Checklist"** poster — something you could stick next to your desk as a reminder for every time you read an AI news story.

Include:
- A title ("How to Read AI News Without Being Fooled" or your own title)
- The five story types from Activity 1 (in brief)
- Five quick questions to ask about any AI headline
- A simple 3-colour rating system (Red/Amber/Green like the one above)
- A short reminder statement at the bottom (your own words)

Make it visually clear and easy to scan — someone should be able to use it in under 60 seconds.

---

## Reflect
Answer these questions out loud or write them down:

1. After this lesson, do you think you will read AI news differently? What specifically will you do that you did not do before?
2. Is there a difference between a journalist writing an exaggerated headline and an AI company publishing a press release claiming their product is "revolutionary"? Who has more responsibility to be accurate?
3. You used AI to help analyse a news article about AI. Is there anything ironic or problematic about that? What are the limits of using AI to fact-check claims about AI?

---

## Share (Optional)
Show someone at home the article you analysed and walk them through your framework. Can you convince them that the article is more (or less) credible than it appears at first? Try to do this without just asserting your opinion — use the evidence from your analysis.
