## Key Concepts

### How AI Is Trained
When a large language model (like ChatGPT or Claude) is created, it's trained on an enormous collection of text — billions of web pages, books, articles, code, and more. During training, the AI adjusts its internal connections to get better and better at predicting what text should come next.

After training, the model is essentially "frozen." It carries its knowledge inside its parameters — like a snapshot of the internet at a moment in time.

> "AI doesn't Google things when you ask a question. It draws on patterns baked in during training. That's why it can be confidently wrong about recent events."

### Where Does the Data Come From?
Training datasets for large AI models typically include:

- **Common Crawl** — a massive scrape of the public internet, billions of web pages
- **Books** — scanned books, some obtained without authors' permission
- **Wikipedia** — the free encyclopaedia
- **Reddit and forums** — conversations and debates
- **Code repositories** — GitHub and other public code
- **News articles** — though often with copyright restrictions

The people who wrote all of this content were rarely asked. They certainly weren't paid.

### The Copyright Problem
When an AI reads a million novels to learn how to write fiction, is that like a human reading a million novels to become a writer — or is it like copying all those books without permission?

This is being debated in courts right now. Some key arguments:

**"It's fair use / it's like learning":**
- Humans learn from books they read, so why can't AI?
- Training produces new outputs, not copies

**"It's copyright violation":**
- Authors never agreed to their work being used this way
- The AI's output competes commercially with the authors
- The AI memorised some content verbatim

### What AI Doesn't Know
Because AI is trained on a fixed dataset with a cutoff date, it doesn't know:
- Events that happened after its training cutoff
- Things that aren't well-represented in public internet text
- Your personal information (unless you tell it)
- What's happening in real time
- Local, niche, or unpublicised information

### Activity 3: The Copyright Thought Experiment (15 mins)

Read this scenario:

> *Sam is a writer who spent 3 years writing a novel. The novel was published online. An AI company scraped the web and used Sam's novel (along with millions of others) to train their AI. The AI can now write novels in a similar style to Sam's. Sam wasn't asked permission. Sam wasn't paid.*

Now answer these questions in your notebook:

1. Do you think what the AI company did was fair? Why or why not?

2. Is this different from a human writer reading Sam's novel and being inspired by it? If so, how?

3. If you were writing a law to handle this, what would it say? Think about: should AI companies have to get permission? Pay authors? Be transparent about what they used?

4. Now flip it: if AI companies had to get permission for every piece of text they used, would it be possible to build powerful AI at all? What's the tradeoff?

## What Good Data Looks Like
Not all training data is created equal. Biased, low-quality, or one-sided data creates biased, unreliable AI.

Consider these data quality problems:
- **Recency bias** — the internet has way more recent text than historical text
- **Language bias** — English dominates the web, so AI is much better in English
- **Source bias** — Reddit is heavily male, young, and Western — its views shape AI personality
- **Selection bias** — academic papers require subscriptions; only the free web got scraped

> "AI is a mirror of what humans have written online. Its gaps and biases reflect ours."
