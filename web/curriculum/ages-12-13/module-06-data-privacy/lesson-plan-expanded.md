# Lesson Plan: Data, Privacy & Your Digital Life
### Builder Level (Ages 12–13) | Module 06 — Expanded

## About This Lesson

Every time you use a free app, you are entering a transaction. The currency is not money — it is data. Your location, your search history, your contacts, the apps you use and when, how long you spend on each screen, what you buy, what you look at without buying, what makes you stop scrolling — all of this is collected, stored, analysed, and sold or used to influence your future behaviour. The reason nearly every app, platform, and online service you use daily is free is because you are paying with something that is, in aggregate, worth an enormous amount of money: information about yourself.

This is not inherently sinister. Data powers services that are genuinely useful. Maps app navigation is only possible because millions of users share their location. Music recommendations improve because the app tracks what you listen to. But there is a spectrum between "useful personalisation" and "privacy invasion that you never consciously agreed to," and most people — including most adults — have very little idea where their apps fall on that spectrum.

For your generation, this matters in a particularly acute way. You are growing up in the first era where AI systems can build highly detailed individual profiles from your digital behaviour and use those profiles to predict and influence your future behaviour. Companies already use AI to predict what you will buy, what political messages you will respond to, how much you would be willing to pay for something, and whether you are likely to be having a difficult time emotionally (a signal marketers find valuable). These are not hypothetical future scenarios — they are current commercial practice.

The power imbalance here is real. Tech companies employ thousands of the world's most skilled engineers and psychologists specifically to make their apps as engaging as possible and to extract as much data as possible. You are, individually, not a match for those resources. But knowledge creates agency. When you understand what is being collected and why, you can make more intentional choices. When you understand that "free" has a cost, you can decide whether that cost is worth paying. And when you learn to read privacy settings (or use AI to translate a privacy policy into plain English), you can act rather than simply accept defaults.

None of this requires deleting all your apps or becoming paranoid. It requires becoming a more conscious participant in the digital economy — someone who makes deliberate choices about their data rather than passively accepting whatever defaults the app designers chose for them. That is a skill that will serve you for your entire life.

## Key Concepts

- **Data as currency:** The economic model by which most free digital services operate — users provide personal data in exchange for access to the service.
- **App permissions:** The specific device capabilities (camera, microphone, location, contacts) that apps request access to when you install them.
- **Digital footprint:** The accumulated record of your online activities — what you search, post, buy, and interact with — which can be stored indefinitely.
- **Data broker:** A company that collects personal data from multiple sources, aggregates it into profiles, and sells those profiles to advertisers, insurers, or other businesses.
- **Privacy by default:** The principle that apps and services should collect the minimum necessary data unless a user actively chooses to share more — the opposite of most current commercial practice.
- **Training data opt-out:** An option some AI tools offer that allows users to prevent their conversations from being used to train future AI models.

---

## How It Works

Modern apps collect data in several ways. **Active collection** includes everything you deliberately input: your name, email, search queries, posts, and messages. **Passive collection** includes everything you do without actively choosing to share it: which notifications you open, how long you look at something before scrolling, what time of day you use the app, your precise location (if location access is granted), your device model and operating system, and which other apps are installed on your phone.

This data does not just sit in a database. AI systems process it to build behavioural profiles. These profiles are used for advertising targeting (showing you ads for things you are statistically likely to want), for content recommendation (predicting what will keep you engaged), and in some cases are sold to third parties — data brokers who aggregate information from many sources into detailed commercial profiles.

The permissions model is the main mechanism you have for controlling passive collection. When you grant an app camera access, it can technically access the camera at any time the app is running — not just when you press the camera button. When you grant "always on" location access, the app can log your precise location continuously in the background. Revoking permissions limits what an app can collect, though active data you have already shared remains stored.

## Real World Examples

**1. Meta's cross-app tracking**
Facebook and Instagram (both owned by Meta) track user behaviour not just on their own platforms but across the web and on other apps through embedded tracking tools. Even if you have never created a Facebook account, if you have visited websites that use Facebook's tracking code, Meta may have a "shadow profile" of your browsing behaviour. This was revealed through EU regulatory investigations and led to significant fines. It demonstrates that data collection extends well beyond the apps you have consciously installed.

**2. Period tracking apps and data sharing**
Following the 2022 US Supreme Court decision on abortion rights, researchers and journalists raised serious concerns about period tracking apps that collected sensitive health data. Several popular apps were found to be sharing user data with third-party advertisers. This was a case where data that seemed entirely private (health information in a personal app) had been commercially shared in ways users had not anticipated or consented to clearly.

**3. TikTok and the data question**
TikTok, owned by Chinese company ByteDance, has been subject to regulatory scrutiny in multiple countries over concerns about where user data is stored and who can access it. The US government banned it on government devices; several other countries have restricted its use. This is an example of data privacy being a geopolitical issue, not just a personal one — where your data lives, who owns the company that holds it, and what laws apply to it are questions with real-world implications.

## Try It — The Digital Privacy Audit

### Part 1: App Permissions Check (15 mins)

Go to: **Settings → Apps → Permissions** (Android) or **Settings → Privacy → Permission Groups** (iOS)

For each category below, list the apps that have access:

| Permission | Apps with access | Do they need it? |
|-----------|-----------------|-----------------|
| Microphone | | |
| Camera | | |
| Location (Always) | | |
| Location (While Using) | | |
| Contacts | | |

For each app that has access, ask: **Does this app actually need this permission to do its job?**

If not — go revoke it right now. It takes 10 seconds per app.

### Part 4: The "What Does It Know?" Thought Experiment (10 mins) — NEW

This activity asks you to think like a data analyst — or like a company that has access to your data.

Pick one app you use regularly (TikTok, YouTube, Instagram, Spotify, or any other). Write down everything that app could know about you based on how you use it:

- How often do you use it and at what times of day?
- What content do you consume and for how long?
- What do you create or post?
- What do you skip, like, or share?
- Where are you when you use it (if location is granted)?
- Who do you interact with?

Now think: if someone built a profile of you from just that one app's data, how detailed would it be? What could they infer about your personality, interests, emotional state, schedule, and social circle?

Write a short "data profile" of yourself as if you were a data analyst describing what you know about this user. Be specific. This exercise tends to be genuinely surprising — the detail you can infer from app behaviour alone is significant.

---

### Part 5: The Default Settings Investigation (10 mins) — NEW

App designers make deliberate choices about default settings. In almost every case, the default is the setting that collects the most data and maximises engagement — not the setting most protective of your privacy. You have to opt out rather than opt in.

Pick one app or service and spend 5 minutes exploring every settings menu. Look specifically for:
- Any setting that says "share data with partners" or "personalised advertising" — is this on or off by default?
- Any setting related to your data being used for AI training
- Any setting related to location, activity tracking, or analytics
- Any option for downloading or deleting your data

For each setting you find, write down:
- What it does (in plain language)
- What its default setting is
- What you think the setting should be and why

Count how many privacy-related settings you find. How many were set to the "collect more" option by default? Is that a design choice or an accident?

## Level Up — Read a Privacy Policy (Sort of)

Pick one app or service you use regularly. Find its privacy policy.

You don't have to read the whole thing. Instead, use AI to summarise it:

```
I'm going to paste a privacy policy. Please summarise it in plain language for a 13-year-old.
Specifically tell me:
1. What data they collect
2. Who they share it with
3. How I can delete my data
4. Anything that seems unusual or surprising
Here is the privacy policy: [PASTE IT]
```

Copy a section of the privacy policy (the data collection section is usually near the top) and paste it in.

**What surprised you most?**

Then go one step further: rate the privacy policy on a scale of 1–5 for each of these:
- **Clarity:** How easy is it to understand?
- **Fairness:** Does the trade (data for service) seem reasonable?
- **User control:** How much control do you actually have over your data?
- **Trustworthiness:** Based on what you have read, do you trust this company with your data?

Write a two-paragraph "privacy review" of the service as if you were writing for a consumer advice website.
