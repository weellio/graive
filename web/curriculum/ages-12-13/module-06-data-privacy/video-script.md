## CONCEPT: What Gets Collected (0:50–3:00)

**[HOST, animated data categories appearing]**

"Let's go through what apps typically collect. Some of this you've probably heard. Some of it might surprise you.

**Basic info:** Name, age, email, country. You gave this when you signed up.

**Device info:** What phone or computer you use, your operating system, your device ID — a unique identifier that follows you even if you delete and reinstall the app.

**Usage data:** What you tap on, how long you spend on each thing, what you search for, what you scroll past, what you scroll *back* to look at again.

**Location data:** Many apps ask for your location. Some use it when you're not actively using the app. If you have location services on — even in the background — some apps are tracking where you go.

**Contacts and connections:** Some apps want access to your contact list, your calendar, who you message and when (though not always the content).

**Behavioural data:** Over time, patterns build up. They can infer your mood, your interests, your politics, your income level, your health concerns — all from behaviour, even without you stating any of it.

And because apps share data with each other through ad networks, your profile follows you across platforms."

---

## HOW IT'S USED (3:00–4:30)

**[HOST, examples of ad targeting]**

"The main commercial use is advertising. Advertisers pay to reach specific people — not just 'young people' but 'young people who've been watching cooking videos, live in a city, and spend money on food.' Your data makes that targeting possible.

But it goes beyond ads. Your data is also used to:
- Train AI models (including many of the AI tools you use)
- Sell to data brokers who build consumer profiles
- Shape what content you're shown (remember the algorithm)
- In some cases, used by insurers, employers, or other institutions (this varies by country and is increasingly regulated)

The important thing to understand: **this is the current deal.** Free services exist because your engagement and data fund them. That's not necessarily evil — but you should know it's the transaction."

## WHAT YOU CAN ACTUALLY DO (4:30–6:30)

**[HOST, practical list]**

"Here's where it gets useful. What can you actually do?

**1. Check your app permissions**
Go to your phone settings → apps → permissions. See which apps have access to your camera, microphone, location, contacts. Revoke anything that doesn't need it. A torch app doesn't need your location.

**2. Use 'while using' not 'always' for location**
Most apps give you the option. Choose 'while using' so apps only track your location when you're actively in them.

**3. Use a privacy-focused browser for some things**
Firefox, Brave, or Safari with tracking prevention on are better than Chrome for privacy. You can use different browsers for different things.

**4. Be thoughtful about what you use accounts for**
You don't need to sign in with a real name to every service. Some things are fine to use anonymously or with a separate email.

**5. Read the privacy summary — not the full terms**
Nobody reads full terms of service. But many services now publish a short plain-language summary. Worth a two-minute skim before you sign up for something new.

**6. Ask: does this app *need* this data?**
Before accepting permissions, ask why an app would need that access. If the reason isn't obvious, it's worth a moment of thought."

## THE AI DIMENSION (6:30–7:15)

**[HOST]**

"One more thing that's specific to AI: when you type things into AI tools — your questions, your problems, your creative work — that data may be used to improve the AI model.

Most AI companies have settings where you can opt out of training. It's worth checking.

This doesn't mean don't use AI. But it means: don't type highly personal information into a free AI tool you know nothing about. Your medical concerns, your legal problems, your private relationships — those are worth keeping private."

---

## CHALLENGE (7:15–7:45)

**[HOST]**

"This week's challenge: do a five-minute privacy audit on your phone.

Go to settings → apps → permissions. Count:
- How many apps have access to your microphone?
- How many have 'always on' location access?
- Is there anything that seems unnecessary?

Revoke at least one permission that you don't think the app actually needs.

That's it. Five minutes. You might be surprised."
