## About This Lesson

Every time you open TikTok, YouTube, Instagram, or any social media platform, an algorithm decides what you see first — and what you never see at all. This is not a small editorial decision made by humans. It is a real-time, personalised, constantly-updating automated system running billions of calculations per day, trained on the behaviour of billions of users. And its goal is not to inform you, entertain you well, or make you a better-rounded person. Its goal, at the most fundamental level, is to maximise the amount of time you spend on the platform.

That single-minded optimisation goal creates some strange and important side effects. The algorithm learns, very quickly, that emotionally intense content keeps people watching longer than neutral content. Outrage, fear, excitement, and controversy are all more "engaging" than calm information. Content that confirms what you already believe is more engaging than content that challenges you. So the algorithm gradually learns to serve you more of whatever already makes you feel something strongly — and that process slowly shapes what you think the world looks like.

This is what researchers call a filter bubble: a personalised information environment where you increasingly only see content that aligns with your existing interests, beliefs, and emotions. You did not choose this bubble consciously. You just kept watching videos and the algorithm drew conclusions. The bubble tightens over time, and most people have no idea it is happening.

Understanding this is not about being paranoid or quitting social media. It is about becoming a more intentional user. When you know that an algorithm is optimising for your engagement, you can start to ask: is what I am seeing representative of reality, or is it a curated selection chosen because it will keep me scrolling? Is my view of a topic informed or echo-chamber shaped? The difference between a person who thinks about these questions and a person who does not is enormous — especially on topics like politics, health, and current events.

This lesson also introduces you to AI as a way of deliberately breaking out of your bubble. Unlike a social media algorithm, an AI tool has no personalisation incentive — it does not know your history or your preferences unless you tell it. That makes it, paradoxically, a more neutral starting point for exploring topics than a platform algorithmically tuned to give you more of what you already like.

## Key Concepts

- **Algorithm:** A set of rules or a mathematical model that decides what content to show you, what product to recommend, or what results to surface — based on your past behaviour and the behaviour of similar users.
- **Filter bubble:** The personalised information environment created when algorithms repeatedly show you content that matches your existing interests and views, narrowing your exposure to different perspectives.
- **Engagement optimisation:** The design principle behind most social media algorithms — maximising the time users spend on a platform, typically by surfacing emotionally activating content.
- **Echo chamber:** A situation where a person only encounters information and opinions that reinforce their existing beliefs, often amplified by algorithmic recommendation.
- **Confirmation bias:** The human tendency to favour information that confirms what we already believe — which algorithms exploit and amplify.
- **Algorithmic transparency:** The degree to which users can understand or know why a platform showed them specific content — currently very limited on most platforms.

## How It Works

Social media recommendation algorithms use a branch of AI called **collaborative filtering** combined with **deep learning**. Here is the simplified version of how TikTok's For You Page works:

1. When you watch a video, the algorithm records signals: Did you watch to the end? Did you rewatch it? Did you like, share, or comment? How long did you pause on it?
2. These signals are combined into a profile of your preferences — not a list, but a mathematical representation of your taste in multi-dimensional space.
3. Every video on the platform is similarly encoded. The algorithm finds videos that are "near" your preference profile in that mathematical space.
4. It tests new videos by showing them to small groups of users with similar profiles and measures engagement. High-performing videos get shown to larger audiences.
5. This process repeats millions of times per day, continuously updating.

The result is a recommendation system that is uncannily good at finding content you will find compelling — but it has no concept of "good for you," "accurate," or "representative." Those are not in its objective function. Only engagement is.

## Real World Examples

**1. YouTube's recommendation rabbit holes**
YouTube's recommendation algorithm has been studied extensively by researchers. It has a documented tendency to push users toward increasingly extreme versions of content they already watch — a phenomenon called "recommendation drift." Someone watching moderate political commentary may be pushed toward more extreme commentary because engagement rates are higher on emotionally intense content. YouTube has made changes to address this, but the underlying engagement incentive remains.

**2. Facebook and the 2016 US election**
Internal research at Facebook, revealed in leaked documents, showed that the company's algorithm was amplifying misinformation and outrage-inducing content because it generated more engagement. Researchers inside the company flagged this, but changes were delayed due to concerns about reduced engagement. This is a real-world example of engagement optimisation creating societal harm.

**3. The "For You Page" and mental health**
Studies of TikTok users have found that users who engage with content about anxiety or body image concerns are quickly served more of the same content — and then progressively more extreme versions. This is the filter bubble operating in a mental health context. TikTok has introduced "content controls" in some regions, but the underlying incentive structure (more engagement = more time on platform) has not changed.

## Part 1: The 48-Hour Algorithm Watch (ongoing)

Before doing the rest of this lesson, start your observation first.

Choose ONE platform you use regularly. For 48 hours, log your observations in a notebook. You don't have to use the platform more than normal — just pay attention when you do.

Each time you notice something, note:

| Observation | Why do you think it was shown to you? | Did it make you feel something? |
|------------|--------------------------------------|--------------------------------|
| Example: Video about [topic] | I watched 3 similar videos yesterday | Yes — excited / annoyed / sad |
| | | |
| | | |
| | | |

Try to log at least **5 observations** over the 48 hours.

## Part 2: The Other Side Experiment (20 mins)

After your 48-hour observation, do this activity.

Think about a topic your feed shows you a lot — a sport, a type of music, a topic from the news, a hobby, a political issue (if relevant).

Now ask an AI to show you the *other side* or a *different angle*:

**Prompt template:**
```
I usually see a lot of content about [TOPIC] that focuses on [YOUR USUAL ANGLE].
Show me 5 interesting perspectives, arguments, or facts about [TOPIC] that I probably
haven't come across before. Include at least one that would challenge my current view.
Present each as a 2-3 sentence summary.
```

Write down:
1. Did any of the perspectives genuinely surprise you?
2. Did any of them make you uncomfortable? Why?
3. Does knowing these exist change how you think about your feed?

## Part 3: Build a Better Search (15 mins)

Algorithms give you what's popular. But sometimes you need what's *accurate* or *balanced*.

Use AI as a research starting point instead of social media. Pick a topic from the news (something you've seen recently) and run this prompt:

```
I want to understand [NEWS TOPIC] properly.
Give me:
1. A 3-sentence neutral summary of what's happening
2. Two different perspectives people have on it (one per side)
3. Two things that both sides agree on
4. One question that remains genuinely unresolved

Don't take sides. I'm trying to understand, not confirm what I already think.
```

Compare this to what you've seen in your social media feed about the same topic. Are they the same? Different? What's missing from each?

## Part 4: Reverse-Engineer Your Own Feed (15 mins) — NEW

This activity helps you see your own filter bubble from the outside.

Spend 5 minutes scrolling your main social media platform as normal. Write down the first 10 pieces of content you see (just brief descriptions — no need to be specific).

Then analyse what you wrote:

1. Count how many of the 10 items fit into the same 1–3 topic categories. What percentage of your feed is those categories?
2. Are there any topics that are completely absent from your feed that you would expect to see if your feed were a balanced sample of what is happening in the world?
3. Try this: deliberately interact (like, comment, or watch fully) with two pieces of content outside your normal interests. Come back 24 hours later. Has anything changed in what is being served to you?

Record your findings. This is you actively experimenting with the algorithm rather than passively receiving from it.

## Part 5: The Algorithm vs. Human Choice Audit (10 mins) — NEW

This activity asks you to make a distinction the algorithm never makes: the difference between what you are shown and what you would actually choose.

Think about the last week of content you consumed on any platform. In your notebook, create two columns:

| Content I was shown and watched anyway | Content I actively sought out myself |
|---------------------------------------|--------------------------------------|
| | |

Try to fill in at least 5 rows of each column.

Then ask yourself:
1. Which column is longer? What does that ratio tell you?
2. Is the content you actively seek out different in quality, topic, or how it makes you feel from the content the algorithm serves you?
3. If you had to switch off algorithmic recommendations entirely and only watch content you deliberately chose, would your media diet look different? Better or worse?

Write a paragraph summing up what you discovered.

## Level Up — Design a "Bubble-Breaker" Habit

Design a personal routine for getting outside your filter bubble. This should be realistic — something you'd actually do once a week.

Answer these:
1. What platform am I most at risk of living in a bubble on?
2. What's one thing I could do once a week to see a different perspective?
3. How could I use AI to help with this?

Write it as a short personal commitment:
> "Once a week, I will [action] to make sure I'm not only seeing [type of content]. I'll use AI to [specific use]."

Then go one step further: design a one-month "bubble-breaking challenge" — four specific actions you will take, one per week, each designed to expose you to a perspective or topic you do not normally encounter. Write it like a personal challenge plan.

## Reflect

1. Before this module, did you think about why you were being shown specific content? Has that changed?

2. Is a filter bubble always bad? Can it ever be useful? Give an example of each.

3. What's the difference between an algorithm choosing what you see and *you* choosing what you see? Does the difference matter?

4. The algorithm has no moral intent — it is just optimising a number. Does that make the outcomes it creates (like echo chambers or mental health effects) less serious? Who should be responsible for those outcomes?

5. If you were designing a social media algorithm for teenagers, what would you optimise for instead of (or in addition to) engagement? What trade-offs would that create?
