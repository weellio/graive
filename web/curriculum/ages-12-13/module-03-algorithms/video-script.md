## HOOK (0:00–0:45)

**[HOST on camera]**

"Quick experiment. Think about the last three videos you watched on YouTube. Or the last three posts that stopped your scroll on whatever app you use.

Now ask yourself: did you *choose* those, or did the app choose them *for you*?

Most people say 'I chose them.' But here's the thing — you chose from a list that someone else designed. You never saw the content that *wasn't* shown to you.

Today we're going to talk about what's actually going on behind that screen. And once you see it, you genuinely cannot unsee it."

**[TITLE CARD: "Algorithms Around You"]**

---

## CONCEPT: What Is a Recommendation Algorithm? (0:45–3:00)

**[HOST, animation/diagram]**

"An algorithm is just a set of instructions. A recipe. If this, then that.

The recommendation algorithms on YouTube, TikTok, Spotify, Instagram — they have one job: **keep you on the platform as long as possible.** More time = more ads = more money.

So they're constantly making bets: *what's the next thing that will keep this person watching?*

And they get really, *really* good at it. Because they have a lot of data about you.

**[ANIMATED: list of data points]**

Every time you watch a video to the end, they note it. Every time you skip after 5 seconds, they note it. Every like, every comment, every pause, every time you rewatch a bit — all of it feeds back into the system.

They know:
- What you click on when you're bored
- What topics get you engaged
- What makes you angry enough to comment
- What keeps you up late

And they use all of that to predict what to show you next.

This is called a **feedback loop**. You watch something → the algorithm shows you more of it → you watch more of that → it shows you even more. Round and round."

---

## THE FILTER BUBBLE (3:00–5:00)

**[HOST, diagram showing two different feed bubbles]**

"Here's where it gets important — and a little uncomfortable.

Because the algorithm optimises for engagement, it gradually narrows down to the stuff that works best *on you specifically*. It finds the topics, styles, and emotions that get a reaction.

This creates what researchers call a **filter bubble** — you end up in a world where almost everything you see confirms what you already think and like.

Imagine two kids, both using YouTube. One starts watching football clips. The other starts watching political commentary. Over a few weeks, their feeds look completely different. They're basically living in different information worlds — even though they're using the same app.

Now add in the fact that **outrage, fear, and strong emotion get more engagement** than calm, balanced information. The algorithm doesn't care if something makes you upset or anxious — if you keep watching, that's a win for the platform.

This doesn't mean every platform is evil. But it does mean: **what you're shown is not neutral.** It's a curated selection designed to keep you engaged, not necessarily to inform you or show you the full picture."

---

## THE AI CONNECTION (5:00–6:00)

**[HOST, camera]**

"So what does this have to do with AI and prompt engineering?

Everything.

The AI tools you use — ChatGPT, Claude, search engines — also have biases built in. Not always the same type, but they reflect patterns from their training data.

And here's the direct connection: **if you don't ask good questions, you get the algorithm's default.**

When you type a vague search or a vague prompt, the system guesses what 'most people' want in that situation. You get the average answer. The popular answer. Not necessarily the *right* one for you.

This is why the RCTFC framework matters beyond just getting better essay help. It's about staying in control of what information you get, rather than letting the system decide for you."

## CHALLENGE (6:00–6:40)

**[HOST, direct to camera]**

"Your challenge this week is an experiment, not an activity.

For the next 48 hours, pay attention to what you're shown online. Pick one platform.

Ask yourself:
1. Why am I being shown this?
2. Is this making me feel good, scared, angry, or excited — and is it doing that on purpose?
3. Am I seeing different perspectives, or always the same kind of thing?

No need to change anything. Just observe. Be the scientist for two days.

Write down 3 things you notice. They don't have to be deep — just honest."
