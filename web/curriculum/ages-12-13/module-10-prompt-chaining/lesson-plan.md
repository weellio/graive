## Key Concepts

### What Is Prompt Chaining?
Prompt chaining means using the output of one prompt as the input for the next — building something complex step by step rather than trying to do everything in one go.

It's like building with Lego: you don't snap all the pieces together at once. You build sections, check them, combine them.

**Single prompt approach:**
> "Write me a short story about a robot who discovers feelings."

**Chained prompt approach:**
1. First: "Give me 5 interesting character concepts for a robot who discovers feelings."
2. Then: "I like option 3. Develop a backstory for this character in 5 bullet points."
3. Then: "Here's the backstory [paste it]. Now write the opening scene — 3 paragraphs, focus on the moment it first feels something."
4. Then: "Here's the opening scene [paste it]. What's the most interesting direction this story could go next? Give 3 options."
5. Then: "I choose option 2. Write the next scene."

> "Chaining gives you control at every step. You check the work before moving on, so errors don't compound."

### Why Is Chaining Better?
When you do everything in one prompt:
- AI makes all the decisions
- You get what it gives you
- One bad assumption ruins everything
- Hard to fix specific parts

When you chain:
- You make decisions at each step
- You can redirect if something goes wrong
- Quality compounds with each good step
- It feels collaborative rather than passive

### The Three Chain Types

**Sequential chain** — each step builds directly on the previous one
Step 1 → Step 2 → Step 3 → Final output

**Branching chain** — you explore options at one step, then pick the best path
Step 1 → [Options A, B, C] → Pick best → Step 2

**Refinement chain** — you produce a version, critique it, then improve it
Draft → Critique → Revised draft → Critique → Final version

## Try It — Build Your First Prompt Chain

### Activity 1: The Story Chain (20 mins)

Follow these exact chain steps. Copy the output of each step into your notebook before running the next.

**Step 1 — Generate options:**
Ask: "Give me 5 one-sentence story concepts involving a young person who discovers an unexpected ability or secret. Make them original — avoid clichés like 'chosen one' or 'magic school.'"

Choose your favourite concept. Write it here: ___

**Step 2 — Develop the character:**
Ask: "Based on this story concept: [paste your chosen concept]. Create a main character. Give me: their name, age, one unusual personality trait, their biggest fear, and one thing they desperately want. Keep it short — bullet points."

Read the character. Adjust anything you want to change. Write your adjusted version: ___

**Step 3 — Develop the world:**
Ask: "Here's my story concept and character: [paste both]. Describe the world or setting in 3–5 sentences. Make it feel real — specific details, not generic."

Rate the world description: ___/10

**Step 4 — Write the opening:**
Ask: "Here's my story concept, character, and setting: [paste all three]. Write the first 3 paragraphs of this story. Start in the middle of an interesting moment — don't start with 'One day...'"

**Step 5 — Critique and improve:**
Ask: "Here's my story opening: [paste]. Give me three specific things that are working and two specific things that could be stronger. Don't rewrite it — just tell me what to improve."

Now make one improvement yourself. Write your improved opening: ___

Compare your final opening with what a single-prompt approach would have given you. Which is better?

---

### Activity 2: The Research Chain (15 mins)

Use chaining for something useful — research on a topic you're genuinely curious about.

**Step 1:** "I want to learn about [your topic]. What are the 5 most interesting questions someone could explore about this?"

Pick the question you find most interesting: ___

**Step 2:** "For this question: [paste question]. What's the current state of knowledge? What do experts generally agree on, and what's still debated?"

**Step 3:** "Based on what you just told me, what's the most surprising or counterintuitive aspect? What would most people not know about this?"

**Step 4:** "If I wanted to learn more about this topic, what types of sources — books, documentaries, fields of study — would you recommend?"

At the end, rate:
- Quality of learning from this chain: ___/10
- Quality of a single question ("Tell me about [topic]") would have been: ___/10 (estimate)

---

### Activity 3: The Refinement Chain (10 mins)

Pick any piece of work — a paragraph you wrote, a project description, an idea.

Run this refinement chain:

**Round 1 critique:** "Here is [your work]. What are the three biggest weaknesses? Be specific."

**You revise** (don't ask AI to rewrite — you do it): ___

**Round 2 critique:** "Here is the revised version [paste]. Is it better? What's still not working?"

**You revise again:** ___

**Final check:** "Rate this final version from 1–10 compared to what it started as, and explain your rating."

AI's final rating: ___/10
My own rating of the improvement: ___/10

## Building a Personal Prompt Chain Library

Now that you understand chaining, you can build reusable chains for tasks you do often.

Design a prompt chain for one of these common tasks:
- Studying for a test
- Preparing for a debate or presentation
- Understanding a confusing text
- Planning a creative project

## Reflect

1. What's the difference in how it *felt* to use a prompt chain versus asking one big question?

2. At which step in your story chain did you make the most important creative decision? What did you decide?

3. What are the risks of chaining? (Hint: if step 1 goes badly, what happens to everything after it?)

Document every step: the prompt you used, what you got, and the decision you made before continuing.
