# Lesson Plan: AI Policy & Civic Engagement
### Innovator Level (Ages 16–18) | Module 05

---

## Objective
Produce a concise, well-reasoned policy brief on one AI governance issue and identify one concrete way to engage with AI policy beyond this course.

---

## What You'll Need
- AI tool + search engine
- Notebook or Google Doc
- 75–90 minutes

---

## Watch First
Watch **Module 05: AI Policy & Civic Engagement**.

Five debates: safety, liability, copyright, deepfakes, access. Know the EU AI Act framework: unacceptable / high / limited / minimal risk.

---

## Exercise 1: The Policy Landscape Map (20 mins)

For each of the five major debates, write 2–3 sentences summarising:
- The core tension
- One strong argument on each side

Use this prompt to help:
```
For each of these AI policy debates, give me:
1. The core tension in one sentence
2. The strongest pro-regulation argument
3. The strongest anti-regulation argument

Debates:
- AI safety and alignment
- Liability and accountability
- Copyright and training data
- Deepfakes and synthetic media
- Access and inequality
```

After reading the AI's summary — verify at least two claims about the EU AI Act with external sources.

---

## Exercise 2: Write a Policy Brief (35 mins)

Choose your most interesting debate from the five. Write a 400–600 word policy brief.

**Format:**
```
POLICY BRIEF: [TITLE]
Issue: [TOPIC]
Author: [Your name]
Date: [Today]

THE PROBLEM
[2–3 sentences: what's the issue, why does it matter now]

THE ARGUMENTS FOR STRONG REGULATION
[3–4 bullet points with brief evidence or reasoning]

THE ARGUMENTS FOR LIGHTER-TOUCH REGULATION
[3–4 bullet points with brief evidence or reasoning]

THE RECOMMENDED APPROACH
[Your position — what specific policy approach would you recommend?]
[Explain your reasoning — which considerations outweigh which?]

ONE SPECIFIC RECOMMENDATION
[A concrete, specific policy action — not 'more regulation' but what kind, how, enforced how]

WHAT WOULD CHANGE YOUR MIND
[What evidence or arguments would shift your recommendation?]
```

**AI usage rules for this exercise:**
- Use AI for research (understanding the debate, finding evidence)
- Write the analysis and recommendation yourself
- Use AI to check your reasoning: "What am I missing? What's the weakest part of my argument?"

---

## Exercise 3: Engagement Mapping (15 mins)

Identify one concrete way you could engage with AI governance in the next year.

Options to research:
- Student AI ethics organisations (AI Safety Student Club, Effective Altruism groups)
- Policy fellowships for young people (check local government, EU, UK organisations)
- Research assistantships at universities working on AI governance
- Public consultations (search for your country's AI consultation processes)
- Competitions (AI ethics essay competitions, etc.)
- Simply: being the person in your community who explains this clearly

Choose one. Write a two-sentence description of what you'd do and why.

---

## Level Up — The Mock Consultation Response

Many governments issue "calls for evidence" before making AI policy. Find one (search: "[your country] AI consultation" or use a previous one from the EU AI Act process).

Write a 200-word response as if you were submitting to the consultation. Use your policy brief as the basis.

This is exactly what advocacy organisations do — and individual submissions are read.

---

## Reflect

1. Of the five debates, which do you think is most underappreciated or underreported in mainstream media? Why?

2. The module says "if you build, you govern." What does that mean for the responsibility of developers and entrepreneurs?

3. What's the tension between moving fast to develop AI (potentially gaining economic advantages or solving problems sooner) and moving carefully (reducing risks)? How would you resolve it?

Share your one specific policy recommendation and your reasoning in one paragraph.
