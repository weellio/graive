## Key Concepts

### What AI Doesn't Do for Leaders
AI is a powerful tool for many tasks. But it does not:
- Take responsibility for decisions
- Understand organisational context and culture
- Know the people involved
- Make ethical judgments independently
- Bear consequences
- Inspire, motivate, or build trust

A leader who outsources judgment to AI still bears full responsibility for the outcomes. "The AI told me to" is not a defence — in law or in ethics.

> "AI can inform every decision. But the decision — and its consequences — remains with you. That's what leadership means in the age of AI."

### AI-Assisted Decision-Making
AI is genuinely useful for decisions when used as a thinking partner, not an authority:

**Good uses:**
- Generating options you hadn't considered
- Stress-testing your reasoning: "What could go wrong with this plan?"
- Researching relevant facts and context
- Drafting communication for review and editing
- Identifying stakeholders or perspectives you haven't considered
- Modelling scenarios: "If we do X, what are the likely second-order effects?"

**Bad uses:**
- Delegating the decision itself to AI
- Using AI output without checking it critically
- Hiding behind AI to avoid difficult conversations
- Presenting AI-generated content as your own thinking to stakeholders

### Communication as Leadership
Leaders spend most of their time communicating: explaining decisions, aligning teams, managing upwards, handling difficult conversations, presenting to stakeholders.

AI can help with all of these — but the leader must ensure:
- **Accuracy** — AI-drafted communication must be verified
- **Authenticity** — it should sound like you, not generic AI output
- **Appropriateness** — AI doesn't know the relationship history and context
- **Ownership** — you can stand behind and defend every word

### Responsible Deployment
When you're the person deploying AI in an organisation or product, you're responsible for:
- Who can use it and for what
- What happens when it fails
- How errors are caught and corrected
- What oversight exists
- How affected people are informed and protected
- Whether vulnerable people are adequately protected

## Try It — Leadership AI Workshops

### Activity 1: AI-Assisted Decision-Making (20 mins)

Choose a real decision you're facing — school, personal project, community, or use one of these scenarios:

**Scenario A:** You run a school newspaper. You're deciding whether to use AI to help write articles — knowing some readers will assume articles are fully human-written.

**Scenario B:** You're leading a team project and two team members have conflicting ideas about direction. You need to make a call.

**Scenario C:** A club you lead wants to use AI to personalise content sent to members. Some members haven't given clear consent for data use.

Use AI to support (not make) the decision:

Step 2 — Ask: "What's the strongest argument against my preferred option?"

Step 3 — Ask: "Who are the stakeholders affected by this decision, and what does each group care about?"

### Activity 2: Leadership Communication Workshop (20 mins)

Good leaders communicate with clarity, care, and authenticity. Use AI as a drafting assistant — but maintain your own voice and judgment.

**Task:** Write a difficult message. Choose one:

- Telling a team member their work isn't good enough, and explaining what needs to change
- Communicating a decision you made that some people won't agree with
- Asking for help or support in a way that's honest about your own limitations
- Delivering critical feedback to someone more senior or experienced than you

**Step 1 — Draft it yourself first (5 minutes):**
Write a rough draft without any AI help. Get your meaning down — don't worry about polish.

My rough draft: ___

**Step 2 — Get AI feedback:**
Ask: "Here's a message I've drafted: [paste draft]. What's unclear? What could hurt the relationship? What's strongest about it? Give me 3 specific suggestions."

AI feedback: ___

**Step 3 — Revise:**
Make the changes you think are right (ignore the ones you don't agree with).

Final version: ___
Changes I made based on AI: ___
Changes I rejected and why: ___

**Step 4 — Authenticity check:**
Read your final version aloud. Does it sound like you? Could you deliver this with genuine conviction?

### Activity 3: Responsible Deployment Audit (15 mins)

You're deploying an AI system within an organisation (school club, small business, charity, team).

Your system: [describe what it does]

Apply this responsible deployment checklist:

- [ ] Have I clearly defined what the AI will and won't do?
- [ ] Do affected people know an AI is being used?
- [ ] Is there a human review process for consequential outputs?
- [ ] Is there a way for people to contest or override AI decisions?
- [ ] Have I considered the worst-case failure mode? What's the plan?
- [ ] Am I complying with relevant terms of service and privacy obligations?
- [ ] Is there a process for ongoing monitoring once deployed?
- [ ] Have I considered the most vulnerable people who might be affected?

For any unchecked box, write what you'd do to address it:

My gaps: ___
My mitigation plans: ___

## The Trust Problem

When AI helps you make decisions or generate communication, other people may not know how much AI was involved. This creates a trust problem:

- If people knew AI drafted your strategy, would they trust it less?
- If people knew AI analysed the data, would they feel their needs were properly understood?
- If people knew AI helped write your message, would they feel you genuinely cared?

There's no universal answer — it depends on context, relationship, and the role of the AI. But as a leader, you should have a clear, honest policy about when and how you use AI — and be prepared to disclose it.

## Reflect

1. Is there a decision you've made (or could make) in a leadership role where AI genuinely helped — not just made it faster, but made it better? What did it add?

2. The hardest parts of leadership are usually human — difficult conversations, conflicting loyalties, ethical dilemmas. Can AI help with these? How much?

3. In 10 years, leaders who use AI well will have a significant advantage over those who don't. But what risks come with that advantage — for the leader, and for the people they lead?

## Challenge
**The Leadership Challenge:**

Over the next two weeks, deliberately use AI to support three real leadership moments:
1. One decision (use the decision framework from Activity 1)
2. One communication (use the drafting workshop approach from Activity 2)
3. One situation where you choose NOT to use AI — and document why you made that choice

For each, write a brief reflection:
- What AI contributed
- What you decided or added yourself
- Whether you'd make the same choice about AI use again

After three weeks, write a 200-word reflection: what have you learned about where AI helps leaders and where it doesn't?
