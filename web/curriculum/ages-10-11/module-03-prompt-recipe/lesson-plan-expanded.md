# Lesson Plan: Prompts Are Like Recipes
### Explorer Level (Ages 10–11) | Module 03

---

## About This Lesson

Have you ever tried to bake a cake without a recipe? You might remember that it needs flour, sugar, and something creamy — but if you leave out the eggs, or add too much baking powder, or forget the butter, the cake comes out wrong. Sometimes it still tastes okay. More often it is a flat, rubbery disaster. Recipes exist because the right ingredients, in the right amounts, in the right order, produce results that are so much better than guessing.

Prompts work exactly the same way. Most people who write prompts just type whatever comes to mind — a bit of instruction, maybe a vague idea of what they want. And like a cake without a proper recipe, the results are often fine but not great. The Prompt Recipe gives you a reliable structure that works every single time, no matter what you are trying to create or learn. It has three ingredients: Context, Instruction, and Format.

Context is everything the AI needs to know before it starts — who you are, what you are working on, what you already know, and what the situation is. Without context, AI makes assumptions — and those assumptions are often wrong. Think of context as setting the scene for a play. The more clearly you describe the stage, the characters, and the situation, the better the actors can perform.

Instruction is what you actually want the AI to do — but a good instruction is specific, not vague. Not "help me with science" but "explain why ice floats on water using an analogy involving something from my kitchen." Format tells the AI how to present the answer — should it be bullet points or paragraphs? Long or short? Formal or funny? Written for an adult or for a child? When all three ingredients are in your prompt, the AI has everything it needs to give you exactly what you are looking for.

---

## Key Concepts

- **Context:** Background information the AI needs — who you are, what you know, and what the situation is.
- **Instruction:** The specific action or task you want the AI to perform — precise and clear.
- **Format:** How you want the answer to look — length, structure, tone, and style.
- **Recipe prompt:** A prompt that deliberately includes all three ingredients for maximum effectiveness.
- **Follow-up prompting:** Continuing the conversation after the first response to fix or improve one specific thing.

---

## Fun Facts

- AI researchers have found that telling an AI "you are an expert in..." at the start of a prompt actually changes the quality of its responses — giving the AI a role (Context) is a real technique used by professionals.
- The format ingredient is so powerful that the exact same information can become either a boring wall of text or a clear, exciting guide just by changing how you ask for it to be laid out.
- Some people write "prompt libraries" — collections of their best-performing prompts — to save and reuse, in the same way a chef saves their best recipes.

## Try It — Cook Up Some Prompts

### Recipe 1: The School Helper (10 mins)

Think of something you're studying in school right now that you find a bit confusing.

Fill in your recipe:
- **Context:** I am _____ years old and I'm learning about _____. I already know _____ but I don't understand _____.
- **Instruction:** Please explain _____ to me.
- **Format:** Use a fun analogy or story. Keep it short — about 3–4 sentences. Use simple words.

Combine into one prompt and run it.

**Did it help you understand? Did anything still confuse you?**
If yes — write a follow-up asking about the bit you still don't get.

---

### Recipe 2: The Creative Helper (10 mins)

Think of something creative you'd like to make (a story, poem, song, joke, game idea).

Fill in your recipe:
- **Context:** I want to create _____. It's for _____ (yourself, a friend, school, fun). The main character / topic is _____.
- **Instruction:** _____ (be specific — what should it do/say/feel like?)
- **Format:** _____ (length, style, who wrote it, how it should sound)

Run your recipe prompt. Then try changing just ONE ingredient and run it again. Which version was better?

---

### Recipe 3: The Missing Ingredient Experiment (10 mins)

This one is sneaky — it shows you *why* all three ingredients matter.

Pick a topic (anything you like). Write the prompt THREE times:
1. With ONLY Context (no instruction or format)
2. With ONLY Instruction (no context or format)
3. With ALL THREE ingredients

Compare all three outputs.

| Version | Output quality (1–10) | What was missing? |
|---------|-----------------------|-------------------|
| Context only | | |
| Instruction only | | |
| All three | | |

**What did each missing ingredient affect?**

---

### Recipe 4: The Role Assignment (10 mins)

One of the most powerful Context tricks is giving the AI a role — telling it to act as a specific kind of expert or character. This changes not just what it says, but how it says it.

Try this experiment. Ask the AI the same question (you choose the question — something you are genuinely curious about) THREE times, but with a different role each time:

**Prompt A:**
> "You are a friendly primary school science teacher who loves making things simple and fun. [YOUR QUESTION]"

**Prompt B:**
> "You are a bored teenager who knows a lot about this topic but explains things in a casual, slightly sarcastic way. [YOUR QUESTION]"

**Prompt C:**
> "You are an enthusiastic documentary narrator in the style of a nature programme. [YOUR QUESTION]"

Compare all three responses. Answer these questions:
- Which was most useful?
- Which was most entertaining?
- Which felt most accurate?
- When in real life would you use each style?

Role assignments are a Context superpower. They can completely change the energy and usefulness of any answer.

---

### Recipe 5: The Format Makeover (5 mins)

Take any long, paragraph-heavy response the AI has given you today. Then send this follow-up:

> "Can you present that same information in a totally different format? Make it a numbered list of the 5 most important points, each with a one-sentence explanation and one practical example."

Compare the original and the reformatted version. Which is easier to use? Which would you actually read?

Write down one situation where each format would be better.

## Reflect
1. Which ingredient do you find easiest to write? Which is hardest?
2. If you could only pick TWO of the three ingredients, which would you keep?
3. Can you think of a time in real life (not AI) when giving someone more context would have helped?
4. After trying the Role Assignment activity, why do you think the role you give the AI makes such a big difference to the answer?
5. Which prompt from today are you most proud of? What made it a good recipe?

## Level Up Challenge

Design the ultimate recipe prompt — one that is so well-crafted it produces something genuinely impressive on the first try, with no need for follow-up.

Your goal: Write a single recipe prompt that generates a complete, print-ready guide for a new student joining your school. The guide should:
- Be written in a friendly, not scary way
- Cover the top 5 things they absolutely need to know
- Include one funny tip and one serious one
- Be the right length to read in under 2 minutes
- Sound like it was written by a current student, not a teacher

Write out your full recipe (Context, Instruction, Format) before running it. After running it, score your own prompt on whether all three ingredients were doing their job. Then revise and run one more time. Did the second version improve?
