# Lesson Plan: AI in Your Daily Life
### Explorer Level (Ages 10–11) | Module 07

---

## About This Lesson

Most people imagine AI as something futuristic — a robot assistant, a self-driving car, a computer playing chess in a science lab. The truth is far more ordinary, and far more interesting. AI is already woven into almost every part of your daily life, working quietly in the background of apps, devices, and services you use without thinking. It is not announcing itself. It just... works. And once you know how to spot it, you will see it everywhere.

Think about the last time you watched a video online and a really good recommendation appeared for something you had not searched for but somehow wanted to watch. That was AI. Think about the last time your phone's keyboard guessed the next word you were about to type and it was exactly right. That was AI. Think about the last time a camera on your phone improved a dark photo automatically, or a navigation app warned you to take a different route because of traffic ahead. All of that — AI, running quietly, doing its job.

What makes this so interesting is that AI in daily life is not one thing — it is dozens of different techniques applied to dozens of different problems. The AI that recommends a song works completely differently from the AI that unlocks your phone with your face. The AI that auto-corrects your typing works differently from the AI that beats you at a video game. Each one was trained on different kinds of data and optimised to solve a specific problem. What they share is that they all learn from patterns — which is the one big idea you need to hold onto.

Understanding where AI is and what it is doing changes your relationship with technology. Instead of being a passive user who accepts whatever the algorithm serves you, you become someone who understands the invisible machinery — who can ask "why is this recommending this to me?", "whose interests does this algorithm serve?", and "what am I not seeing because of the choices this AI is making on my behalf?" Those are the questions of a smart, aware digital citizen — and that is what this lesson is designed to develop.

## Key Concepts

- **Recommendation algorithm:** An AI system that predicts what content you want to see next, based on what you and similar users have watched, listened to, or clicked before.
- **Computer vision:** AI that can understand and interpret images or video — used in face unlock, photo enhancement, and security cameras.
- **Natural language processing (NLP):** AI that understands and generates human language — used in voice assistants, spell-check, and chatbots.
- **Filter bubble:** When a recommendation AI only shows you content similar to things you have already liked, making your online world narrower over time.
- **Data personalisation:** The way AI uses your past behaviour to customise what you see, hear, or are offered.

---

## Fun Facts

- Netflix's recommendation AI is so central to the service that the company estimates it saves over $1 billion per year by keeping subscribers watching — if the AI did not exist, far more people would stop subscribing because they could not find things to watch.
- The face recognition AI in modern smartphones was trained on millions of photographs of human faces — it has "seen" more faces than any person could look at in thousands of lifetimes.
- Spam filters (the AI that keeps junk emails out of your inbox) are some of the oldest and most effective AI systems ever built — they learn to spot new types of spam within hours of those spam emails first appearing.

## Try It — Spot the Hidden AI

### Activity 1: The AI Treasure Hunt (10 mins)

AI is hiding everywhere. Your job is to find it.

Go through this list and tick every one that applies to your life:

**On your phone or tablet:**
- [ ] My phone suggests words when I type a message
- [ ] My phone unlocks using my face or fingerprint
- [ ] A map app has ever suggested the fastest route for me
- [ ] A camera has ever made my photos look brighter or removed red-eye
- [ ] An app has ever recommended a video, song, or game to me

**In games:**
- [ ] I have played a game where enemies figure out where I am hiding
- [ ] A game has ever adjusted its difficulty because I was doing too well or struggling
- [ ] I have played against a computer opponent that felt like it was thinking

**In music and video:**
- [ ] Spotify, YouTube, or another app has ever said "you might like this"
- [ ] A voice assistant (Siri, Alexa, Google) has ever answered a question for me
- [ ] Subtitles or captions have ever appeared automatically on a video

Count your ticks. Write down your total: ______

Most people get at least 7. Every single one of those is AI doing its job.

Now pick **three** from your list. For each one, write one sentence explaining what you think the AI is doing. For example:

> "When YouTube recommends a video, I think the AI looks at what I have watched before and finds videos that are similar."

You do not need to be exactly right — just give it your best guess. You will check your thinking in Activity 3.

---

### Activity 2: Interview a Smart Assistant (10 mins)

If you have access to a voice assistant (Siri, Alexa, Google Assistant) or a chatbot like ChatGPT, this activity will show you just how much AI can understand — and where it gets confused.

Ask a smart assistant **all five** of these questions and note what happens:

1. "What is the weather like today?" — Does it know where you are?
2. "Tell me a joke." — Is the joke actually funny?
3. "What did I eat for breakfast?" — What does it say?
4. "Remind me to do my homework in one hour." — Does it understand the request?
5. "Who is my best friend?" — What does it say?

After asking all five, think about this:

- Questions 1, 2, and 4 probably worked well. Why do you think that is?
- Questions 3 and 5 probably failed or gave a strange answer. Why?

The big idea: AI assistants are good at questions that have answers in public data or in your device's settings. They cannot know personal things about your life — your breakfast, your friends, your feelings — unless you have told them.

Write two sentences: one thing the assistant was good at, and one thing it could not do.

---

### Activity 3: Check Your Guesses (10 mins)

Remember those three guesses you wrote in Activity 1? Now let's fact-check them using an AI tool itself.

Open ChatGPT or Claude and ask:

> "I am 10 years old learning about AI. Can you explain in simple words how [name the thing — e.g. Spotify recommendations] uses AI? Keep it short and easy to understand."

Do this for each of your three guesses.

Then compare:
- Was your guess correct, partially correct, or quite different?
- Did anything surprise you?

Write a short paragraph (3–5 sentences) about what you found out. Start with: "Before this lesson, I thought..."

---

### Activity 4: The Filter Bubble Investigation (10 mins)

This activity explores one of the most important and sometimes worrying things about recommendation AI: the filter bubble.

When an AI only recommends things similar to what you have already liked, your experience of the internet gets narrower over time. You keep seeing the same types of content, the same opinions, the same styles — and things that are different or challenging rarely reach you.

Think about your own experience of recommendations (YouTube, Spotify, a game store, or anywhere):

1. What types of content do you get recommended most often? (List 3–5 types)
2. When was the last time an AI recommendation surprised you with something genuinely different from your usual interests?
3. Can you think of something interesting you might be missing because the AI has never seen you show interest in it?

Then ask an AI tool: "What are the downsides of recommendation algorithms? Why do some people worry about filter bubbles?"

Write 3–5 sentences summarising what you learned. Does any of it apply to your own online experience?

## Draw It!

Draw a picture of yourself going through a single morning — from waking up to arriving at school (or sitting down at your desk at home).

Circle every moment where AI might be involved. Think about:
- Your alarm or phone
- The route to school
- Music or podcasts you listen to
- The suggestions on your apps
- Any cameras you pass

Label each circle with what the AI is doing. Try to find at least **five AI moments** in your morning.

Add a title to your picture: **"My AI-Powered Morning"**

## Reflect
Answer these questions out loud or write them down:

1. Before this lesson, did you think AI was a big part of your daily life? Has that changed?
2. Most of the AI you spotted was trying to be helpful. Can you think of a situation where one of those "helpful" AIs might actually be annoying or unfair?
3. Spotify and YouTube recommend things based on your past choices. What could go wrong with that? (Hint: think about whether you would ever discover anything new.)
4. Now that you know about filter bubbles, what is one thing you could do to deliberately break out of your recommendation bubble?
5. Should companies have to tell you clearly when AI is making decisions about what you see? Why or why not?

## Level Up Challenge

Conduct a 24-hour AI audit.

For one full day, keep a running list of every time you notice AI at work around you. Include:
- The app, device, or service
- What the AI was doing
- Whether you thought it did a good job or not
- One question the AI's action raised for you

At the end of the day, count your total AI interactions and write a one-paragraph reflection: "I had no idea how often AI was affecting my day. The most surprising moment was..."

If you cannot do a full day, do it for 2–3 hours and estimate what a full day would look like.
