# Video Script: When AI Gets It Wrong
### Explorer Level (Ages 10–11) | Module 04 | ~6 minutes

---

## HOOK (0:00–0:35)

**[HOST, holding up a funny AI error — could be a made-up example]**

"I once asked an AI to tell me the name of the highest mountain in Australia. It told me Mount Kosciuszko. That's correct!

Then I asked it what the population of a town near me was. It gave me a number with total confidence.

The number was completely made up.

Same confidence. Totally different accuracy. How do you know which is which?

That's today's episode."

**[TITLE CARD: "When AI Gets It Wrong"]**

---

## WHY AI MAKES MISTAKES (0:35–2:00)

**[HOST, friendly explanation]**

"Remember — AI is a pattern spotter. It learned from a huge amount of information. But a few things can go wrong:

**1. The training data was old.** AI doesn't always know about recent events. Things that happened after it was trained? It might not know — or worse, it might guess.

**2. The pattern it matched was wrong.** Sometimes AI confidently matches the wrong pattern. It sounds right, but it's not.

**3. It filled in a gap.** If AI doesn't know something specific — like the exact population of your town — it might just invent something that *sounds right*. This is called a **hallucination**.

Hallucination is a funny word. It means AI said something confidently that isn't true — like it saw something that wasn't there."

---

## THE THREE CHECKS (2:00–4:00)

**[HOST, ticking off on fingers]**

"So how do you know when to trust AI and when to double-check?

Here are my three checks:

**Check 1: Is this a fact that could be wrong?**
Things like names, numbers, dates, statistics, recent news — these can be wrong. Creative things (stories, poems, ideas) can't really be 'wrong,' just good or not.

If it's a fact → check it!

**Check 2: Does it make sense?**
Read the answer and ask: does this actually make sense? Does anything sound odd or too convenient? If something seems too perfectly right, it might be too perfectly made up.

**Check 3: Can I verify it?**
Can you Google it? Can you check a book? Can you ask a teacher or parent? If you can verify it in 30 seconds — do it.

The rule is simple: **if you're going to share it or use it for something important — check it first.**"

---

## HOW TO FIX IT (4:00–5:00)

**[HOST, demo]**

"And here's the really useful part: when AI gets something wrong, you don't have to start over. You can *fix it*.

If the AI wrote a story but got a detail wrong — just tell it:
'That's not right — the character is supposed to be a scientist, not a baker. Can you fix that?'

If it gave you a fact that seems wrong — say:
'I'm not sure that's correct. Where would I look to verify this? And can you try again?'

AI doesn't get embarrassed. It doesn't take it personally. You can correct it as many times as you want.

This is called **debugging** — a word from programming that means finding and fixing mistakes. You're debugging your prompt and the AI's output."

---

## CHALLENGE (5:00–5:35)

**[HOST]**

"Your challenge: go on a fact-checking mission!

Ask AI three questions where the answers are facts — things you can check. Then actually check them using Google or a book or asking an adult.

Score the AI: how many did it get right?

Also try asking: 'Are you sure about that?' after an answer and see if it changes its mind. Sometimes it does!

Write down what you find."
