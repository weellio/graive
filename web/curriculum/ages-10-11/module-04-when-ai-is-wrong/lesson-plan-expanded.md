# Lesson Plan: When AI Gets It Wrong
### Explorer Level (Ages 10–11) | Module 04

---

## About This Lesson

Imagine asking a very well-read friend for help with your homework. This friend has read thousands of books, encyclopedias, and websites — but they read them all a couple of years ago and cannot check anything new. They also have a habit of sounding absolutely certain even when they are guessing. Oh, and sometimes when they do not know something, instead of saying "I don't know," they invent a plausible-sounding answer. That is AI, and it is exactly why this lesson might be the most important one in the whole course.

AI makes a specific type of mistake called a hallucination — a word that sounds dramatic but really just means "making something up confidently." This happens because AI is not looking up facts the way you look something up in a book. It is predicting what words should come next based on patterns. Most of the time, those patterns lead to correct information. But sometimes the AI pattern-matches its way into producing something that sounds real but isn't. It might invent a book title, get a historical date wrong, or describe a scientific process that nobody has ever actually proven.

The tricky part is that AI hallucinations do not look different from correct information. The AI does not add a little warning sign when it is unsure. It delivers made-up information in exactly the same calm, confident tone as completely accurate information. That is what makes this skill so important: you cannot rely on how confident the AI sounds. You have to develop your own instinct for what needs checking, and your own habits for checking it.

The good news is that this is a learnable skill. Once you understand the types of questions where AI tends to go wrong — specific facts, recent events, local information, quotations by real people — you start to develop a healthy instinct. You stop trusting everything automatically. You start asking: "How would the AI know this? Can I verify it? Does this actually make sense?" Those three questions are the foundation of everything in this lesson.

---

## Key Concepts

- **Hallucination:** When AI confidently states something false — not because it is lying, but because it is pattern-matching rather than fact-checking.
- **Fact-checking:** The process of verifying information against a reliable, independent source.
- **Confidence vs. accuracy:** These are not the same thing. AI can sound very confident while being completely wrong.
- **Primary source:** The original, authoritative place a fact comes from — a scientific study, an official website, a direct quote.
- **Verification habit:** The regular practice of checking AI claims before using or sharing them.

---

## Fun Facts

- In a test by lawyers in the United States, an AI tool invented fake legal cases — complete with realistic-sounding case names, judges, and dates — that had never actually existed. The lawyers used these in real court documents before noticing.
- Researchers have found that AI is especially likely to hallucinate when asked about obscure topics, recent events, or specific statistics — exactly the kinds of things that are hardest to double-check quickly.
- When you tell an AI "you are wrong about that," it will often apologise and change its answer — even if its original answer was actually correct. This tells you something important: AI changes its answer based on social pressure, not because it has checked anything.

## Try It — The Fact-Check Mission

### Mission 1: Test Three Facts (15 mins)

Ask AI three factual questions. Choose things you can check:

Ideas for questions:
- What is the capital city of [a country you're studying]?
- How many bones are in the human body?
- What year was [a famous thing] invented?
- What is the largest animal on Earth?
- What is [a word you don't know] defined as?

| Question | AI's Answer | I checked and found: | Correct? |
|----------|-------------|---------------------|----------|
| | | | |
| | | | |
| | | | |

**Score:** AI got ___ out of 3 correct.

---

### Mission 2: The Confidence Test (10 mins)

After AI answers something — try asking: **"Are you sure about that?"**

What happens? Does it:
- Stick with its original answer? ✅
- Immediately change to a different answer? (consider this a warning sign)
- Admit it might not be certain? ✅

Try this with 2 different questions. Write down what happens.

**What does this tell you about trusting AI?**

---

### Mission 3: Fix a Mistake (5 mins)

Ask AI to write something creative but include a specific detail that's wrong on purpose:

Example: "Write a story about a whale who lives in the desert."

Then tell the AI: "Actually, whales can't live in the desert — they breathe air but need the ocean. Can you fix the story so it makes more scientific sense while still being fun?"

See how it adjusts!

---

### Mission 4: The Quotation Trap (10 mins)

One of the most common types of AI hallucination involves quotations — AI often "quotes" famous people saying things they never actually said.

Try this: Ask the AI for three famous quotes about learning, courage, or kindness (you choose the topic). Ask it to include the full name of the person who said each quote.

Then take ONE of those quotes and search for it on Google. Paste the exact quote into your search engine with the person's name. See if you can find a reliable source confirming they actually said it.

| Quote | Person AI claimed said it | Did you find a reliable source? | Verdict |
|-------|--------------------------|--------------------------------|---------|
| | | | |

Write one sentence about what you found. Were any of the quotes hard to verify?

**The big idea:** Famous fake quotes spread all over the internet — and AI learns from the internet. This means AI can confidently "recall" quotes that have been invented and shared millions of times online.

---

### Mission 5: The Recency Check (5 mins)

AI has a knowledge cut-off date — a point in time after which it does not know what happened in the world. This makes it unreliable for recent events.

Ask the AI: "Who won [a major recent sports competition or award]?" Choose something that happened within the last year or two.

Then search for the real answer.

Did the AI know? Did it admit it might not know? Or did it guess confidently?

Write down what happened and why this matters for anyone using AI for research on current events.

## The Hallucination Spotter Card

Draw this in your notebook as a reminder:

```
THE HALLUCINATION SPOTTER

Before you believe something AI says:

[] Is this a FACT (not just a story)?
  - If yes, check it!

[] Does this MAKE SENSE?
  - If it seems oddly perfect... double check

[] CAN I VERIFY IT in 30 seconds?
  - Then do it!

[] Is this about a REAL PERSON or REAL QUOTE?
  - Extra caution — AI often invents these

[] Is this about RECENT EVENTS?
  - AI may not know — search for current info
```

## Reflect
1. Did AI get all three of your facts right? Were you surprised?
2. What happened when you said "Are you sure?" — what does that make you think?
3. In what situations would it be *most important* to fact-check AI? (Think: when might getting it wrong cause real problems?)
4. After the Quotation Trap activity, do you think it matters if someone shares a fake quote that sounds inspiring, even if the person never said it? Why or why not?
5. What is the difference between AI being wrong and AI "lying"? Can AI actually lie?

## Level Up Challenge

Become a hallucination hunter.

Ask the AI to write a short (200-word) article about a real but relatively obscure historical event (something from history class, or something you are curious about). Then fact-check every single specific claim in the article — every date, name, place, and number — against a reliable source.

Create a table like this:

| Claim in AI's article | Source I checked | Verdict: Correct / Wrong / Unverifiable |
|----------------------|-----------------|----------------------------------------|
| | | |
| | | |

Count the total number of checkable claims. How many were correct? What percentage was that?

Finally, write a one-paragraph "editor's note" at the bottom of the article explaining which parts need correcting before the article could be published.

This is actually what journalists and researchers do when they use AI as a first draft tool. You just did a real professional skill.
