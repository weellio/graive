# Lesson Plan: AI and Art — Who's the Artist?
### Explorer Level (Ages 10–11) | Module 07

## Objective
By the end of this lesson, you'll understand how AI generates images, why people argue about whether AI can be "creative," and what makes human-made art special.

## Key Concepts

### What Is an AI Image Generator?
AI image generators like DALL-E, Midjourney, and Adobe Firefly have looked at hundreds of millions of images from the internet. They've learned what things *look like* — cats, sunsets, paintings, cartoon characters — and they can combine those patterns to make new pictures when you describe what you want.

> "AI makes images by mashing up patterns from images it has already seen. It doesn't dream them up from nothing."

### The Creativity Question
Here's something that even adults argue about: if an AI makes a beautiful painting, is it *creative*?

Some people say: Yes! The result is beautiful and new.
Some people say: No! It's just remixing. Real creativity means having feelings, experiences, and ideas.

There's no easy answer — and that's what makes it interesting to think about.

### Who Is the Artist?
When you use an AI tool to make an image, think about who contributed:
- **You** wrote the description (called a "prompt")
- **The programmers** built the AI
- **The AI** generated the image
- **The human artists** whose work trained the AI

It's a bit like a team — but who gets the credit?

## Try It — Become an AI Art Director

### Activity 1: Your First AI Artwork (10 mins)

Open an AI image tool. You are going to be the **art director** — that means you're in charge of telling the AI exactly what to make.

Try these prompts one at a time:

1. `a friendly dragon reading a book in a cosy library`
2. `a robot made of vegetables having a picnic`
3. `a magical forest at night with glowing mushrooms and tiny stars`

For each image, write down:
- What did you like about it?
- What was surprising or weird?
- Did it match what you imagined in your head?

**My favourite prompt was:** ___
**Because:** ___

---

### Activity 2: Human vs AI Art Comparison (10 mins)

Now do this experiment:

**Part A:** Pick one of the prompts from Activity 1. Draw it yourself on paper. Take about 5 minutes — it doesn't have to be perfect. Just draw what YOU imagined.

**Part B:** Look at your drawing and the AI's version side by side.

Answer these questions:
1. Which one looks more like what was in your head?
2. Which one do you think is more interesting or unique? Why?
3. What does your drawing have that the AI's doesn't?

My drawing had: ___
The AI's image had: ___

---

### Activity 3: The Creativity Rating (5 mins)

Think about what you just experienced and rate these statements:

"The AI image felt creative and surprising." ___/10

"My own drawing felt creative and surprising." ___/10

"I think AI can be called an artist." ___/10

"I think humans will always make better art than AI." ___/10

There are no right or wrong answers! These are YOUR opinions.

## The Tough Question: Borrowed Art?

Here's something important to know: when AI was trained, it learned from images made by real human artists — many of whom didn't agree to have their art used for training.

Some artists are upset about this. They feel like the AI "copied" their style without asking or paying them.

Think about this:
- If someone read all your stories and learned to write *just like you* — how would you feel?
- Is that the same as copying, or is it just learning?

Write one sentence about how you think AI should treat the artists whose work it learned from:

I think AI companies should: ___

---

## Reflect
Answer out loud or write your answers:

1. What is one thing that surprised you about how AI makes images?
2. Do YOU think AI is being creative, or just very good at copying? Explain your thinking.
3. If you were going to use AI to make artwork for a school project, what would you want to tell people about how it was made?

## Challenge
**The Better Prompt Challenge:**

Take one of these starting prompts and try to make it as specific and creative as possible. The more detail you add, the more interesting the result usually is.

Starting prompt: `a dog at school`

Try adding: style (cartoon, realistic, watercolour), time of day, what the dog is doing, what it's feeling, what's in the background...

My best version of the prompt: ___

Compare your first result with your final result. Which is better, and why?
