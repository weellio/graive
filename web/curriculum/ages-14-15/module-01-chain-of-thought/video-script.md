Ask AI to *think through a problem with you* — step by step, out loud, showing its reasoning — and you get something completely different. You get a thinking partner.

That's chain of thought prompting. And it's the difference between using AI as a vending machine and using it as a colleague.

Let me show you what I mean."

## THE PROBLEM WITH ASKING FOR ANSWERS (0:45–2:30)

**[HOST, screen share]**

"Here's a real limitation most people don't know about: AI models actually perform worse on complex problems when you just ask for the answer directly.

Let me demonstrate.

**[TYPE:]** 'A train leaves London at 9am travelling at 80mph. Another train leaves Birmingham at 9:30am travelling towards London at 100mph. The cities are 120 miles apart. When do they meet?'

**[Show output — often the AI gets this wrong or rushes to an answer]**

Now watch what happens when I change the prompt:"

**[TYPE:]** 'Work through this step by step, showing each calculation. Before giving an answer, identify all the variables, set up the equation, then solve it. A train leaves London at 9am...'

**[Show output — AI walks through the problem correctly]**

"Same problem. But when I asked it to *show its work*, it actually got the right answer. This is called Chain of Thought — and it works because thinking in steps reduces errors, just like it does for humans."

---

## CONCEPT: What Is Chain of Thought? (2:30–4:30)

**[HOST, animated breakdown]**

"Chain of Thought (CoT) prompting is a technique where you ask the AI to reason through a problem *before* giving its final answer. You're making the intermediate thinking visible.

Three ways to trigger it:

**1. Explicit instruction**
> 'Think through this step by step before giving your final answer.'
> 'Reason through this carefully. Show your work.'

**2. Breaking the problem yourself**
Instead of one question, you sequence the problem:
> 'First, identify the key factors. Then, consider the pros and cons of each. Finally, give me a recommendation.'

**3. Roleplay the thinking**
> 'Pretend you are a detective. Lay out the evidence before you state your conclusion. What do you know? What do you not know? What can you infer?'

**[ANIMATED: three prompt patterns side by side]**

The key insight is this: **process matters as much as output.** When you can see the reasoning, you can catch errors, push back on steps you disagree with, and understand *why* you got a particular answer."

## DEMO: Using CoT for a Real Problem (4:30–7:00)

**[HOST, screen share — picking a meaty non-math problem]**

"Let me show you chain of thought on something more interesting than a maths problem.

I'm going to ask AI to help me think through whether a school should ban smartphones.

**Bad version:**
> 'Should schools ban smartphones?'

**[Show output — generic pros and cons, no real depth]**

**CoT version:**
> 'I want you to reason through the question of whether schools should ban smartphones. Don't give me an immediate answer.
>
> First, identify the key stakeholders (students, teachers, parents, school admin) and what each would care about most.
>
> Second, identify what the evidence actually shows about smartphones and learning — separate what's well-established from what's debated.
>
> Third, identify the strongest argument FOR a ban and the strongest argument AGAINST.
>
> Fourth, identify what information would be most important to know before making a decision.
>
> Only after doing all of this, give me your tentative view.'

**[Show output — genuinely thoughtful, multi-perspective analysis]**

See how much richer that is? It didn't just give me an answer — it helped me *think*.

And here's the move that makes you smarter: after you read it, **push back on the step you disagree with.** Not the conclusion — a specific step in the reasoning.

**[TYPE:]** 'I think step 2 is missing something — you didn't mention the difference between phone access during class vs during breaks. Can you revise that section?'

**[Show how the AI refines its analysis]**

You're not just consuming AI output. You're having an argument with it. That's when it becomes genuinely useful."

## THE METACOGNITIVE MOVE (7:00–8:00)

**[HOST, camera]**

"Here's the most advanced thing I'll say today.

Using chain of thought isn't just about getting better AI outputs. It's about learning *how to think*.

When you practice asking AI to break down its reasoning, you start doing the same thing in your own head. You get better at:
- Separating what you know from what you're assuming
- Identifying where an argument is weakest
- Seeing problems from multiple angles before reaching a conclusion

The goal isn't to let the AI think for you. The goal is to let the AI's process *model* good thinking — and pick up those habits yourself."

---

## CHALLENGE (8:00–8:30)

**[HOST]**

"Your challenge: pick a decision you need to make or a question you've been thinking about. Could be school-related, personal, or about the world.

Use chain of thought prompting to work through it with AI. At least four steps before the final answer.

Then — push back on one step. Change one assumption. See how the conclusion changes.

What did you learn from the process that you wouldn't have gotten from just asking for an answer?"
