## Background

Intellectual maturity is not the same as knowing a lot of facts. Facts can be looked up; intellectual maturity cannot be downloaded. It is the capacity to hold a complex question in your mind without prematurely resolving it, to genuinely consider perspectives you initially disagree with, to update your beliefs when evidence demands it, and to act decisively even under uncertainty. These are capacities you build through practice — through repeatedly engaging with hard questions in structured, demanding ways.

Across the Thinker curriculum, you have done exactly this. You have learned to use AI as a tool for structured reasoning rather than a shortcut around it. You have investigated the sources and consequences of AI bias. You have followed the money through the digital economy and understood how attention is commodified. You have applied task-level analysis to real careers. You have distinguished productive from exploitative AI use in learning. You have thought seriously about digital identity, AI and mental health, copyright, fact-checking, and global AI equity. You have designed a solution to a real problem. Each of these modules asked you not just to receive information but to engage with it — to test it, push back on it, apply it, and form your own views.

The Thinker Challenge is your opportunity to synthesise all of this. You will choose a genuine AI controversy — one with real stakes, real disagreement among thoughtful people, and no easy answer — and you will research it, argue it, and defend a position. This is not an exercise in performing an opinion. It is an exercise in forming one carefully and being willing to have it challenged.

The topics available to you span everything from surveillance and privacy to creative rights to the governance of powerful AI systems. Each one connects to things you have studied in this curriculum, but each also extends into territory you will need to explore fresh. The ability to encounter a new domain, quickly orient yourself to the key debates, find and evaluate sources, and form a defensible position is exactly the skill that serves you in university, in careers, and as a citizen making sense of an increasingly complicated world.

A note on the debate: arguing for a position you hold is relatively easy. Arguing for a position you do not hold — or having your position genuinely challenged by someone arguing the other side — is harder and more valuable. The willingness to engage seriously with the best version of an argument you disagree with, rather than dismissing the weakest version, is the mark of an intellectually honest thinker. Strive for that in this challenge.

## The Big Picture

The AI controversies in this challenge are not hypothetical debates for a classroom exercise. They are live questions being decided right now in legislatures, courts, boardrooms, and editorial offices around the world. The EU AI Act, passed in 2024, directly addresses several of them. The US is actively debating AI regulation, copyright reform, and the governance of advanced AI systems. Journalists, lawyers, policymakers, ethicists, and engineers are working on all of these questions simultaneously.

Your voice — informed, reasoned, and willing to be tested — is genuinely needed in these debates. Not eventually, when you are an adult professional, but now. Young people who can articulate thoughtful positions on these issues, who can present evidence, acknowledge complexity, and hold their ground under challenge, are exactly the kind of citizens and future professionals that institutions need. This is not flattery; it is a realistic assessment of a gap in public discourse.

## Key Terms

> **Position paper:** A short, structured written argument stating a clearly defined stance on a controversial question, supported by evidence, and engaging honestly with opposing arguments.
>
> **Steelmanning:** Presenting the strongest possible version of an argument you oppose — the intellectual opposite of a strawman.
>
> **Burden of proof:** The obligation to provide sufficient evidence for a claim. In debates, the question of who bears the burden of proof (the person proposing change, or the person defending the status quo) is itself often contested.
>
> **Evidence vs. argument:** Evidence is factual information (statistics, research findings, documented cases). Argument is the logical structure connecting evidence to a conclusion. Both are required for a sound position.
>
> **Intellectual humility:** The recognition that your current beliefs may be wrong and the willingness to revise them when presented with sufficient reason to do so.
>
> **False dichotomy:** A logical error in which two options are presented as the only possibilities when in fact a range of positions exist. Often used to polarise debates that actually have nuanced middle grounds.
>
> **Epistemic standards:** The criteria by which claims are evaluated — what counts as good enough evidence, what level of certainty is required before acting, whose testimony is credible.

## The Challenge — Two Parts

---

### Part 1: The Research Project (35–40 mins)

**Step 1 — Choose Your Controversy (5 mins)**

Choose one AI controversy from this list, or propose your own:

1. Should AI-generated content be labelled? Should platforms be legally required to mark AI text, images, and audio?
2. Should schools ban the use of AI for homework and essays? Or should they teach students to use it responsibly?
3. Should facial recognition AI be used by police? What safeguards, if any, would make it acceptable?
4. Should AI therapy apps be regulated as medical devices?
5. Should there be a global "pause" on the development of the most powerful AI systems while governments catch up with regulation?
6. Should AI companies be legally liable when their AI causes harm?
7. Should countries be allowed to use AI surveillance tools to monitor their own citizens?

Write your chosen controversy at the top of your document: **"My Controversy: ______"**

---

**Middle Ground:**
Are there any nuanced positions — people who say "yes, but only if..." or "no, except when..."? What conditions or safeguards come up repeatedly in the debate?

---

**Step 3 — Your Position (15 mins)**

Write a **400-word position paper:**

**Paragraph 1 — Introduction:**
State the controversy and briefly explain why it matters. End with your thesis — your position in one sentence.

**Paragraph 2 — Your strongest argument:**
Present the most compelling reason for your position. Include evidence from your research.

**Paragraph 3 — Acknowledge the other side:**
Describe the strongest argument against your position fairly. Then explain why you are not persuaded by it, or why your argument outweighs it.

**Paragraph 4 — Conclusion:**
Restate your position. Note one condition that would change your mind — what evidence or development would make you reconsider?

Read it back. A good position paper should be something an intelligent person who disagrees with you would take seriously.

---

### Part 2: The Debate (30–35 mins)

**If you have a partner or small group:**

Run a structured debate. You will defend your position paper; your partner or group will argue against it.

**Debate Format:**

| Stage | Who | Time |
|-------|-----|------|
| Opening statement | Person A | 2 mins |
| Opening statement | Person B | 2 mins |
| Rebuttal | Person A | 2 mins |
| Rebuttal | Person B | 2 mins |
| Questions from partner/group | Both | 4 mins |
| Closing statements | Both | 1 min each |

After the debate, discuss:
- Did arguing against your own position (if you did) change your thinking at all?
- What was the strongest argument made against your position?
- Is there anything you want to change about your position paper after the debate?

**If you are working solo:**

Write a **"Devil's Advocate" document** — a 200-word argument against your own position, made as strong as possible. Then write a 100-word response to it.

## Deep Dive — The Expert Landscape

For any significant AI controversy, there is an expert ecosystem — researchers, advocacy organisations, policy bodies, and industry groups who are actively working on the issue. Understanding who the key voices are and what their positions and incentives are is essential context for forming your own view.

**Research task:** For your chosen controversy, map the expert landscape.

Use AI and a search engine:
```
Who are the most prominent expert voices on [CONTROVERSY]?
List at least six organisations or individuals on different sides:
- Academic researchers working on this question
- Policy organisations or think tanks
- Industry bodies or company positions
- Civil society organisations or advocacy groups
- Government bodies or regulatory agencies
For each, give their position and any relevant information about their funding or potential incentives.
```

Then verify this overview with your own searching. Look specifically for:
- Anyone whose position might be influenced by who funds them (e.g., a "think tank" funded by tech companies arguing against tech regulation)
- Academic researchers publishing peer-reviewed work with no obvious commercial interest
- Affected communities or individuals — whose lived experience is relevant to this debate?

After mapping the landscape, add a short paragraph to your position paper: "The main expert voices I considered in forming this position were..."

This is the difference between an opinion and an informed position.

## Debate This

Use these questions for extended discussion or as additional debate topics if you want to explore further:

1. **Should AI systems be required to explain their decisions in plain language to the people they affect?** This is called "explainability" or "interpretability." A hiring algorithm that rejected your application could, in principle, be required to explain which factors drove the decision. But some AI systems are so complex that meaningful explanations are technically difficult. Should "I cannot explain my decision" be an acceptable answer for an AI system making consequential choices about people's lives?

2. **Is the development of general AI — systems that can perform any cognitive task at human level — something we should be trying to accelerate, slow down, or stop?** Some of the world's most eminent AI researchers and technologists have signed letters calling for a pause. Others argue that safety is best advanced by moving quickly and making progress in the open. This is the defining technical-political question of the coming decade. Where do you stand?

3. **Should the people most affected by an AI system have a formal vote on whether it is deployed?** Community consent is standard in some contexts (planning applications, clinical trials, environmental decisions). AI systems deployed in policing, healthcare, education, and benefits allocation profoundly affect communities. Should those communities have a formal veto? What would a consent process for AI deployment look like?
