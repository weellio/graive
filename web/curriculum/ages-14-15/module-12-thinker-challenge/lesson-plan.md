# Lesson Plan: Thinker Challenge
### Thinker Level (Ages 14–15) | Module 12

---

## Objective
By the end of this lesson, you have completed a structured research project and a debate exercise on a genuine AI controversy — demonstrating your ability to understand multiple perspectives, argue a position with evidence, and engage with difficult questions that have no easy answers.

---

## What You'll Need
- A device with internet access
- A free AI tool (ChatGPT free or Claude free)
- Access to Google Scholar, news databases, or your school library
- Paper and coloured pens OR a digital document
- A partner or small group for the debate (optional but strongly recommended)
- About 75–90 minutes (this is your capstone — do it justice)

---

## Watch First
Watch **Module 12: Thinker Challenge** with a parent or on your own.

Remember: The goal of a Thinker is not to have the loudest opinion — it is to have the most carefully examined one. By the end of this challenge, you should be able to say: "I have looked at this from every angle. Here is where I stand and here is why — and here is what would change my mind."

---

## The Challenge — Two Parts

---

### Part 1: The Research Project (35–40 mins)

**Step 1 — Choose Your Controversy (5 mins)**

Choose one AI controversy from this list, or propose your own (check with a parent or teacher if unsure):

1. Should AI-generated content be labelled? Should platforms be legally required to mark AI text, images, and audio?
2. Should schools ban the use of AI for homework and essays? Or should they teach students to use it responsibly?
3. Should facial recognition AI be used by police? What safeguards, if any, would make it acceptable?
4. Should AI therapy apps be regulated as medical devices?
5. Should there be a global "pause" on the development of the most powerful AI systems while governments catch up with regulation?
6. Should AI companies be legally liable when their AI causes harm?
7. Should countries be allowed to use AI surveillance tools to monitor their own citizens?

Write your chosen controversy at the top of your document: **"My Controversy: ______"**

---

**Step 2 — Research Both Sides (20 mins)**

Before you form your own view, your job is to understand the strongest arguments on each side.

For each side, find at least **two real sources** (news articles, academic papers, expert opinions, or official reports). Do not rely only on AI — go to real sources.

Use this research framework:

**Side A — The Case FOR [your controversy position]:**
- Argument 1: [What is the strongest reason in favour?]
- Source: [Where does this argument come from — who makes it?]
- Evidence: [What facts or examples support it?]

- Argument 2: [Second-strongest argument]

- Evidence:

**Side B — The Case AGAINST [your controversy position]:**

- Evidence:

**Middle Ground:**
Are there any nuanced positions — people who say "yes, but only if..." or "no, except when..."? What conditions or safeguards come up repeatedly in the debate?

---

**Step 3 — Your Position (15 mins)**

Now that you have researched both sides, write a **400-word position paper** — a short, structured argument for your view.

Structure it like this:

**Paragraph 1 — Introduction:**
State the controversy and briefly explain why it matters. End with your thesis — your position in one sentence.

**Paragraph 2 — Your strongest argument:**
Present the most compelling reason for your position. Include evidence from your research.

**Paragraph 3 — Acknowledge the other side:**
Describe the strongest argument against your position fairly. Then explain why you are not persuaded by it, or why your argument outweighs it.

**Paragraph 4 — Conclusion:**
Restate your position. Note one condition that would change your mind — what evidence or development would make you reconsider?

Read it back. A good position paper should be something an intelligent person who disagrees with you would take seriously — not because you softened your argument, but because you engaged honestly with theirs.

---

### Part 2: The Debate (30–35 mins)

**If you have a partner or small group:**

Run a structured debate. You will defend your position paper; your partner or group will argue against it (even if they personally agree with you — arguing a position you do not hold is a powerful thinking exercise).

**Debate Format (15-minute version):**

| Stage | Who | Time |
|-------|-----|------|
| Opening statement | Person A | 2 mins |
| Opening statement | Person B | 2 mins |
| Rebuttal | Person A | 2 mins |
| Rebuttal | Person B | 2 mins |
| Questions from partner/group | Both | 4 mins |
| Closing statements | Both | 1 min each |

After the debate, discuss:
- Did arguing against your own position (if you did) change your thinking at all?
- What was the strongest argument made against your position?
- Is there anything you want to change about your position paper after the debate?

**If you are working solo:**

Write a **"Devil's Advocate" document** — a 200-word argument against your own position, made as strong as possible. Then write a 100-word response to it. This is the solo equivalent of the debate — arguing with yourself is an underrated way to strengthen your thinking.

---

## Design It!

Create a **"Thinker Certificate"** — your capstone completion document.

Include:
- Your name and the date
- Your chosen controversy
- Your position (one sentence)
- The strongest argument you encountered that challenged your view (one sentence)
- Three things you now know about AI that you did not know at the start of the Thinker curriculum
- One question you still have that this curriculum did not fully answer
- A personal motto for how you will engage with AI in your life going forward (one sentence — make it yours)

Sign it. This represents the thinking work you have done across 12 modules.

## Final Self-Assessment

Score your work across both parts:

| Criteria | 1 point | 2 points | 3 points |
|----------|---------|---------|---------|
| Research quality — did you use real sources beyond AI? | Only AI | One real source | Two or more real sources per side |
| Both sides — did you represent the opposition fairly? | Strawman or ignored | Acknowledged briefly | Genuinely engaged with |
| Position paper — is your argument evidence-based and structured? | Opinion without evidence | Some structure and evidence | Well-structured with clear evidence |
| Debate/Devil's advocate — did you genuinely engage with opposition? | Minimal | Decent effort | Genuinely challenged your own view |
| Overall — would a thoughtful adult take this seriously? | Probably not | Likely yes | Definitely yes |

**Total: ___ / 15**

| Score | Thinker Rank |
|-------|-------------|
| 13–15 | Master Thinker — you are ready for Innovator level |
| 10–12 | Skilled Thinker — excellent analytical work |
| 7–9 | Developing Thinker — strong effort, deepen the research |
| Under 7 | Emerging Thinker — the courage to engage hard questions matters |

## Reflect
Answer these questions out loud or write them down:

1. You have spent 12 modules thinking carefully about AI — not just how to use it, but what it means, who it affects, and what it gets wrong. How has your overall view of AI changed since Module 1?
2. What is the most important idea from the Thinker curriculum that you will carry with you — something that will change how you think or act going forward?
3. The next level — Innovator — is about building and shipping real things with AI. What do you want to build? What problem do you want to try to solve?

## Share (Optional)
Post your position paper somewhere real — share it with a teacher, publish it in a school blog, or simply have a proper conversation with an adult about your controversy and your position. The world needs young people who can engage with complex issues carefully and courageously. You have been practising exactly that.
