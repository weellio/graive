## Key Concepts

### AI Surveillance Technologies

**Facial Recognition:**
AI that identifies individuals from camera footage or photos by analysing facial geometry. Used by police, airports, retailers, and some governments.

**Object/Behaviour Recognition:**
AI that detects specific behaviours in video — loitering, running, carrying weapons, unusual crowds.

**Social Media Monitoring:**
AI that scans public posts for keywords, sentiment, and patterns — used by governments to track activists and by companies to monitor sentiment.

**Predictive Policing:**
AI that uses historical crime data to predict where and by whom crimes are likely to occur in the future.

**Social Credit Systems:**
A government system (most developed in China) that scores citizens on behaviour, financial history, and social compliance — affecting access to services, travel, and education.

**Gait Recognition:**
AI that identifies people by how they walk — even when their face is hidden or obscured.

> "Unlike a police officer who must decide to follow you, an AI surveillance system can track millions of people simultaneously, permanently, at almost no cost. This scale changes everything."

### The Security Argument
Proponents of AI surveillance argue:
- Genuine threats exist: terrorism, crime, missing children, trafficking
- AI catches things humans miss — faster pattern recognition
- Most people have "nothing to hide"
- Surveillance deters crime before it happens
- In democratic societies, oversight mechanisms prevent abuse

### The Civil Liberties Argument
Critics argue:
- Chilling effect: when people know they're watched, they self-censor and conform — damaging free expression, protest, and dissent
- Bias: facial recognition has documented higher error rates for darker-skinned women — meaning innocent people face more wrongful stops
- Mission creep: surveillance systems built for one purpose are always used for more
- No "nothing to hide": everyone has things they do legally but privately — medical, sexual, political, religious
- Authoritarian capture: even in democracies, surveillance infrastructure can be repurposed if governments change

### The "Nothing to Hide" Problem

> "The 'nothing to hide' argument assumes the government is always acting in good faith, that its definition of suspicious behaviour never changes, and that you will always be on the right side of those definitions. History suggests none of these are safe assumptions."

Think about: Were civil rights activists "innocent" under the surveillance systems of their time?

## Try It — Case Study Analysis

### Activity 1: The Case Files (20 mins)

Read each case study and answer the questions below each one.

**Case A: Live Facial Recognition at a Football Match**
A city deploys live facial recognition cameras at a major stadium, connected to a database of known criminal suspects. Supporters entering the match are scanned without being told. When a person matches a suspect on the database, police approach them. In testing, the system produced a 92% false positive rate — meaning most people flagged were innocent.

Questions:
1. What are the legitimate security arguments for this use?
2. What are the civil liberties concerns?
3. Does the 92% false positive rate change your view? What does it mean in practice?
4. Should spectators have been told they were being scanned? Why?

**Case B: Predictive Policing**
A city uses AI to predict which streets and individuals are most likely to be involved in crime, based on historical arrest data. Police are deployed more heavily in predicted areas. Critics note the data reflects historical over-policing of minority neighbourhoods — the AI effectively recommends policing the same communities more.

Questions:
1. If the AI's predictions are "statistically accurate" based on past data, is it still unfair? Explain your reasoning.
2. What does this system optimise for — crime reduction, or replicating historical patterns?
3. Who benefits from this system, and who is harmed?

**Case C: Social Credit in an Authoritarian State**
A government implements a social credit score affecting citizens' access to trains, planes, loans, and schools. Scores are reduced by jaywalking, failing to pay fines, spreading "negative" content online, or associating with people who have low scores.

Questions:
1. What might a government argue as the benefits of this system?
2. What does "spreading negative content online" mean in practice for political dissent?
3. Could a version of this system work in a democracy with strong protections? What protections would be needed?
