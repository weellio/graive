## Background

The digital divide — the gap between those with access to digital technology and those without — has been a concern since the early days of the internet. Early interventions focused on physical infrastructure: getting computers and internet connections to schools, libraries, and communities in low-income countries. As connectivity has spread, the divide has shifted from access to quality: who is represented in the data that powers digital systems, whose knowledge is encoded in AI tools, and whose cultural context is understood by the technology.

When we talk about AI being "trained on data," we are describing a process that is profoundly unequal in its inputs. The internet, from which most AI training data is scraped, reflects the demographics of who created digital content and in what languages. Roughly 55% of all web content is in English, a language spoken as a first language by only about 4% of the world's population. Mandarin Chinese, the first language of roughly 12% of people, accounts for around 3% of web content. Swahili, spoken by over 200 million people in East Africa, is represented by a tiny fraction of a percent. The consequence is that language models have vastly richer representations of some cultures than others — and this is not a technical problem so much as a consequence of historical inequalities in who got online first and who had the resources to create digital content at scale.

The implications are practical and immediate. Medical AI trained predominantly on images of lighter skin may fail to accurately diagnose conditions on darker skin. Agricultural AI trained on Western farming data may give harmful advice to farmers in sub-Saharan Africa or South Asia who face different soils, climates, and pest species. Legal AI trained on US or UK law is not fit for purpose in countries with entirely different legal traditions. Translation AI for high-resource languages is extraordinary; for low-resource languages, it may produce dangerous errors in medical or legal contexts where precision matters.

There is also a deeper problem about whose values get encoded. AI systems make decisions about what is "appropriate" content, what counts as "harmful" speech, what kinds of questions it is acceptable to answer. These decisions are made by humans — specifically, by the engineers, researchers, and content moderators at AI companies, most of whom are from Western, educated, industrialised backgrounds. Cultural practices that are normal or important in one context — forms of religious expression, political speech, traditional medical knowledge — may be filtered out or flagged as problematic by systems calibrated on very different cultural assumptions.

These are not problems without solutions, but the solutions require deliberate investment, genuine partnerships, and a recognition that the goal is not just to extend existing AI systems to underserved populations but to involve those populations in shaping AI from the ground up. Indigenous language AI projects, community-controlled AI governance, participatory data collection — these approaches exist and are producing results, but they require sustained commitment from governments, institutions, and the technology industry.

## The Big Picture

Global AI equity is one of the defining justice issues of the technology field. The ability to participate fully in an AI-enabled economy will increasingly depend on whether AI systems work well in your language and cultural context. Countries and communities that are underserved by global AI systems will face a compounding disadvantage: already less economically powerful, they will also be less able to benefit from AI productivity gains and more likely to be harmed by AI errors. Addressing this requires people who understand both the technical and political dimensions — careers in global health technology, international development, language technology, AI policy, and human rights all intersect with this set of problems.

## Key Terms

> **Low-resource language:** A language for which there is relatively little digitised text available to train AI models, typically resulting in weaker AI performance compared to high-resource languages like English.
>
> **Digital divide:** The gap between those who have meaningful access to digital technology and those who do not — expanding to include quality of AI representation, not just physical access.
>
> **Cultural calibration:** The extent to which an AI system's responses, values, and assumptions reflect a particular cultural context rather than being genuinely universal.
>
> **Participatory design:** An approach to technology development that involves intended users as active participants in design, testing, and governance, rather than passive recipients.
>
> **Language localisation:** Adapting a product not just for translation but for cultural context — idioms, norms, expectations, and values specific to a community.
>
> **Data sovereignty:** The principle that data about a community or nation should be subject to the laws and governance structures of that community or nation.
>
> **Training data representation:** How evenly or unevenly a model's training data reflects the diversity of its potential users.

## Think Deeper — Who Is AI For?

### Activity 1: The Language Experiment (15 mins)

**Part A — The Same Question, Multiple Languages:**

Ask your AI tool the same question in English, then in two or three other languages.

Try: "What are some famous traditional foods from your region, and what do they mean to people culturally?"

Or: "Who are the most important writers or poets from your cultural tradition?"

Test in English, and then in two of these languages if possible:
- Spanish
- Hindi or Bengali
- Swahili or Yoruba
- Arabic
- Japanese or Mandarin
- Welsh or Irish Gaelic
- An indigenous language if you have access

For each response:
- How long and detailed was the answer?
- Did it feel like the AI had rich knowledge, surface knowledge, or almost no knowledge?
- Did the AI answer from within the culture, or did it describe the culture from an outside perspective?

Write a brief comparison. Which language got the richest, most culturally embedded response?

**Part B — Ask directly:**

Ask your AI: "How much training data do you think exists in [language X] compared to English? And what are the practical consequences of that imbalance for speakers of that language?"

---

### Activity 2: Who Gets Left Out — Case Studies (15 mins)

**Case 1 — Facial Recognition Bias:**
Research by MIT Media Lab (Joy Buolamwini and Timnit Gebru, 2018) found that commercial facial recognition systems had significantly higher error rates for darker-skinned faces, particularly darker-skinned women. The systems were trained on datasets that overrepresented white male faces.

**Case 2 — Medical AI and Skin Tone:**
A study in The Lancet found that AI systems trained to detect skin conditions performed significantly worse on darker skin tones, because most medical training images featured lighter skin.

**Case 3 — Translation Quality Gaps:**
Major translation AI tools perform dramatically better for high-resource languages than for low-resource languages. For people in these communities, AI-powered translation offers limited help — and sometimes produces dangerous mistranslations in medical or legal contexts.

**Case 4 — Cultural Value Differences:**
AI safety filters calibrated by human reviewers, most of whom work in English-speaking countries, may flag content that is normal or important in one cultural context as problematic because the reviewers lacked cultural context.

**Your task:** For each case study:
1. Write one sentence identifying the root cause
2. Write one practical recommendation for how the problem could be reduced
3. Note who bears the cost of each problem — is it the AI company, or the affected community?

---

### Activity 3: Imagining a More Equitable AI (15 mins)

**Three broad approaches:**

**Approach A — Better Representation:** Actively include more diverse training data, partnering with communities in the Global South, indigenous groups, and speakers of low-resource languages.

**Approach B — Local Models:** Support the development of smaller, locally-trained models that serve specific languages and cultures well, rather than one global system.

**Approach C — Community Governance:** Give communities a voice in how AI systems that serve them are designed, trained, and governed.

**Your task:**

Part A — Write one paragraph for each approach: what it would achieve, what its main practical challenge is, and who would need to make it happen.

Part B — Write a 200-word policy recommendation for a government where 80% of the population does not speak English and most current AI tools barely work in the local language.

## Deep Dive — A Community in the Digital Dark

This activity asks you to investigate the real situation for a specific underserved language community.

**Research task:** Choose ONE language community that you believe is likely to be underrepresented in AI training data. This could be:

- An indigenous language (e.g., Māori, Welsh, a Native American language, an Australian Aboriginal language)
- A widely spoken but digitally underrepresented language (e.g., Amharic, Hausa, Bengali, Swahili)
- A sign language (e.g., British Sign Language, American Sign Language)

Use AI and a search engine to investigate:

1. **How many people speak this language?** Is the speaker population large, small, concentrated, or dispersed?

2. **What is the estimated representation of this language in AI training datasets?** (Look for research on language representation in large language models — the BigScience group and other researchers have published analyses.)

3. **What AI tools, if any, have been specifically developed for this language community?** Are there local projects, government initiatives, or academic efforts?

4. **What are the practical consequences of poor AI support for this community?** Think about translation, education, healthcare, government services, and economic participation.

5. **Are there activists, researchers, or organisations working on AI equity for this community?** What is their approach?

After your research, write a 250-word "Profile of AI Exclusion" — a document that could be used to raise awareness about the situation facing this community and why it matters.

## Design It!

Design a **"Cultural AI Audit"** template — a one-page tool that an organisation could use to check whether an AI system they are considering using is likely to work equitably for their community.

Your template should include sections for:
- What languages does the AI support? At what quality level?
- Was the AI's training data diverse in terms of culture and geography?
- Has the AI been tested on the demographic groups that will use it?
- What happens when the AI is wrong — who bears the cost?
- Who was involved in designing and testing the AI?

For each section, write 2–3 specific questions that the organisation should ask before deploying the tool.

Label it clearly: **"AI Equity Checklist — Before You Deploy"**

## Debate This

1. **Should global AI companies be required to provide equivalent-quality AI services in all major world languages before they can operate in a country?** This would be a powerful equity requirement — but would also be extraordinarily costly and might slow AI deployment in the regions that most need it. Is this a reasonable trade-off to require?

2. **Is the development of local AI models by individual communities the right solution — or does it risk fragmenting a technology that works best at global scale?** Universal translation, for example, requires a model that understands all languages. Local models might serve communities better in some contexts but cut them off from the benefits of global scale in others. How do you weigh these?

3. **Is English's dominance in AI training data just a neutral consequence of digital history — or is it a new form of cultural imperialism that deserves a political response?** When the dominant AI systems embody Western values and English cultural assumptions, and when these systems are exported globally, who is responsible for addressing the resulting inequity?

## Reflect

1. If you were designing a new AI training dataset from scratch to be as globally representative as possible, what would you prioritise? Who would you need to work with?

2. Is a world where AI mainly works well in English, Mandarin, and Spanish simply a reflection of existing global inequality — or is it a new layer of inequality being created and locked in?

3. You might be a member of a cultural or linguistic minority yourself, or you might come from a well-represented group. How does your own position shape your view of who is responsible for solving this problem?

4. The case of medical AI performing worse on darker skin tones is not hypothetical — it is documented and ongoing. What does it say about a healthcare system that deploys such a tool knowing this limitation? Who is responsible for that harm?

5. If AI systems remain primarily designed for English-speaking, Western users, and the rest of the world adapts to those systems, what is lost? Is there something irreplaceable about having AI tools that are truly embedded in a specific culture and language?
