# Lesson Plan: AI Across Cultures
### Thinker Level (Ages 14–15) | Module 10

---

## Objective
By the end of this lesson, you can explain why AI performs unevenly across languages and cultures, identify which communities are most likely to be underserved by current AI systems, and argue for what a more equitable AI future could look like.

---

- A device with internet access
- A free AI tool (ChatGPT free or Claude free) — ideally one that allows language switching
- Paper and coloured pens OR a digital document
- If possible: a family member or friend who speaks a language other than English to help test (optional but valuable)
- About 45 minutes

Watch **Module 10: AI Across Cultures** with a parent or on your own.

Remember: The most widely used AI systems were trained predominantly on English-language text, mostly from Western internet sources. That is not a neutral starting point — it is a set of choices that embedded cultural biases into the technology, and those choices have real consequences for billions of people.

## Think Deeper — Who Is AI For?

### Activity 1: The Language Experiment (15 mins)

The internet is not evenly distributed across languages. Roughly 55% of all web content is in English, yet only about 17% of the world speaks English as a first language. AI models trained on internet data therefore know far more about English-speaking cultures than any other.

Let us test this directly.

**Part A — The Same Question, Multiple Languages:**

Ask your AI tool the same question in English, then in two or three other languages (you can use Google Translate to translate your question if needed, or simply type "[language] version of this question" and ask the AI to respond).

Try this question: "What are some famous traditional foods from your region, and what do they mean to people culturally?"

Or this one: "Who are the most important writers or poets from your cultural tradition?"

Test in English, and then in two of these languages if possible:
- Spanish
- Hindi or Bengali
- Swahili or Yoruba
- Arabic
- Japanese or Mandarin
- Welsh or Irish Gaelic
- An indigenous language if you have access

For each response:
- How long and detailed was the answer?
- Did it feel like the AI had rich knowledge, surface knowledge, or almost no knowledge?
- Did the AI answer from within the culture, or did it describe the culture from an outside perspective?

Write a brief comparison. Which language got the richest, most culturally embedded response?

**Part B — Ask directly:**

Ask your AI: "How much training data do you think exists in [language X] compared to English? And what are the practical consequences of that imbalance for speakers of that language?"

Note what it says. Then ask: "What are some AI applications that would be especially useful for rural communities in [low-income country], and what barriers exist to deploying them there?"

### Activity 2: Who Gets Left Out — Case Studies (15 mins)

Let us examine four documented cases where AI systems have performed differently or caused harm based on cultural or linguistic factors.

**Case 1 — Facial Recognition Bias:**
Research by MIT Media Lab (Joy Buolamwini and Timnit Gebru, 2018) found that commercial facial recognition systems had significantly higher error rates for darker-skinned faces — particularly darker-skinned women — compared to lighter-skinned faces. The systems were trained on datasets that overrepresented white male faces.

*Why it matters:* Facial recognition is used in hiring software, border control, policing, and banking identity verification. Higher error rates for some groups means those groups experience more wrongful rejections, more false flags, more friction.

**Case 2 — Medical AI and Skin Tone:**
A study published in The Lancet found that AI systems trained to detect skin conditions from photographs performed significantly worse on darker skin tones, because most medical training images featured lighter skin. Dermatology AI trained on skewed data could misdiagnose conditions in patients whose skin was not well-represented.

**Case 3 — Translation Quality Gaps:**
Major translation AI tools perform dramatically better for high-resource languages (English, Spanish, French, German) than for low-resource languages (many African, indigenous, and smaller Asian languages). For people in these communities, AI-powered translation offers limited help — and sometimes produces dangerous mistranslations in medical or legal contexts.

**Case 4 — Cultural Value Differences:**
AI safety filters that decide what content is "appropriate" are calibrated by human reviewers, most of whom work in English-speaking countries. This means content that is normal or important in one cultural context (religious practices, forms of dress, cultural rituals) may be flagged as problematic because the reviewers lacked cultural context.

**Your task:** For each case study:
1. Write one sentence identifying the root cause (training data? Reviewer demographics? Economic incentives?)
2. Write one practical recommendation for how the problem could be reduced
3. Note who bears the cost of each problem — is it the AI company, or the affected community?

### Activity 3: Imagining a More Equitable AI (15 mins)

Understanding the problem is step one. Step two is thinking about solutions.

**Three broad approaches to the equity problem:**

**Approach A — Better Representation:**
Actively include more diverse training data. Partner with communities in the Global South, indigenous groups, and speakers of low-resource languages to create training datasets that represent them. This requires investment, consent, and genuine partnerships — not just scraping more data.

**Approach B — Local Models:**
Rather than one global AI system trained mostly on Western data, support the development of smaller, locally-trained models that serve specific languages and cultures well. Ethiopia could have a model trained on Amharic. Indigenous communities could have models trained on their own oral histories and languages.

**Approach C — Community Governance:**
Give communities a voice in how AI systems that serve them are designed, trained, and governed. This means AI is not just built for communities but with them and, eventually, by them.

**Your task:**

Part A — Write one paragraph for each approach: what it would achieve, what its main practical challenge is, and who would need to make it happen.

Part B — Imagine you are an advisor to a government in a country where 80% of the population does not speak English and most current AI tools barely work in the local language. Write a 200-word policy recommendation for how this government should approach AI development in its country. Use one or more of the three approaches above.

## Design It!

Design a **"Cultural AI Audit"** template — a one-page tool that an organisation could use to check whether an AI system they are considering using is likely to work equitably for their community.

Your template should include sections for:
- What languages does the AI support? At what quality level?
- Was the AI's training data diverse in terms of culture and geography?
- Has the AI been tested on the demographic groups that will use it?
- What happens when the AI is wrong — who bears the cost?
- Who was involved in designing and testing the AI?

For each section, write 2–3 specific questions that the organisation should ask before deploying the tool.

Label it clearly: **"AI Equity Checklist — Before You Deploy"**

---

## Reflect
Answer these questions out loud or write them down:

1. If you were designing a new AI training dataset from scratch to be as globally representative as possible, what would you prioritise? Who would you need to work with?
2. Is a world where AI mainly works well in English, Mandarin, and Spanish (the dominant web languages) simply a reflection of existing global inequality — or is it a new layer of inequality being created and locked in?
3. You might be a member of a cultural or linguistic minority yourself, or you might come from a well-represented group. How does your own position shape your view of who is responsible for solving this problem?

## Share (Optional)
If you have family members or friends who speak languages other than English, ask them to try your AI tool in their language and give you honest feedback on the quality. Ask specifically: "Does it feel like it understands your cultural context, or does it feel like it is describing your culture from the outside?" This lived feedback is something no study can fully capture.
