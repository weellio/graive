## Background

Copyright law exists to solve a specific economic problem: creative work is expensive to produce and cheap to copy. Without some legal protection, nothing stops a competitor from copying your novel, your song, or your painting and selling it for less than you can. If creators cannot recoup their investment from their work, the incentive to create diminishes. Copyright, at least in theory, solves this by giving creators a time-limited monopoly over their work — the ability to decide who can copy, distribute, and profit from it.

The history of copyright is also the history of new technologies disrupting these protections and forcing the law to adapt. The printing press made text easy to reproduce and required entirely new legal frameworks. Photography in the nineteenth century raised the question of whether a mechanical process could produce art at all — and if so, who owned it. The introduction of music sampling in hip-hop in the 1980s led to a decade of legal battles over what counted as infringement and what counted as transformative use. The internet created entirely new challenges around digital reproduction and distribution that legislatures and courts are still grappling with.

Generative AI represents perhaps the most radical disruption to intellectual property frameworks since the printing press. It creates a three-stage problem. First, training these models required consuming enormous quantities of human-created work — billions of images, texts, songs, and artworks, most of them created by humans who did not consent and have not been compensated. Second, the models can now generate outputs that compete directly with human creators in the marketplace — producing images, texts, music, and code at near-zero cost. Third, the models can replicate the distinctive styles of specific living artists with uncanny accuracy, potentially destroying the market for the original artist's work while using that artist's life work as raw material.

The legal status of all three stages is genuinely unsettled. Courts in the US, UK, EU, and elsewhere are hearing cases that will determine whether training on copyrighted material constitutes infringement, whether AI-generated outputs can be copyrighted, and what liability AI companies bear for outputs that closely resemble specific human works. Different jurisdictions are reaching different conclusions. The EU's AI Act includes some provisions relating to generative AI and copyright disclosure. The US Copyright Office has issued guidance suggesting AI-generated works cannot be copyrighted without meaningful human creative input. But comprehensive, settled law does not yet exist, and the cases working through courts will shape the landscape for decades.

At a human level, the stakes are real. Illustrators report losing commercial commissions to clients who now use AI image generators trained on illustrators' own work. Voice actors have had their voices synthesised without consent. Musicians are hearing their styles replicated. Stock photography companies have seen their business models disrupted by AI-generated images. These are not abstract harms — they are concrete economic threats to the livelihoods of working artists. At the same time, AI-generated creative tools have genuinely democratised some forms of creativity, allowing people with ideas but limited technical skill to produce visual and textual content they could not have created otherwise.

## The Big Picture

The copyright debate is ultimately a debate about value: whose creative labour is valued, how it is compensated, and who controls the conditions of creative work. These questions intersect with economics (how do creators sustain themselves?), law (what rules should govern AI-generated content?), ethics (is training on someone's work without consent acceptable?), and culture (what does it mean to make something "original"?). The field of intellectual property law is expected to grow significantly as these disputes multiply. Journalism, policy, advocacy, and academic research in this area are all live and active.

For you personally, even if you are not a professional creator, these debates are relevant because you create things. You take photos, write posts, make videos, code projects. Understanding your own rights and the rights of others is practical knowledge.

## Key Terms

> **Copyright:** A legal right that gives creators exclusive control over the reproduction, distribution, and adaptation of their original work for a set period (typically the creator's life plus 70 years in most countries).
>
> **Fair use / fair dealing:** Legal exceptions to copyright that allow limited use of copyrighted material without permission — for purposes like criticism, education, commentary, or parody. The boundaries are contextual and often litigated.
>
> **Training data:** The content that AI models learn from. Whether using copyrighted material as training data constitutes infringement is currently contested in courts.
>
> **Public domain:** Works not protected by copyright — either because the protection has expired, the creator waived it, or it was never eligible. Anyone can use public domain works freely.
>
> **Derivative work:** A creative work based on or incorporating elements of an existing copyrighted work. Creating a derivative work generally requires the original creator's permission.
>
> **Style vs. expression:** Copyright protects specific creative expression (the particular words of a poem, the specific lines of a drawing) but not style, ideas, or techniques. You can legally paint "in the style of" any artist.
>
> **Moral rights:** In many countries (though not the US), creators have rights to be credited for their work and to object to distortions of it, separate from the economic copyright.

## Think Deeper — Ownership, Training, and the Law

### Activity 1: Understanding the Legal Landscape (15 mins)

**The Three Flashpoints:**

**Flashpoint 1 — Training Data:**
To train an AI image generator or text model, companies collected billions of images, texts, books, and artworks from the internet. Much of this was protected by copyright. The companies argue this is "fair use" — using data to learn, not to reproduce. Artists and authors argue they never consented and should be compensated.

*Real case:* In 2023, a group of visual artists sued Stability AI (maker of Stable Diffusion), Midjourney, and DeviantArt, claiming their artwork was used in training without permission.

*Real case:* The New York Times sued OpenAI and Microsoft in 2023, claiming millions of its articles were used to train GPT models without permission.

**Flashpoint 2 — AI-Generated Output:**
If AI creates an image, who owns it? Current US Copyright Office rulings say that works created entirely by AI without human creative input cannot be copyrighted — they fall into the public domain. But what about when a human writes a detailed prompt? How much human input is "enough" for copyright protection?

*Real case:* In 2023, the US Copyright Office partially rejected copyright registration for an AI-generated comic book called "Zarya of the Dawn," saying the AI-generated images themselves could not be copyrighted, but the human-written text and arrangement could be.

**Flashpoint 3 — Style Imitation:**
AI can generate images "in the style of" any living artist. Copyright does not protect style — only specific works. So an AI generating images "in the style of [artist name]" is technically legal in most jurisdictions, even if the original artist finds it deeply unfair.

*Question:* Should "style" be legally protected? And if so, how would you even define and enforce it?

**Your task:** For each of the three flashpoints, write:
1. The strongest argument that current copyright law is being violated
2. The strongest counter-argument (why it might be legal or acceptable)
3. What you think a fair rule should be

### Activity 2: Analyse Real Positions (15 mins)

Four different groups have very different stakes in the copyright debate. Read each perspective and then complete the analysis task.

**The AI Company:**
"Training on publicly available data is no different from a human artist learning by looking at other artists' work. When you study a painting, you are not paying the painter for your education. Our models create new things — they do not reproduce originals."

**The Original Artist:**
"A human artist cannot look at my painting and instantly generate a thousand variations of my exact style to sell commercially. AI can. My livelihood is being competed with by a system trained on my own work, without my consent, without payment, and often without attribution."

**The Lawyer/Academic:**
"Existing copyright law was not designed for this scenario. We need updated legislation that balances incentivising innovation with protecting the rights of human creators. The market alone will not produce a fair outcome."

**The General Public / Consumer:**
"I can now access beautiful, personalised illustrations for my projects that I could never have afforded to commission. This democratises creativity. Not everything needs to be locked behind professional fees."

**Your task:**

Part A — Rank these four perspectives from "most persuasive to you personally" to "least persuasive." Explain your ranking in 3–4 sentences.

Part B — Now steelman the perspective you ranked last — write the strongest possible version of that argument, even if you disagree with it.

Part C — After doing Part B, did your ranking change at all? Why or why not?

### Activity 3: Apply It to Your Own Work (15 mins)

**Scenario A:** You use AI to generate an illustration for your school project presentation. Your teacher asks if you made it. What do you say?

**Scenario B:** You write a story with significant AI assistance — AI wrote the opening and closing sections, you wrote the middle. You enter it in a school writing competition. Is this acceptable? Does it depend on the competition rules?

**Scenario C:** You are a musician. You use an AI music tool to generate a backing track, then record your own vocals and lyrics over it. You post it on a music sharing platform and it gets 10,000 plays. Do you owe anything to the AI company? To the musicians whose recordings trained the model? Or is it fully yours?

**Scenario D:** A classmate asks you to generate AI artwork for their assignment "because you are good at prompting." They take credit for the output. Is this different from one student helping another write an essay? What are the important distinctions?

For each scenario, write:
- What you would actually do
- What you think the ethical thing to do is (these may be different)
- One principle that guides your thinking

After working through all four, write a 3-sentence **personal policy statement** about how you will handle AI-assisted work going forward.

## Deep Dive — Following the Cases

The law in this area is being written in real time. Following actual legal cases is one of the best ways to understand what is at stake.

**Research task:** Choose ONE of these active or recent legal cases and research it in depth:

1. **New York Times v. OpenAI and Microsoft** (2023-ongoing) — The Times alleges that its articles were used to train language models that now compete with its journalism.

2. **Getty Images v. Stability AI** (2023-ongoing) — Getty alleges that its photographs, including watermarks, appeared in training data and outputs.

3. **Authors Guild v. OpenAI** (2023) — A group of prominent authors, including John Grisham and George R.R. Martin, sued claiming their books were used without permission.

4. **Andersen v. Stability AI, Midjourney, DeviantArt** (2023) — Artists alleging their visual work was used without consent in training image generation models.

Use AI to get an overview, then verify with real news sources:
```
Explain the [CASE NAME] lawsuit in detail.
What exactly is being alleged?
What is the defendant's main argument?
What outcome are the plaintiffs seeking?
What is the current status of the case?
What would each possible outcome mean for AI companies and for artists?
```

After your research, write a 200-word analysis: What is the most important principle at stake in this case, and why should someone who is not a lawyer or an artist care about the outcome?

## Debate This

1. **Should AI companies be required to pay a licensing fee into a collective fund that compensates all creators whose work was used in training?** This model already exists in some form for music radio play (collecting societies distribute royalties). Could it work for AI training? Who would administer it? How would you calculate how much each creator contributed?

2. **Is the comparison between AI learning and human learning valid?** AI companies argue that training on copyrighted data is like a human learning from reading copyrighted books — you are not paying the author for the education. Critics say this comparison is false: a human who reads a novel cannot then produce infinite novels in the author's style at industrial scale. Who has the better argument?

3. **Should "human-made" become a legally meaningful label for creative works?** Some artists are calling for a certification or labelling system — like "organic" food or "handmade" crafts — that identifies works made without AI. Should this be legally standardised? Would consumers pay more for certified human-made art, music, or writing?

1. Copyright protection is designed to give creators a financial incentive to make things. If AI can make things without financial incentive, does copyright law still serve its purpose?

2. Some artists have started labelling their work "human-made" as a selling point. Do you think this will become a meaningful distinction that consumers care about?

3. What do you think the copyright situation will look like in 10 years? Will artists have stronger protections, weaker protections, or will the whole system look completely different?

4. The AI company's argument is that training on data is like learning from experience, not like copying. Do you find this analogy convincing? Where does it hold up and where does it break down?

5. Think about something you have created — a piece of writing, an image, a piece of music, code, a project. How would you feel if it was used without your knowledge to train an AI that then competed with you commercially?
