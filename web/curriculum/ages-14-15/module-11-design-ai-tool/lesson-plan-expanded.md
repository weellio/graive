## Background

Humans have been designing tools for as long as there have been humans — from stone hand-axes shaped to fit the grip, to the printing press, to the smartphone. What distinguishes good tool design from bad is not technical sophistication alone. A technically sophisticated tool that solves the wrong problem, is too complicated for its users, or creates worse problems than it solves is a failure by any meaningful measure. The history of technology is littered with technically impressive products that failed because the designers understood their own cleverness better than they understood the people they were designing for.

The discipline of design thinking emerged in the late twentieth century partly as a corrective to this tendency. Design thinking is not about aesthetics — it is about process. It starts with deep observation of real users in their real contexts: what are they actually trying to do? What prevents them from doing it? What workarounds have they already developed? What would make their lives genuinely easier? Only after thoroughly understanding the problem — a stage some design frameworks call "empathise" and "define" — does a designer move to generating solutions, prototyping, and testing them.

Human-centred design has produced some of the most successful products and services of the modern era. The original iPod succeeded not because Apple invented portable music players (others had done that) but because Apple understood what made existing players frustrating — too many steps between wanting to hear a song and hearing it — and designed around that insight. The success of simple, accessible AI assistants like Siri and Alexa came from understanding that people did not want to learn to operate a complex system; they wanted to speak naturally and have things happen.

AI adds a new dimension to design thinking. The question is not just "what do users need?" but "what do users need that AI specifically makes possible in a way that was not possible before?" AI is excellent at certain things: personalising responses based on individual input, processing natural language, finding patterns across large amounts of data, generating options for human consideration, and being available at scale. AI is poor at other things: understanding context it was not given, exercising genuine judgment in novel situations, taking responsibility for its outputs, and recognising the emotional stakes of a situation. Good AI tool design uses AI for what it is actually good at while building in human oversight for what it is not.

There is also the matter of unintended consequences. Every technology creates effects beyond its intended purpose. Social media was designed to connect friends; it also amplified misinformation and contributed to mental health crises. GPS navigation was designed to help drivers find their way; it also made many drivers unable to navigate without it. AI tools designed to help students learn could make learning more accessible or could short-circuit the effortful processing that builds understanding. Responsible design requires thinking seriously about second and third-order effects — not just "will this work?" but "what will this change that we did not intend to change?"

## The Big Picture

Designing AI tools is one of the fastest-growing and most generously compensated skill areas in the technology sector. UX designers, product managers, AI ethicists, and human-computer interaction researchers are all doing work that intersects with what you will practise in this module. But beyond career considerations, the ability to think like a designer — to move from problem to insight to solution in a structured way — is valuable in almost any context where you are trying to make something better. Whether you are improving a school system, designing a community programme, or building a business, the design thinking process is applicable.

## Key Terms

> **Design thinking:** A human-centred problem-solving process that emphasises understanding users deeply before generating solutions, and testing solutions early and often.
>
> **Problem statement:** A clear, specific articulation of a problem, who experiences it, what causes it, and what the consequence is. The foundation of good design work.
>
> **User persona:** A fictional but research-based representation of a typical user — their context, motivations, frustrations, and goals.
>
> **Minimum viable product (MVP):** The simplest possible version of a product that delivers real value and can be tested with real users. Encourages learning quickly rather than building perfectly.
>
> **Affordance:** A quality of a design that signals how it should be used. A button that looks clickable has good affordance; a door with no handle has poor affordance.
>
> **Unintended consequence:** An effect of a technology or policy that was not anticipated or intended by its designers.
>
> **Stress-testing:** Deliberately trying to break or challenge a design by imagining extreme users, edge cases, or adversarial uses.

## Think Deeper — From Problem to Plan

### Activity 1: Find a Real Problem Worth Solving (15 mins)

**The Problem-Finding Process:**

Think about your school and community. Consider these prompts:
- What do students in your school regularly struggle with or complain about?
- What process in your school is inefficient, confusing, or time-consuming?
- What information do students or parents often need but cannot easily find?
- What decision do students make that would benefit from better information or support?
- What support service is unavailable or under-resourced that AI could help with?

**Your task — Problem Discovery Interview:**

Identify a problem you want to design for. Then speak to (or message) at least **two people** who experience this problem. Ask them:

1. "Can you describe the problem in your own words?"
2. "When does this problem happen? How often?"
3. "What do you currently do to work around it?"
4. "What would a perfect solution look like to you?"
5. "What is most frustrating or annoying about the current situation?"

Write up your interview notes. Your goal is to understand the problem from the user's perspective, not just your own.

---

### Activity 2: Design the Solution (20 mins)

**Component 1 — The Problem Statement:**
Write a single, specific problem statement in this format:
> "[User type] struggles with [specific problem] because [root cause], which means [consequence for them]."

**Component 2 — The User:**
Who specifically is your primary user? Describe them in detail:
- Age and context
- What they know and do not know about the problem
- What devices/technology they have access to
- What would make them actually use your tool (and what would put them off)

**Component 3 — The Core Features:**
What does your tool do? List 3–5 core features. For each one:
- Name the feature
- Explain what it does in one sentence
- Explain how AI specifically makes this feature possible

**Component 4 — The Data Question:**
What data would your AI tool need to work? Where would that data come from? Who controls it? Are there privacy concerns?

**Component 5 — The Risk Assessment:**
Identify at least three risks your tool carries and write a mitigation for each. Risks might include:
- Privacy or data security
- Students misusing or gaming the tool
- AI giving wrong or harmful advice
- The tool working better for some students than others
- Making teachers or counsellors feel replaced rather than supported

---

### Activity 3: Use AI to Stress-Test Your Design (15 mins)

Open your AI tool and describe your tool idea. Then ask:

1. "What are the three most likely ways this tool could fail or cause harm?"
2. "What similar tools already exist? What can I learn from them?"
3. "What would a student who does NOT want to use this tool say about it? What objections would they have?"
4. "What would a parent be worried about if their child used this tool?"
5. "What would need to be true for this tool to actually change behaviour and not just be used once and forgotten?"

For each response, note whether the AI spotted something you had not thought of, and how it changes your design.

After this process, write one paragraph describing how your design changed or improved as a result of the AI stress-test.

## Deep Dive — Learning from Failure

Good design requires understanding why things fail — not just why they succeed. Many AI tools have launched with strong intentions and produced unexpected or harmful outcomes.

**Research task:** Choose ONE real AI tool or AI-assisted system that was deployed and then had to be withdrawn, significantly modified, or became controversial because of unintended consequences.

Some examples to research:
- Microsoft's Tay chatbot (2016) — a Twitter chatbot that began generating offensive content within hours of launch
- Amazon's AI hiring tool (2018) — developed to automate CV screening, found to systematically downgrade women's applications
- Apple's CSAM detection system (2021) — designed to detect child abuse material on iPhones, withdrawn after privacy concerns
- Meta's Galactica (2022) — an AI for scientific research, withdrawn after generating confident-sounding but false information
- Various predictive policing AI systems — deployed in cities and later withdrawn or banned after evidence of racial bias

For your chosen case, research:

1. What was the tool designed to do? What was the intended benefit?
2. What went wrong? Was it a technical failure, a design failure, or a deployment failure?
3. Did the designers anticipate this risk? Was it in their risk assessment?
4. How did they respond when the problem emerged?
5. What should future designers learn from this case?

Write a 200-word "Design Failure Report" — structured like a post-mortem that a real design team might write.

Then: compare the failure you identified to your own design. Are there any parallels? Does this case study reveal any risks in your tool that you had not considered?

## Design It!

Create a **One-Page Pitch** for your AI tool — as if you were presenting it to your school's leadership team or a small grants committee.

Your pitch page should include:
- **The Name** of the tool
- **The Problem** (2 sentences maximum — what problem, for whom)
- **The Solution** (3 bullet points — what your tool does)
- **Why AI?** (1 sentence — what AI makes possible that was not possible before)
- **Who uses it and how** (a brief scenario: "A student opens the app when...")
- **Key safeguards** (2 bullet points about how you handle data, errors, and risks)
- **What success looks like** (how would you know the tool was working?)
- A simple sketch of the tool's main screen or interface

Make it visual and convincing. Imagine you have two minutes to present this. Would someone lean forward and think "we should build this"?

## Debate This

1. **Should AI tools designed for use by people under 18 require a different approval process than tools for adults?** Young people are still developing and may be more susceptible to certain kinds of harm from AI tools. Should there be a regulatory body that specifically reviews AI products intended for schools or young people before they can be deployed?

2. **Is it ethical to deploy an AI tool in a school without informed consent from students and parents — explaining in plain language what data the tool collects and how it is used?** Current practice varies enormously. Many school-adopted software tools collect detailed behavioural data. How much transparency is required before a tool can be used with minors?

3. **Should schools be required to trial AI tools with a small group before school-wide deployment?** Evidence from the introduction of technology in schools suggests that tools adopted without adequate piloting often fail to deliver their promised benefits and sometimes cause harm. Should evidence of effectiveness be required before schools adopt AI tools?

## Reflect

1. The hardest part of good design is not coming up with ideas — it is understanding the problem deeply enough that your solution actually fits. How did the interview process in Activity 1 change or sharpen your understanding of the problem?

2. You identified risks in Activity 2. Were there risks that were genuinely difficult to mitigate — where you could not find a satisfying answer? What does that tell you about the limits of your tool?

3. If your tool were built and deployed in your school tomorrow, what would be the single most important thing you would want to monitor to know if it was working — or causing harm?

4. The Design Failure research showed that well-intentioned tools can cause harm when deployed at scale. How does knowing this change how you think about your own design? At what stage would you want a human expert to review your tool before it reached real users?

5. Good design requires empathy — the ability to genuinely understand how other people experience a problem. Did talking to real users reveal anything you would not have anticipated by thinking about the problem on your own? What does this tell you about the limits of designing for others without their involvement?
