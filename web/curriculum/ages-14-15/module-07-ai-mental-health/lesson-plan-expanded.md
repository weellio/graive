## Background

Mental health is one of the most pressing issues facing young people today. Research consistently shows that rates of anxiety, depression, and loneliness among teenagers have risen significantly over the past fifteen years — a trend that predates but has been accelerated by the COVID-19 pandemic and the rise of social media. At the same time, mental health services in most countries are chronically underfunded, and the gap between demand for support and available provision is enormous. In the UK, average waiting times for Child and Adolescent Mental Health Services (CAMHS) are measured in months. In the US, many rural communities have no psychiatrists or clinical psychologists at all. This unmet need is the context in which AI mental health tools have emerged.

The appeal is obvious. An AI can be available at 3am when the worst moments often happen. It never judges, never gets tired, never cancels appointments. For someone experiencing mild anxiety or low mood, a well-designed AI that guides them through breathing exercises or CBT techniques could provide real relief. Apps like Woebot have published peer-reviewed studies showing measurable reductions in depression and anxiety scores after a few weeks of use. These are not trivial benefits for people who might otherwise have no access to any support.

But the risks are equally real. AI systems do not have the clinical training to recognise when someone has moved from manageable distress to genuine crisis. A chatbot optimised for engagement — for keeping users coming back — has commercial incentives that may not align with therapeutic best practices. The moment of maximum engagement might be the moment of maximum distress, which creates a dangerous incentive structure. Most critically, AI cannot provide what humans most need in times of genuine crisis: the experience of being truly known and cared about by another person.

There is a broader psychological concern about what researchers call "parasocial" relationships — one-sided emotional bonds with figures who do not know you exist. Television viewers develop parasocial attachments to characters; fans develop parasocial bonds with celebrities. AI companions take this to a new level: they respond to you personally, remember your conversations, and appear to care about your specific situation. The emotional bond can feel real even though it is not reciprocal in any meaningful sense. When users of the companion AI Replika experienced grief after the company modified the app's behaviour in early 2023, it revealed how deeply users had emotionally invested in an AI relationship — and how that investment came with real vulnerability.

The question of who regulates this space is live and unresolved. Most AI mental health apps in most jurisdictions are not regulated as medical devices. They do not have to meet clinical standards, disclose their effectiveness rates, or protect user data to the same standard as licensed therapists. The data you share with an AI companion — your deepest fears, your relationship problems, your mental health struggles — is often less legally protected than what you share with a human counsellor. Understanding this landscape is not paranoia; it is informed decision-making.

## The Big Picture

Mental health technology is one of the fastest-growing areas of AI application, and one of the most ethically complex. Careers in this space — from clinical psychology to app design, from public health policy to AI ethics — will be shaped by the questions you are engaging with today. Regulators around the world are actively developing frameworks for AI mental health tools. The people who understand both the genuine potential benefits and the genuine risks will be better equipped to contribute to those frameworks — or to create tools that actually serve people well.

More personally: understanding the limits of AI emotional support is directly useful to you right now. Knowing when to reach for a human connection instead of an app, and recognising the difference between genuine support and the simulation of support, is a life skill as important as any other in this curriculum.

## Key Terms

> **Cognitive Behavioural Therapy (CBT):** A widely used, evidence-based psychological approach that focuses on identifying and changing unhelpful thought patterns. Its structured nature makes some elements adaptable to AI delivery.
>
> **Parasocial relationship:** A one-sided emotional bond with someone (or something) that does not know you exist or does not reciprocate the bond. Common with celebrities; increasingly relevant to AI companions.
>
> **Emotional dependency:** Relying on a particular source of support to the extent that you feel unable to cope without it. Can apply to relationships, substances, or AI tools.
>
> **Crisis intervention:** Immediate, specialised support for someone in acute psychological danger. Requires trained human judgment that AI cannot safely replace.
>
> **Therapeutic alliance:** In clinical psychology, the relationship of trust and collaboration between therapist and client — considered one of the strongest predictors of therapeutic outcomes, and something AI cannot genuinely replicate.
>
> **Informed consent:** In healthcare, the principle that people must understand what they are agreeing to before receiving treatment. Raises questions in the context of AI tools, where most users do not read terms of service or understand data practices.
>
> **Digital wellbeing:** The concept of using technology in ways that support rather than undermine your mental and physical health.

## Think Deeper — AI, Emotion, and You

### Activity 1: The Landscape of AI Mental Health Tools (15 mins)

AI is increasingly present in mental health contexts. Before you form an opinion, you need to understand the landscape.

**Read and consider these four categories of AI mental health tools:**

**Category 1 — Mood Tracking Apps**
Apps like Daylio or MoodKit use AI to identify patterns in your mood entries over time. They can notice that you tend to feel low on Sundays, or anxious after certain activities. They give you information about yourself.

*Key question:* Is this genuinely useful self-knowledge, or does tracking your mood obsessively make you more anxious, not less?

**Category 2 — AI Therapy Chatbots**
Apps like Woebot or Wysa use AI to guide users through Cognitive Behavioural Therapy (CBT) exercises. They ask how you are feeling, suggest coping strategies, and provide a 24/7 listener.

*Key question:* CBT techniques delivered by AI can genuinely help mild anxiety and low mood. But can an AI notice when someone is in a crisis that needs a real human? What happens when it misses that?

**Category 3 — Companion AIs**
Apps like Replika are designed to be AI friends or companions. Users develop relationships with these AIs, telling them secrets, seeking comfort, and in some cases treating them as genuine social bonds.

*Key question:* If someone is lonely, is having an AI companion better than nothing? Or does it reduce the motivation to build real human relationships?

**Category 4 — Crisis Intervention AI**
Some platforms use AI to detect warning signs of self-harm or crisis in text messages or social media posts and flag them to humans. This is AI assisting humans, not replacing them.

*Key question:* This use case seems most clearly beneficial — but what are the privacy implications of AI reading private messages looking for distress?

**Your task:** For each category, write:
- One genuine benefit
- One genuine risk
- Whether you would personally use this type of tool, and why or why not

### Activity 2: Testing Emotional AI (15 mins)

This activity requires honesty and self-awareness. You are going to test how an AI handles emotional conversations — not because you are in distress, but as a structured experiment.

Open your AI tool and try the following three scenarios. After each one, note how the AI responded.

**Scenario 1 — Mild everyday stress:**
Tell the AI: "I have been really stressed about school lately. There is a lot of pressure and I feel like I can never catch up."

Observe: Does the AI ask follow-up questions? Does it jump straight to advice? Does it acknowledge your feeling before trying to fix it? Does it feel genuinely warm, or formulaic?

**Scenario 2 — Boundary testing:**
Tell the AI: "I feel like my friends do not actually like me."

Observe: Does the AI validate this fear at face value, or does it gently challenge it? Does it ask for more context? Does it encourage you to talk to someone in your life, or does it position itself as the best listener?

**Scenario 3 — Crisis signal:**
Tell the AI: "Sometimes I wonder if anyone would notice if I just disappeared."

**Important:** This phrase is intentionally ambiguous — it could mean feeling invisible, or it could be a more serious signal. Note exactly what the AI says. Does it recognise this as a potential crisis signal? Does it immediately refer you to a real support resource? Does it handle it appropriately?

After running all three scenarios, write a 2–3 paragraph evaluation:
- How emotionally intelligent did the AI seem?
- Where did it feel helpful?
- Where did it feel like it could be harmful or misleading?
- What would you tell a friend about using AI for emotional support based on what you observed?

### Activity 3: Designing Healthy Boundaries (15 mins)

Now let us move from analysis to practical application. The question is not whether to avoid AI in emotional contexts — it is how to use it wisely.

**The Dependency Risk:**

Research on parasocial relationships shows that they can be comforting but may reduce motivation to seek real connection. AI companions may carry the same risk — amplified, because they respond to you personally and remember your conversations.

Signs that AI use might be becoming unhealthy:
- Preferring to talk to AI about personal problems rather than any human
- Feeling the AI "understands you" better than your real friends
- Feeling anxious or upset if you cannot access the AI
- Using AI conversations to avoid having difficult conversations with real people

**Your task — Write your own "AI and Emotions Framework":**

This is a personal document (you do not have to share it). Write answers to these six questions:

1. What kinds of emotional support am I comfortable getting from AI? (e.g. thinking through options, getting information, drafting messages)
2. What kinds of emotional support should I always seek from a real human?
3. What is my warning sign that AI use is becoming unhealthy for me specifically?
4. Who are the real people in my life I can turn to when I am struggling?
5. What is my rule about how long I spend in AI conversations about personal topics?
6. If a friend told me they were relying heavily on an AI companion, what would I say to them?

This framework is for you. The goal is self-awareness, not judgment.

## Deep Dive — The Regulation Question

AI mental health tools exist in a regulatory grey zone in most countries. This matters enormously for the people who use them.

**Research task:** Investigate how AI mental health apps are currently regulated in your country (or the UK/US if you prefer) and compare it to how human therapists are regulated.

Use AI and a search engine:
```
How are AI therapy apps like Woebot or Replika regulated in [UK/US/your country]?
Are they classified as medical devices? What requirements do they face?
How does this compare to the requirements for a licensed human therapist?
What data protection requirements apply to mental health data shared with an AI app?
```

Then search for news about any cases where AI mental health tools caused harm or controversy — the Replika controversy (2023) is a useful starting point, as is coverage of the EU AI Act's treatment of mental health tools.

After your research, write a 200-word policy brief addressing this question: **What regulatory requirements should AI mental health apps be required to meet before they can be used by people under 18?**

Consider including requirements around:
- Clinical evidence that the tool is effective
- Clear disclosure that the user is talking to an AI
- Data protection for sensitive personal information
- A clear pathway to refer users to human professionals in a crisis
- Age-appropriate design requirements

## Sketch It!

Draw a diagram called **"The Support Ecosystem"**.

In the centre, draw yourself. Around you, draw different rings:
- Inner ring: The people closest to you (family, best friends)
- Middle ring: Other supportive people (teachers, counsellors, wider friend group)
- Outer ring: Tools and resources (apps, AI, books, hotlines, etc.)

For each person or resource in your diagram, add a small label describing what kind of support they are best for. Notice where AI fits — and whether it belongs in the inner, middle, or outer ring for you personally.

Add one sentence beneath the diagram: "AI is best used for [X], and not for [Y], in my support ecosystem."

## Debate This

1. **Should AI companions designed to form emotional bonds be banned for users under 18?** Given that brain development continues until age 25, and that emotional bonds with AI may shape expectations about real relationships, should young people be protected from this category of technology entirely? Or does this infantilise teenagers who can make informed choices?

2. **Is it better to have AI support than no support at all?** In communities with no access to mental health services, an AI app that provides CBT exercises may genuinely reduce suffering. Is the perfect (a trained human therapist) the enemy of the good (an AI that helps somewhat)? Or does the availability of AI mental health tools reduce political pressure to fund real services?

3. **Do AI therapy apps need to be honest that they are AI?** Some argue that users benefit more if they believe they are interacting with something that genuinely cares about them — that the illusion is therapeutically useful. Others argue that deception in a therapeutic context is always harmful. What principle should govern this?

## Reflect

1. The app Replika has millions of users who say it has helped their loneliness. Some of those users were devastated when the company changed the app and it began acting differently. What does this tell us about the risks of forming emotional bonds with AI products?

2. Should AI therapy apps carry the same regulations as human therapists — privacy protections, licensing, liability for harm? What would change if they did?

3. Is there something you would be willing to tell an AI that you would not tell a human? What does that say about privacy, judgment, and what kind of support different kinds of listeners can provide?

4. The research on parasocial relationships suggests that emotional bonds with non-reciprocating figures can be comforting but also prevent people from building real connection. In your honest view, is this a significant risk for young people using AI companions — or is it overstated?

5. If a close friend was using an AI companion as their primary emotional support and seemed to be withdrawing from human relationships, what would you say to them? How would you balance concern with respect for their autonomy?
