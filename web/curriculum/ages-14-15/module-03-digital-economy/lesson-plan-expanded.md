## Background

When you scroll through Instagram, watch YouTube, or search on Google, you are participating in one of the most lucrative industries in human history — and you are not the customer. You are the product. This is not a rhetorical flourish or a conspiracy theory. It is the literal business model: free services, funded by advertising, where the value being sold is the ability to place targeted messages in front of specific groups of people based on detailed profiles built from their behaviour.

The digital attention economy took shape in the early 2000s, but its current dominance was not inevitable. Early internet business models varied widely — some platforms charged subscription fees, some sold software, some tried micropayments for content. The shift toward advertising-funded free services accelerated when companies discovered that behavioural data — not just demographic data — allowed them to target advertising with unprecedented precision. The more you could learn about what someone actually cared about, what they clicked on, what they read and for how long, what they bought, the better you could predict what they would click on next. That predictive ability is worth enormous amounts of money to advertisers.

AI is central to the modern digital economy in several distinct ways. Recommendation systems use machine learning to decide what content to show you next — maximising engagement, which means maximising the time you spend on the platform, which means maximising advertising revenue. This is why YouTube autoplay often leads you down progressively more extreme content, why TikTok can absorb hours you did not intend to spend, and why Facebook shows you content that provokes emotional reactions. The algorithm is not trying to enrich your life. It is optimising for a metric — engagement — that correlates with revenue, and it happens to do so by exploiting psychological triggers.

The major platforms have also become infrastructure in ways that give them structural power over the economies they sit within. Google controls roughly 90% of global search, giving it power over how information is found and therefore which businesses and ideas can be discovered. Amazon combines a marketplace, a logistics network, a cloud computing provider, and an advertising platform — and the data from each feeds the others. Meta owns the dominant social platforms in many countries and the dominant messaging apps. When platforms reach this level of market concentration, their design choices affect not just individual users but entire cultural and economic ecosystems.

The creator economy — the system by which individuals can build audiences and earn money through content creation — is partly a response to this concentration. Platforms like YouTube, Substack, and Patreon allow creators to build direct relationships with audiences and earn revenue without traditional gatekeepers like record labels or publishers. But this too is mediated by algorithms, platform fees, and terms of service that the creator does not control. The platform can change its revenue share, alter the algorithm, or demonetise content at any time. Building a business on a platform you do not own is a risk that every creator faces.

## The Big Picture

Understanding the digital economy is not just about protecting yourself as a consumer — it is about understanding the landscape you will work in. Whether you become a journalist, a designer, a teacher, a scientist, or an entrepreneur, the digital economy will shape how your work reaches people, how it is valued, and how you sustain yourself. Media companies, publishing houses, music labels, retail businesses, and professional services have all been transformed — often violently — by the shift to digital-first business models. Knowing how platforms work, who they serve, and where the money flows gives you a strategic literacy that most people lack.

The question of regulation is also live in ways it was not a decade ago. The EU's Digital Markets Act and Digital Services Act impose new requirements on large platforms around transparency, competition, and content moderation. Similar debates are happening in the UK, US, and beyond. The people who will shape these policies — politicians, regulators, lobbyists, journalists — need to understand the economics deeply. That could be you.

## Key Terms

> **Attention economy:** An economic model in which human attention is treated as a scarce resource to be captured and sold to advertisers.
>
> **Behavioural data:** Information about what you actually do — what you click, watch, search, buy — as opposed to what you say about yourself. More valuable to advertisers because it is harder to fake.
>
> **Recommendation algorithm:** An AI system that decides what content to show you next, typically optimised for engagement.
>
> **Network effect:** When a product becomes more valuable as more people use it. Facebook is valuable partly because everyone else is on it.
>
> **Platform monopoly:** When a single company controls access to a critical digital market or infrastructure.
>
> **Creator economy:** The system by which individuals build audiences and earn revenue directly through content creation, often via platforms.
>
> **Average revenue per user (ARPU):** A key metric for platform businesses — how much advertising or subscription revenue each user generates on average per year.

**As a consumer:**
- What do you get from the platform?

## Deep Dive — The Advertising Machine

Most people use platforms daily without understanding how the advertising system actually works. This activity takes you inside it.

**Research task:** Investigate how targeted advertising works in practice.

Use AI and search to find answers to these questions:

1. What is the difference between a "cookie," a "device fingerprint," and a "lookalike audience"? Why does it matter that advertisers can target you using data even when you have blocked cookies?

2. How much does it cost to show 1,000 people an ad on Facebook or Instagram? (Look up "Facebook CPM" — cost per mille, meaning cost per thousand impressions.) Now calculate: if Facebook has 3 billion monthly active users and shows each of them, say, 20 ads per day, what is the rough theoretical maximum revenue from advertising?

3. Find the actual reported annual advertising revenue for Facebook (Meta) for the most recent year available. How does it compare to your calculation? What explains the gap?

4. Data brokers are companies that collect data about people from multiple sources (public records, loyalty cards, shopping data, app tracking) and sell profiles to advertisers and other buyers. Research one data broker company — Acxiom, LexisNexis Risk Solutions, or Experian are examples. What kinds of data do they hold, and who can buy it?

**After your research, write 200 words:** If someone says "I don't mind ads because they're just annoying, not harmful," what would you say to them based on what you now know about how the system actually works?

## Debate This

1. **Should social media platforms be allowed to use algorithmic recommendation systems?** If recommendations optimise for engagement, and engagement correlates with outrage and anxiety, should platforms be legally required to show users chronological feeds by default? Who should decide — users, regulators, or the market?

2. **Is it ethical to offer free services in exchange for data?** Some economists argue this is a straightforward value exchange: you get a useful service, they get valuable data. Others argue most users do not meaningfully understand what they are consenting to, making it a form of exploitation. Who is right?

3. **Should platform monopolies be broken up?** Amazon, Google, Meta, and Apple collectively dominate the digital economy in ways that would trigger antitrust action in other industries. Some economists argue their dominance stifles competition and innovation. Others argue their scale is what makes them useful and that competition from each other is sufficient. What is the right policy?
