## Background

The challenge of verifying information is not new — it predates AI by centuries. Medieval scholars developed complex systems for establishing the authenticity of historical documents. Journalists have operated with the principle of "if in doubt, leave it out" for well over a hundred years. Scientists developed peer review specifically because researchers are fallible and biased, and independent verification is required before a claim earns the label of scientific knowledge. The skills of source evaluation, cross-referencing, and distinguishing primary from secondary sources are foundational to all serious intellectual work.

What AI has done is make the verification problem more acute in a particular way. Before generative AI, information that appeared on the internet was created by humans and, while wrong or misleading information was certainly common, there was at least a human author somewhere whose credibility, incentives, and track record you could assess. AI-generated content strips out that accountability layer. A language model produces text that is syntactically fluent and tonally authoritative regardless of whether the underlying claim is true. There is no difference in style between a confidently stated correct fact and a confidently stated fabrication.

The phenomenon researchers call "hallucination" is a consequence of how large language models work. These models generate text by predicting the most statistically probable next token given the context. They do not retrieve information from a database of verified facts. They predict what plausible text would say, based on patterns in their training data. Most of the time, what plausible text would say happens to be true, because most of the text on the internet is roughly accurate. But when the model encounters a gap — a question about a specific statistic, a precise date, an obscure citation — it fills the gap with a plausible-sounding completion rather than saying "I don't know." The result is fabricated citations, invented statistics, and false attributions that sound completely real.

This matters most in situations where stakes are high and errors are hard to detect. A student who uses AI to research a history essay may submit incorrect dates or misattributed quotes without knowing it. A journalist who uses AI to draft background on a story may include fictitious sources that embarrass the publication. A medical professional who uses AI to look up drug dosages or treatment protocols may act on incorrect information with serious consequences. The AI does not know it is wrong, does not signal uncertainty, and does not distinguish between high-stakes and low-stakes errors.

The solution is not to avoid AI — it is to treat AI outputs the way any good researcher treats any unverified source: as a lead to follow up, not as a conclusion to rely on. This is actually how good researchers have always approached secondary sources. A Wikipedia article, a news summary, a textbook chapter — none of these are final sources. They are pointers toward primary sources that can actually be verified. Adding AI to this workflow requires extending the same critical discipline to AI-generated content.

The skills you build in this module — identifying claim types, locating primary sources, cross-referencing independently, assessing source credibility — are transferable to every domain of intellectual work. They are the core of what it means to know something rather than merely to have heard it.

## The Big Picture

Information literacy — the ability to evaluate, verify, and use information critically — is increasingly cited by employers, universities, and researchers as one of the most critical skills for the coming decades. In an environment where AI can generate unlimited plausible-sounding content, and where social media amplifies misinformation at speed, the ability to distinguish reliable from unreliable information is both scarce and valuable.

Careers in journalism, law, medicine, science, policy, intelligence analysis, and virtually every field that involves making decisions based on information require this skill. Even in everyday life — evaluating health claims, financial decisions, political arguments — the person who has a systematic approach to verification is less likely to be deceived and more likely to reach sound conclusions.

## Key Terms

> **Hallucination:** In AI, the generation of confident-sounding false information. Not an occasional bug but a structural consequence of how language models work.
>
> **Primary source:** The original document, data, or creative work — the thing itself. An original research paper, a government report, a historical document, a published book. The most reliable level of source for most claims.
>
> **Secondary source:** A work that analyses, interprets, or summarises primary sources. Textbooks, news articles, Wikipedia entries, and review papers are secondary sources. Useful as pointers but not final verification.
>
> **Peer review:** The process by which scientific research is evaluated by independent experts before publication. Peer-reviewed papers are more reliable than non-reviewed content, though not infallible.
>
> **Citation:** A reference to the source of a claim. AI-generated citations may be real but misquoted, or may be entirely fabricated (a "hallucinated citation").
>
> **Cross-referencing:** Checking a claim against two or more independent sources to increase confidence in its accuracy.
>
> **Source credibility:** The degree to which a source can be trusted based on its track record, expertise, incentives, and methodology.

## Think Deeper — Trust, But Verify

### Activity 1: Understanding Why AI Hallucinates (10 mins)

Open your AI tool and ask it these questions. For each answer, try to verify it using a quick search:

1. "What is the population of [your country]?" — Check against an official government census or statistics website
2. "Who won [a specific award, e.g. the Booker Prize] in [year approximately 5–10 years ago]?" — Check the official award website
3. "What did [a historical figure] say about [a topic they actually wrote about]?" — Look for the original source

Write down: What type of error did you find (wrong number, fake quote, outdated information, something else)? How would you have known it was wrong without checking?

---

### Activity 2: Build the Verification Workflow (20 mins)

**The 5-Step AI Verification Workflow:**

**Step 1 — Identify the claim type.**
Is it a statistic? A historical fact? A quote? A scientific claim? A legal claim? Different claim types require different verification sources.

**Step 2 — Ask for the source.**
Ask the AI: "What is the source for that claim?" Warning: AI may invent a source. Treat it as a lead, not a confirmation.

**Step 3 — Go to primary sources.**
- For statistics: Look for the original dataset or report (World Bank, ONS, WHO, official national statistics)
- For scientific claims: Look for the original peer-reviewed paper (Google Scholar, PubMed)
- For historical facts: Look for the primary historical document or a major academic reference work
- For quotes: Find the original speech, letter, book, or interview

**Step 4 — Cross-reference with two independent sources.**
One source confirming something is not enough.

**Step 5 — Note the date.**
Even verified information may be outdated.

**Now practise:**

Choose one of these topic areas and ask AI to give you a detailed summary with at least 5 specific facts (statistics, names, dates, claims):
- Climate change statistics
- The history of the internet
- The science of sleep
- The effects of social media on teenagers
- Space exploration milestones

Take those 5 specific facts and run them through the 5-step workflow. Document each step for each fact.

### Activity 3: The High-Stakes Check (15 mins)

**Low stakes:** Using AI to suggest birthday party ideas. If something is wrong, the consequence is minor.

**Medium stakes:** Using AI to help with a school essay. If something is wrong, you might submit incorrect work.

**High stakes:** Using AI to understand medical information, legal rights, financial decisions. If something is wrong, the consequences could be serious.

Think about the last week or two. Write down five times you have used AI (or would realistically use AI). For each one:
1. What was the task?
2. What is the stakes level (Low / Medium / High)?
3. Did you (or would you) fact-check? With what level of rigour?

Write a short personal policy: "I will always fully verify AI outputs when [conditions]. I will do a quick spot-check when [conditions]. I will use AI with minimal checking only when [conditions]."

**Bonus challenge:** Ask AI to intentionally give you a mix of correct and incorrect facts about a topic you know well. Then try to identify the false fact using your workflow.

## Deep Dive — How Misinformation Spreads

Verifying AI outputs is one application of information literacy. Another is understanding how misinformation spreads more broadly — and why humans are poor natural detectors of it.

**Research task:** Investigate the psychology and mechanics of misinformation using AI and a search engine.

Key questions to explore:
```
What does research say about why people believe misinformation even when they are educated and intelligent?
What is "motivated reasoning" and how does it make people resistant to correction?
What is the "illusory truth effect" and why does repetition make false claims seem more credible?
What does research say about the effectiveness of fact-checking in changing people's minds?
```

After your research, find ONE real example of a significant piece of misinformation that spread widely online and was eventually debunked. Choose something you can research in depth (COVID-related misinformation, a viral political claim, a health myth — there are many documented examples).

For your chosen example, document:
1. What was the original false claim?
2. How did it spread? What made it shareable?
3. Who tried to correct it, and how effectively?
4. Why did people believe it even when corrections were published?
5. What does this case reveal about the limits of fact-checking as a solution to misinformation?

Write a 200-word analysis. This exercise is not about AI specifically — it is about understanding why information verification matters even in contexts where AI is not involved.

## Debate This

1. **Should AI tools be required to include uncertainty scores or confidence levels with every factual claim they make?** This would make the verification problem more manageable — but it would also require the AI to accurately know what it knows, which may be technically very difficult. Is this a reasonable requirement to impose on AI developers?

2. **Is Wikipedia actually underrated as a source?** Teachers often tell students not to cite Wikipedia, but well-maintained Wikipedia articles contain references to primary sources throughout, are edited by communities of people who care about accuracy, and are often corrected faster than traditional encyclopaedias. When is Wikipedia a reasonable source to use, and when is it not?

3. **Does the existence of AI hallucination make AI fundamentally unsuitable for journalism, education, and research — or is the same verification discipline we should always apply sufficient to manage the risk?** In other words, is AI a new kind of problem or a more powerful version of an old problem?

## Reflect

1. If AI sometimes confidently states wrong information and you cannot always tell, does that make AI a reliable tool for serious research? How should students and journalists adjust their practice?

2. Wikipedia is often dismissed as unreliable — but well-maintained Wikipedia articles cite primary sources throughout. How might Wikipedia be useful in your verification workflow even if it is not itself a final source?

3. The skill of going to primary sources is valuable regardless of AI. How would this workflow change how you approach research for a school essay, even if you were not using AI at all?

4. Think about information you have shared with others — in conversation, on social media, in class work. Can you identify an instance where you shared something that turned out to be wrong or uncertain? How did you feel when you found out?

5. The "illusory truth effect" shows that simply hearing a false claim repeatedly makes it feel more credible. What practical implications does this have for how you consume news and social media?
