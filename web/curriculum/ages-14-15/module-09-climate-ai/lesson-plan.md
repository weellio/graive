## Key Concepts

### AI's Energy Footprint

Training large AI models requires enormous amounts of computing power — and computing power requires electricity. Consider some estimates:

- Training GPT-3 produced an estimated 552 tonnes of CO2 equivalent — roughly the same as driving a car 1.5 million kilometres
- A single ChatGPT query uses approximately 10x more energy than a Google search
- Data centres globally already account for around 1–2% of world electricity consumption — and AI is growing that rapidly
- The water used for cooling AI data centres is also significant — Microsoft reported an 34% increase in water consumption in 2022, partly attributed to AI

> "The irony is real: the more we use AI to help us solve climate change, the more carbon we emit using AI. But the calculation isn't simple — it depends on what the AI is being used for."

### The Counterargument: AI as Climate Tool
AI is also being applied directly to climate challenges:

- **Climate modelling** — AI dramatically speeds up climate simulations that used to take weeks
- **Energy grid optimisation** — Google's DeepMind AI reduced cooling energy use in its data centres by 40%
- **Materials discovery** — AI helps identify new materials for batteries and solar panels
- **Deforestation detection** — satellite + AI systems can detect illegal logging in near real-time
- **Agricultural efficiency** — AI optimises irrigation and fertiliser use, reducing emissions
- **Fusion energy research** — AI is being used to solve key technical problems in clean fusion energy
- **Carbon capture optimisation** — AI optimises direct air capture of CO2

The argument: even if AI has a large footprint, if it helps solve climate change faster, the net effect is positive.

### The Tradeoff Framework
How do we weigh these competing effects?

Factors to consider:
1. **Energy source** — AI running on renewable energy has much lower climate impact
2. **What it replaces** — if AI replaces a more carbon-intensive process, the net effect may be positive
3. **Leverage effects** — AI that helps design a better solar panel may offset its own emissions millions of times over
4. **Additionality** — would this climate benefit have happened without AI?
5. **Rebound effects** — if AI makes energy cheaper to use, does that lead people to use more of it?

## The Rebound Effect Problem

One major concern in energy policy is the **rebound effect** (also called the Jevons paradox): when technology makes something more efficient, people often use more of it, wiping out the efficiency gains.

Examples:
- More fuel-efficient cars → people drive more and buy bigger cars → total fuel use stays similar
- LED lighting is much more efficient → cities install more lighting → total energy use for lighting doesn't fall much

The concern: if AI makes computation so powerful and cheap, won't people just use vastly more computation, overwhelming any efficiency gains?

> "Efficiency gains don't automatically lead to less resource use. They often lead to more use. This is one of the hardest problems in climate policy."

## The Green AI Movement

Some researchers are now calling for "Green AI" — measuring and reporting the carbon cost of AI systems, and prioritising research that achieves good performance at lower computational cost.

Currently, AI research often rewards whoever builds the biggest model with the most impressive results — regardless of the carbon cost.

Green AI argues we need to:
- Report the carbon footprint of AI models alongside performance metrics
- Reward research that achieves equivalent results with less compute
- Power AI data centres entirely with renewables
- Locate data centres where renewable energy is cheapest
