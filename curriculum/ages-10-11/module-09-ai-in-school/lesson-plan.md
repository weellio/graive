# Lesson Plan: AI in Your School Life
### Explorer Level (Ages 10–11) | Module 09

---

## Objective
By the end of this lesson, you'll be able to explain the difference between using AI to help you learn and using it to avoid learning — and you'll have your own personal guide for using AI ethically at school.

---

## What You'll Need
- A device with internet access
- Your AI tool (ChatGPT free or Claude free)
- Paper and pens
- About 35–45 minutes

## Key Concepts

### AI as a Tutor vs AI as a Shortcut
AI can do two very different things for you at school:

**As a tutor (good!):**
- Explain something you don't understand in a different way
- Quiz you on a topic to test yourself
- Give you feedback on your own writing
- Help you brainstorm ideas before you write

**As a shortcut (risky):**
- Write your essay for you to hand in
- Answer questions on a test you're supposed to do yourself
- Give you answers to copy without understanding them

> "Using AI to understand something better = learning. Using AI to avoid understanding = cheating yourself."

### Why Does It Matter?
When AI does your homework FOR you, you might get a good grade — but you miss out on actually learning. And here's the thing: your teachers give you work so that your brain gets stronger, like exercise. If AI lifts the weights for you, your brain doesn't get any stronger.

### What Most Schools Say
Most schools currently allow:
- Using AI to help you understand something
- Using AI for brainstorming or getting ideas
- Using AI to proofread your own writing

Most schools do NOT allow:
- Copying AI text and handing it in as your own
- Using AI during tests or exams unless specifically allowed
- Pretending AI-generated work is all your own without saying so

**Important:** Rules vary by school and teacher! Always check with your teacher if you're unsure.

## Try It — The School AI Experiment

### Activity 1: AI as a Tutor (10 mins)

Think of something from school you've been confused about recently. Maybe a maths topic, a science concept, something from history...

My confusing topic: ___

Now open your AI tool and ask it to explain it to you. But here's the key rule: **you're not allowed to just copy the explanation. You have to understand it well enough to explain it in your own words.**

After reading the AI's explanation, close it and write your own explanation in 3–5 sentences:

My explanation in my own words: ___

Did the AI's explanation help? Rate it: ___/10

---

### Activity 2: Is It Okay? (10 mins)

Read each school situation below. Decide: **OK**, **Ask Your Teacher**, or **Not OK**.

Write your answer and a short reason why.

1. You don't understand what "photosynthesis" means. You ask AI to explain it and take notes. ___
   Why: ___

2. You have to write a story for English class. You ask AI to write the whole story, then copy it out. ___
   Why: ___

3. You have a maths test tomorrow. You ask AI to quiz you with practice questions. ___
   Why: ___

4. You have to write about your favourite hobby. You ask AI for ideas to include, then write it yourself. ___
   Why: ___

5. You don't understand question 4 on your homework. You ask AI "What is the answer to [question 4]?" and copy the answer. ___
   Why: ___

6. You wrote a paragraph but it sounds clunky. You ask AI to "make this sound better" and copy the new version word-for-word. ___
   Why: ___

7. You ask AI to quiz you with questions about the book you read for English. ___
   Why: ___

## The Honest Test
Before submitting any school work where you used AI, ask yourself these three questions:

1. **Could I explain this to my teacher in my own words?**
   If no — you probably relied too much on AI.

2. **Did I learn something from doing this?**
   If no — AI might have done the learning for you.

3. **If my teacher asked me "did you use AI?" would I be comfortable being honest?**
   If no — you probably crossed a line.

> "The best use of AI at school is when it helps you understand more, not when it understands things instead of you."

## Reflect
1. Can you think of a time (even imaginary) when using AI for school could backfire or go wrong?
2. Do you think schools should teach students how to use AI properly? Why or why not?
3. What's one way you could use AI this week to actually improve your learning (not skip it)?

## Challenge
**The AI Tutor Test:**

Pick a subject you have a test or exam coming up on. Use AI as a study partner to prepare — but using ONLY these allowed techniques:
- Ask it to quiz you with questions and give you feedback
- Ask it to explain things you got wrong
- Ask it to give you a summary to test yourself against

After studying this way for 20 minutes, write down:
- 3 things you now understand better: ___, ___, ___
- 1 thing you still need to work on: ___
- How helpful was AI as a study partner: ___/10

Did it feel different to using AI to just give you answers? How?
