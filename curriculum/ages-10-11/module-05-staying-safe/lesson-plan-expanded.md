# Lesson Plan: Staying Safe Online
### Explorer Level (Ages 10–11) | Module 05

---

## About This Lesson

The internet is one of the most powerful tools ever created. It puts the knowledge of every library in history into the palm of your hand. It connects you to friends, games, creativity, and learning from across the entire world. And because it is so powerful and so present in your life, it is also worth thinking carefully about how you use it — not to make you scared, but to make you confident.

Think of the internet like a massive city you can explore. Most of the city is friendly, useful, and genuinely wonderful. There are great shops, fascinating museums, brilliant parks. But like any city, there are also a few streets you should not walk down alone, people who are not quite who they say they are, and situations that can catch you off guard if you are not paying attention. A city is not dangerous — but a smart visitor knows the basic rules that keep them safe so they can enjoy everything without worry.

One of the most important things to understand about online safety in the age of AI is that the boundary between "real" and "made up" is getting harder to see. A realistic-looking photo might be AI-generated. A friendly message might not be from a real person. A "quiz" asking for personal information might be designed to steal that information. AI makes all of these things easier to create and harder to spot — which is why the five rules in this lesson are more important than ever.

The good news is that online safety is not really about being suspicious of everything. It is about developing a set of reliable habits — like automatically wearing a seatbelt in a car, or looking both ways before crossing a road. Once these habits are built in, they protect you without you having to think hard every time. By the end of this lesson, you will have made your own personal safety card to help you remember the habits that matter most.

---

## Key Concepts

- **Private information:** Details that, if shared with the wrong person, could put you in danger — home address, phone number, school name, your daily routine, passwords.
- **Online identity:** The version of yourself you choose to show online — you have control over how much you share.
- **Digital footprint:** Everything you post, share, and do online leaves a trace — some of it permanent and searchable.
- **Trusted adult:** A specific person you have already decided you would tell if something online felt wrong or uncomfortable.
- **Phishing:** When someone online pretends to be trustworthy in order to get your personal information or money.

---

## Fun Facts

- The average person has over 100 online accounts — which means most people are sharing personal details in far more places than they realise.
- Researchers have shown that AI can now generate a realistic-sounding fake "friendly conversation" fast enough to run thousands of them at once — which is why being cautious about who you talk to online matters more now than ever before.
- Your digital footprint starts building from your very first online post and can follow you for years — things posted at age 10 have sometimes shown up in searches when those same people were applying for jobs at 25.

## Try It — Make Your Safety Card

### Part 1: The Five Rules Card (15 mins)

Draw or write out your own version of the Five Online Rules. Make it yours — decorate it, use your own words, add drawings.

The five rules to include:
1. Keep private information private
2. Not everyone online is who they say they are
3. Think before you post
4. Tell someone if something feels wrong
5. Use AI honestly and safely

For each rule, write **one example** in your own words. Like: "Private information means... (my address, my school, my phone number)."

---

### Part 2: The Adult Interview (5 mins)

Find a trusted adult (parent, grandparent, older sibling, teacher).

Ask them: "What's one online safety rule you think is really important?"

Write their answer on your card and add their name.

---

### Part 3: The Private vs OK Test (5 mins)

For each of these, decide: **Private** (don't share) or **OK to share** (generally fine):

| Information | Private or OK? |
|------------|---------------|
| Your favourite colour | |
| Your home address | |
| Which sport you play | |
| Your school name | |
| A funny story about your day | |
| Your phone number | |
| Your opinion on a movie | |
| Your full name + what you look like + where you go after school | |

**Discuss with an adult: why is the last one different from just sharing your name?**

---

### Part 4: The Scenario Game (10 mins)

Read each scenario below. For each one, decide what you would do — and write a one-sentence explanation of why.

**Scenario A:** A person you have been chatting to online for a week — who you have never met in real life — says they live near you and asks which school you go to. They seem really friendly.

**Scenario B:** You receive a message saying: "Congratulations! You have been selected for a free gaming gift card. Click here and enter your name, age, and home address to claim your prize."

**Scenario C:** A friend sends you a funny photo to share. It shows a person who looks embarrassed in a real situation. Your friend says "everyone is sharing it."

**Scenario D:** An AI chatbot on a website asks: "To personalise your experience, what is your full name and birthday?"

**Scenario E:** Someone in an online game you play starts asking personal questions — they say they are also 11 years old but want to know where you live and what you look like.

For each one, write:
- What I would do: _______________
- Why: _______________

Discuss your answers with a trusted adult if you can. There are no trick answers — the point is to practise thinking through these situations before you encounter them.

---

### Part 5: The AI Safety Special (5 mins)

AI tools are a specific online environment worth thinking about carefully.

Answer these questions:
1. When you type something into an AI tool like ChatGPT, who might be able to see it? (Hint: the company behind the AI tool can see conversations — treat it as semi-public.)
2. Should you type your real full name, address, or password into an AI chat? Why or why not?
3. If an AI tool gives you advice about something important (health, a difficult situation with a friend, a legal question), what should you do before following that advice?

Write down your three answers. Share them with a trusted adult and discuss whether they agree.

---

## Reflect (do this with a parent if possible)
1. Have you ever seen anything online that made you feel uncomfortable? (No need to share details — just yes or no.)
2. If something weird happened online, who would you tell? Name them.
3. Is there anything about being online that you've wondered about or felt unsure about?
4. Looking at your digital footprint — the things you post and share — is there anything you wish you had kept more private?
5. After this lesson, what is one thing you are going to do differently online?

---

## Level Up Challenge

Design an online safety guide for a younger sibling, cousin, or friend aged 7–8.

The guide should:
- Use simple language that a younger child can understand
- Cover the three most important rules you think they need to know first
- Include at least one example of a tricky situation they might face and what to do
- Be no more than one page long (so they will actually read it)
- Have a friendly, not scary, tone — you want them to feel confident, not frightened

Use AI to help you draft it if you like — but use your own judgement to check it makes sense for a real 7-year-old, not just a generic child.

After you write it, read it to someone and ask: "Would a 7-year-old understand this?"

---

## Keep Your Card!

Put your Five Rules card somewhere you'll see it — on your desk, in your notebook, on your wall.

This one's not about AI skills — it's about keeping yourself safe while you explore everything the internet and AI have to offer.
