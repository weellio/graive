# Lesson Plan: Remix and Improve
### Explorer Level (Ages 10–11) | Module 09

---

## About This Lesson

Think about the best song you have ever heard. It did not arrive fully formed in the musician's head and then get recorded perfectly in one take. It was written, rewritten, played to friends who said "the chorus needs something," revised again, rehearsed, recorded, sent back for a different mix, and only released after dozens of versions that the public never heard. Every great creative work — every song, painting, book, video game, and film — is the result of iteration. The word "iteration" just means doing something, improving it, doing it again, and repeating until it is right.

AI is the world's most patient collaboration partner. You can tell it to change one thing, then change another thing, then go back and change the first thing again, then try a completely different direction — and it never gets tired, never gets frustrated, never sulks because you rejected its first idea. This makes it an extraordinary tool for iterative improvement, but only if you know how to use follow-up messages effectively. Most people stop after the first response, even when it is just okay. The real magic is in what happens next.

The key to good iteration is being specific about what is wrong. "Make it better" does not work. "Make it better" gives the AI no information about what you think is missing. But "the ending feels rushed — can you add two more sentences that slow down the action and let the reader feel the tension?" — that works. You are diagnosing a specific problem and prescribing a specific fix. Good iterators are good diagnosticians. They read what they got, identify the weakest part, and target it precisely.

There is also an important creative principle here: you do not always have to get better at iteration by going forward. Sometimes the best move is to try a completely different direction with your second prompt and see if you prefer it — then cherry-pick the best elements of both versions. Professional editors, designers, and musicians do this all the time. They create multiple versions, then assemble the best final product from the strongest parts of each one.

---

## Key Concepts

- **Iterative prompting:** The process of improving an AI's output step by step through targeted follow-up messages.
- **Diagnosis:** Identifying the specific weakness in an AI response that you want to fix.
- **Follow-up prompt:** A message that continues a conversation and refines a previous response, rather than starting from scratch.
- **Version control:** Keeping track of different versions of something so you can compare them and choose the best elements.
- **Specific feedback:** Improvement instructions that name a precise problem and suggest a precise fix — the opposite of vague feedback like "make it better."

---

## Fun Facts

- Professional authors often write 10 to 20 drafts of a chapter before it is ready for a reader. With AI assistance, that iteration cycle can happen in minutes rather than months.
- In the music industry, the concept of remixing — taking an existing song and reworking it into something new — has existed for decades. AI remixing is just the newest version of a creative tradition that has always been central to music.
- Research into AI and creativity shows that humans who work with AI through multiple iterations consistently produce results that are rated as more creative and higher quality than either the human or the AI could produce alone in a single step.

## Try It — The Remix Challenge

### Activity 1: Start With a Bad Prompt (10 mins)

Let us deliberately write a terrible prompt and see what happens.

Open your AI tool and type this exact prompt:
> "Write a story."

Read what you get. It is probably a story — but it is probably not very interesting, very personal, or very useful to you. The AI had no idea what you wanted, so it guessed.

Now you are going to remix it. With each remix, add one more piece of information. After each version, copy the AI's response into your notes.

**Remix 1 — Add a character:**
> "Write a story about a girl who loves dinosaurs."

**Remix 2 — Add a setting:**
> "Write a short story about a girl who loves dinosaurs, set in a modern city."

**Remix 3 — Add a problem:**
> "Write a short story about a girl who loves dinosaurs, set in a modern city. She discovers a real dinosaur egg in the park and has to decide what to do with it."

**Remix 4 — Add tone and length:**
> "Write a short, funny story (about 150 words) about a girl who loves dinosaurs. She is growing up in a modern city and discovers a real dinosaur egg in the park. She has to decide whether to keep it secret or tell someone."

Compare your four responses side by side.

Write down your answers to these questions:
- Which response was most interesting? Why?
- Which part of the prompt made the biggest difference?
- What would you add in a Remix 5 to make it even better?

---

### Activity 2: Fix a Broken Output (10 mins)

Sometimes the AI gives you an answer that is almost right — but something is off. Your job is to diagnose the problem and fix it with a follow-up instruction.

Try this prompt:
> "Explain how volcanoes work. Make it interesting for a 10-year-old."

Read the response. Now find at least two things you would like to change. Here are some things to look for:
- Is it too long?
- Is it boring?
- Does it use words that are too complicated?
- Is it missing something cool?
- Does it feel like a textbook rather than something a real person wrote?

For each problem you find, write a **specific follow-up prompt** to fix just that one thing. For example:
- "That was good but too long. Can you shorten it to 5 sentences without losing the coolest facts?"
- "Instead of saying 'magma', can you describe it in a way that helps me picture it — like what it looks like and feels like?"
- "Add one sentence at the end that tells me something about volcanoes that most people do not know."

Try at least two follow-up prompts. Write down what changed each time.

**Key insight:** You do not have to start over when AI gives you a bad answer. You can keep the conversation going and steer it toward what you actually want.

---

### Activity 3: The Remix Race (10 mins)

This is a fun challenge. You have exactly 3 attempts to get the best possible output.

Your goal: Get the AI to write the best possible birthday message for a friend who is obsessed with a specific hobby (you choose the hobby — it could be football, drawing, gaming, baking, anything).

**Rules:**
- Attempt 1: Write any prompt you like
- Attempt 2: Remix it based on what was wrong with Attempt 1
- Attempt 3: Final remix — make it perfect

After your 3 attempts, read all three birthday messages aloud (or to yourself).

Then score each one out of 10 on:
- How personal does it feel? (1–10)
- Would my friend actually like receiving this? (1–10)
- Does it sound like a real human wrote it? (1–10)

Add up the scores for each attempt. Did your score improve across the three attempts?

Write one sentence about what you learned about prompting from this activity.

---

### Activity 4: The Mix and Match Version (10 mins)

Sometimes the best final version is built from different parts of different drafts — not just a linear improvement of one draft.

Try this: Ask the AI to write the opening paragraph of a story twice, using two completely different approaches:

**Version A:**
> "Write the opening paragraph of a mystery story set in a school. Make it spooky and tense — the reader should feel something is very wrong."

**Version B:**
> "Write the opening paragraph of a mystery story set in a school. Start in the middle of the action — show, don't tell. Begin with a single strange detail that makes the reader want to know more."

After you read both, try to write a third version yourself — combining the best sentence from Version A with the best idea from Version B. You can type this third version yourself or ask the AI to combine them for you.

Which final version is best? Was the combined version stronger than either original?

**The lesson:** Iteration does not have to be linear. You can go sideways, combine elements, and build your final version from the best pieces of multiple drafts.

---

### Activity 5: The Micro-Improvement (5 mins)

This quick activity practises the skill of precision.

Take any sentence from any AI output you have generated today — preferably one that is almost good but not quite.

Write three different follow-up prompts to improve just that one sentence in three different ways:

1. Make it more vivid and specific (add a sensory detail)
2. Make it funnier
3. Make it shorter without losing its meaning

Run all three. Which single sentence improvement would you keep?

## Reflect
Answer these questions out loud or write them down:

1. Why do you think AI gives vague answers to vague prompts? What does that tell you about how AI works?
2. Is iterative prompting (improving step by step) something you do in other parts of your life — like editing a drawing, rewriting a sentence in your essay, or practising a skill? How is it similar?
3. After doing these activities, do you think getting good at prompting is a skill worth practising? Why or why not?
4. In the Mix and Match activity, you built a new version from parts of two different drafts. Can you think of other creative areas where this "best of both" approach might work well?
5. What is the hardest part of iteration for you personally — coming up with the specific feedback, or knowing when to stop and say "this is good enough"?

## Level Up Challenge

The Iterative Speed Run.

Your goal: starting from the prompt "Write a poem," reach the best poem you have ever got from AI in the fewest number of iterations possible.

Rules:
- You must start with "Write a poem." — no additions, no changes
- Each iteration must be a single, specific improvement instruction
- You must note what you changed and why after each step
- Stop when you genuinely think the poem is something special

After you finish, write up your "iteration log" — a numbered list of every prompt you sent, what changed, and what the result was.

Then challenge someone: Show them your first output ("Write a poem") and your final output side by side. Can they believe it started from the same boring prompt? How many iterations did it take?
