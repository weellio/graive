# Lesson Plan: What Is AI?
### Explorer Level (Ages 10–11) | Module 01

---

## About This Lesson

Have you ever wondered how your phone knows what word you are about to type next, or how a video game enemy seems to "think" about where you are hiding? The secret behind all of that is Artificial Intelligence — but it is probably not what you imagine. Most people picture a robot with a glowing brain, or a supercomputer that thinks like a human. The truth is actually more interesting, and a lot more understandable, than that.

Think about how you learned to recognise a dog. Nobody sat you down and said, "A dog has four legs, pointy ears, and makes a woof sound — remember that forever." Instead, you just saw hundreds of dogs over the years — at the park, in books, on TV — and your brain quietly built up a picture of what "dog" means. AI learns in almost exactly the same way. It is shown millions and millions of examples until it gets very good at spotting patterns. It is not thinking. It is not imagining. It is matching patterns — incredibly fast, at an enormous scale.

Imagine you have a friend who has read every book, article, and website ever written. They have not understood all of it the way you understand things, but they have noticed which words tend to appear next to which other words, which facts come up again and again, which stories follow similar shapes. When you ask them a question, they pull together all those patterns to give you a response. That is roughly how a modern AI language model works. It is a pattern spotter at a scale that is almost impossible to imagine.

Here is the most important thing to keep in mind before we start: because AI learns from patterns in human-made information, it sometimes gets things wrong. It can sound very confident while being completely mistaken. It can repeat outdated information. It can even make things up — this is called a "hallucination." Your job as an Explorer is to learn how to use AI's pattern-spotting power while always staying the critical, thinking human in the room.

---

## Key Concepts

- **Artificial Intelligence (AI):** A computer system that learns to do tasks by studying millions of examples, rather than being given a fixed set of rules.
- **Pattern matching:** The core skill of AI — finding similarities and connections across huge amounts of data.
- **Training data:** The examples AI was shown during its learning phase; everything it "knows" comes from this.
- **Hallucination:** When AI states something false with total confidence because it is filling in a pattern, not checking facts.
- **Critical thinking:** The human superpower that AI does not have — the ability to question, verify, and judge information.

---

## Fun Facts

- The AI behind ChatGPT learned from a text dataset so large that if you read it out loud without stopping, it would take over 50,000 years to finish.
- AI can beat the world's best chess players, but it cannot reliably count how many letters are in a word — because chess is all patterns, but counting requires a different kind of thinking.
- The first computer program that played checkers was written in 1951 — meaning AI has been learning games from examples for longer than most of your grandparents have been alive.

## Try It — Play with the Pattern Spotter

### Activity 1: The Animal Game (10 mins)

Open your AI tool and try these questions:

1. "What animal would make the world's worst pet and why?"
2. "What animal would make the BEST pet if you lived underwater?"
3. "Invent a brand new animal by combining two existing animals. What does it look like? What is it called? What does it eat?"

For each answer, write down:
- Did it surprise you?
- Did it make sense?
- Was there anything that seemed wrong or silly?

---

### Activity 2: The Pattern Experiment (10 mins)

AI learned from patterns. Let's test that!

Ask the AI to complete these:

1. "A dog says woof. A cat says meow. A dragon says..."
2. "Red, orange, yellow... what comes next in this pattern and why?"
3. "Every good story has a beginning, a middle, and... what? Tell me 5 things a good story ending needs."

Notice: it's not looking these up. It's using what it knows about patterns in language.

---

### Activity 3: Catch a Mistake (10 mins)

Ask the AI a question you *know* the answer to. Something from school, your hobby, or your local area.

Then check: did it get it right?

**My question:** _______________________
**What AI said:** _______________________
**Was it right?** Yes / No / Almost

Try this with 2–3 questions. Remember: it's okay if it gets things wrong — that's important to know!

---

### Activity 4: The Confidence Trap (10 mins)

Here is something tricky about AI: it almost always sounds confident, even when it is wrong. Let us test this.

Ask the AI a question about something very specific and local that it probably would not know — like the name of a small shop in your town, a local sports team's recent score, or a school event.

Then ask the AI: **"How confident are you in that answer, on a scale of 1 to 10?"**

Write down:
- What did it say when you asked the question?
- How confident did it say it was?
- Do you think it was actually right?

Now try this with one question you know for certain it can get right (like: "What is the capital city of France?") and compare the confidence scores.

**What did you notice about how AI talks about its own confidence?**

This activity teaches you one of the most important AI literacy skills: a confident answer is not the same as a correct answer.

---

### Activity 5: AI vs. You (5 mins)

This is a mini challenge. Ask the AI to describe your favourite food in a way that would make someone really want to eat it.

Then YOU write your own description of the same food in 3–4 sentences.

Read both back. Which one feels more real? Which one would actually make your mouth water?

Often, your human description will win — because you have actually tasted it, smelled it, felt excited about it. AI has only read descriptions. That is a real and important difference.

## Draw It!

In your notebook or on paper, draw what you think AI looks like on the inside. Not a robot — draw the *idea* of it. What does "pattern matching" look like to you?

Some ideas: a giant spider web of connected words? A river of flowing text? A massive filing cabinet? There's no wrong answer. This is just to help you remember the concept.

---

## Reflect
Answer these questions out loud or write them down:

1. What's one thing that surprised you about how AI works?
2. What's one thing AI can do that impressed you?
3. What's one reason you should check what AI tells you before using it?
4. AI learns from examples made by humans. If humans sometimes have wrong ideas or biases, what does that mean for AI?
5. Is there anything AI did in these activities that you could not have done yourself? Is there anything you did that AI probably could not do?

---

## Level Up Challenge

This one is for fast finishers who want a bigger brain workout.

Ask the AI to explain how it works — in its own words. Use this prompt:

> "Can you explain, in simple language for an 11-year-old, how you actually work? What is happening inside you when I ask you a question?"

Read the answer carefully. Then try to find at least one thing in the explanation that you think might not be completely accurate or might be oversimplified. Use what you learned in this lesson to spot where the AI might be leaving something out.

Write a paragraph starting with: "The AI explained itself by saying... but I think the part about... might not be the full story because..."

This is a genuinely challenging task — it requires you to use a tool to evaluate itself, which is a very advanced kind of thinking.
