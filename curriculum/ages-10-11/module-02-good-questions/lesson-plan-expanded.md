# Lesson Plan: Asking Good Questions
### Explorer Level (Ages 10–11) | Module 02

---

## About This Lesson

Imagine you walk into the world's best restaurant and tell the chef: "Make me some food." You might get something edible — but it probably will not be your favourite meal. Now imagine you say: "I am really hungry and I love spicy things, but I hate mushrooms. Can you make me something warm with lots of flavour, that takes under 20 minutes?" Suddenly, the chef has everything they need to cook something amazing. That is exactly the difference between a vague question and a specific one — and it is the single biggest skill you can develop as an AI user.

Most people who try AI for the first time get underwhelming results. They type something like "write me a story" or "help with my homework" and get back something dull and generic. Then they think: "AI is not that impressive." But the problem is almost never the AI. It is the question. AI is more like a powerful tool than a magic trick — and just like any tool, the results depend entirely on how well you use it.

The three things that transform a vague prompt into a specific one are Setting, Stakes, and Style. Setting tells the AI where and what — the context of what you want. Stakes tells the AI why it matters — the problem or goal your character or topic is dealing with. Style tells the AI how it should feel — the tone, voice, and energy of the response. When you include all three, you give the AI enough material to produce something genuinely surprising and tailored to your vision.

The other powerful technique is iteration — going back and improving. Real writers do not write one draft and call it done. Scientists do not run one experiment. Even musicians do not record one take. The magic of working with AI is that you can iterate at the speed of typing. Each follow-up message is a chance to steer the output closer to exactly what you imagined. By the end of this lesson, you will have tried this for yourself and felt the difference — and that difference is enormous.

---

## Key Concepts

- **Prompt:** The message or question you send to an AI tool — the quality of your prompt determines the quality of the output.
- **Setting:** The context of your request — where it takes place, what it is about, and who is involved.
- **Stakes:** What matters in the scenario — the problem, the goal, or what is at risk.
- **Style:** The tone, voice, and format you want — funny, spooky, dramatic, short, rhyming, etc.
- **Iteration:** The process of improving step by step — sending follow-up messages to refine and improve what the AI gives you.

---

## Fun Facts

- The way you word a prompt can change an AI's answer dramatically — researchers have found that adding the phrase "think step by step" to a maths problem makes AI far more likely to get it right.
- AI researchers actually study "prompt engineering" as a serious field of work — large companies pay people whose entire job is to figure out the best ways to phrase instructions for AI.
- In tests, the difference in quality between the best-worded prompt and the worst-worded prompt for the same task can be so large that the outputs look like they came from completely different systems.

## Try It — The Vague vs Specific Showdown

### Round 1: Story Mode (10 mins)

**Vague prompt:** Type this exactly: `Write me a story.`
Copy the output. Underline the most boring bits.

**Now build your specific version:**
Fill in the blanks:

```
Setting:  A story set in _______________________
Stakes:   Where the main character has to _______________________
          before _______________________
Style:    Written in a _______________________ tone
          (funny / spooky / exciting / sad / adventure-style)
```

Combine them into one sentence prompt and run it.

Compare the two outputs. Circle your favourite sentences in the second one.

**Which was better? By how much? (Scale of 1–10)**
- Vague: ____/10
- Specific: ____/10

---

### Round 2: Poem Mode (10 mins)

**Vague prompt:** `Write me a poem.`
Copy the output.

**Your specific version:** Pick something you love (pet, sport, hobby, favourite food).

Use this template:
```
Write a [NUMBER]-line poem about [YOUR THING].
The poem should sound like [CHARACTER/PERSPECTIVE].
Make it [STYLE: funny / dramatic / rhyming / silly].
Include at least one surprising image.
```

Example: *Write a 8-line poem about pizza from the perspective of someone who has never tried it before. Make it dramatic and a little bit suspicious of the cheese.*

Run your specific version. Compare!

---

### Round 3: The Iteration Challenge (10 mins)

Take your specific story or poem from above. Now improve it with follow-up messages.

Try at least TWO of these:
- "Make it 20% funnier"
- "Add a plot twist at the end"
- "Make the main character a lot more nervous"
- "Change the ending so it's unexpected"
- "Make it rhyme"
- "Add a detail about what the character smells"

Each time, write down what you asked and what changed.

**What was the best version? What made it the best?**

---

### Round 4: The Perspective Shift (10 mins)

This activity shows you how dramatically the Style ingredient can transform the same content.

Pick any simple object near you right now — a cup, a pen, a shoe, a book.

Now ask the AI to describe this object FOUR times, each time from a completely different perspective:

1. From the perspective of a scientist examining it for the first time
2. From the perspective of the object itself, feeling sad and lonely
3. From the perspective of a very dramatic TV presenter on a shopping channel trying to sell it
4. From the perspective of a 3-year-old who has never seen one before

After you get all four responses, rank them from most interesting to least interesting. Which perspective produced the most surprising writing?

**The lesson:** The same subject can produce wildly different, brilliant writing depending on the style instruction you give. You have enormous creative power just in the way you ask.

---

### Round 5: The Constraint Challenge (5 mins)

Great writers sometimes use constraints to unlock creativity. Try giving the AI one unusual rule to follow.

Write a prompt for a short story (you choose the subject) and include one of these unusual constraints:

- "The story must contain exactly zero adjectives."
- "Every sentence must start with the next letter of the alphabet (A, B, C, D...)."
- "The main character is not allowed to speak — they can only communicate through actions."
- "The story must be told entirely in questions."

Run it and see what happens. Constraints often force more creative, interesting writing than total freedom.

**Write one sentence about what you learned from this activity.**

## The Three S's Poster

In your notebook, draw a poster or card you can keep:

```
┌─────────────────────────────────┐
│     THE THREE S's               │
│                                 │
│  SETTING  — Where/when/what?    │
│  STAKES   — What's at risk?     │
│  STYLE    — What should it      │
│               feel like?        │
└─────────────────────────────────┘
```

Decorate it! Put it somewhere you'll see it when you're writing prompts.

## Reflect
Answer out loud or write:

1. What was the biggest difference between your vague and specific prompts?
2. Which of the Three S's made the biggest difference for you?
3. What would you try to ask AI to help with in real life?
4. When you used follow-up messages to iterate, what changed in the output? What does this tell you about how AI understands what you want?
5. Were there any moments where the AI surprised you — where it produced something you could not have come up with yourself?

## Level Up Challenge

Here is a genuinely tricky creative challenge for fast finishers.

Write a single prompt — one message — that gets the AI to produce a piece of writing that does ALL of the following at the same time:

- It is set in a world where one specific technology does not exist (your choice which one)
- It is written in the voice of a character who thinks this missing technology is a good thing
- It ends with an unexpected discovery that makes the reader question whether the character was right
- It is exactly 100 words long (ask the AI to count, then check it)

This requires you to pack a lot of Setting, Stakes, and Style information into one clear, precise prompt. Write out your prompt before running it, then compare your plan to the result.
