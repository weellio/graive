# Video Script: Asking Good Questions
### Explorer Level (Ages 10–11) | Module 02 | ~6 minutes

---

## HOOK (0:00–0:35)

**[HOST, holding up two pieces of paper]**

"I'm going to ask AI two things. They're about the same topic. Watch what comes out.

First ask: 'Write a story.'

**[SHOW generic output: 'Once upon a time there was a person who had an adventure...']**

Now ask: 'Write a funny story about a 10-year-old inventor who accidentally makes their dog genius-level smart, but the dog only wants to use its intelligence to outsmart squirrels.'

**[SHOW amazing, detailed, funny output]**

Same AI. Completely different result.

The secret? Being specific. That's what today is all about."

**[TITLE CARD: "Asking Good Questions"]**

---

## THE VAGUE VS SPECIFIC GAME (0:35–2:30)

**[HOST, fun game-show energy]**

"Here's a challenge — I call it the Vague vs Specific game. And you can play it yourself at home.

Vague question: 'Tell me about space.'
Specific question: 'What would it feel like to float in zero gravity on the International Space Station for the first time? Describe it like I've never heard of space before.'

Vague question: 'Write a poem.'
Specific question: 'Write a 6-line poem about my cat who thinks she's the most important creature in the universe. Make it sound like she wrote it herself.'

Every time you go from vague to specific — the output gets way better.

Why? Remember — AI is a pattern spotter. When you're vague, it has to guess which pattern you want. When you're specific, you're pointing it at exactly the right pattern.

Think of it like ordering food. 'I'll have something' gets you... whatever. 'I'll have a large cheese pizza with extra pepperoni, no mushrooms, with garlic bread' gets you exactly what you want."

---

## THE THREE SPECIFICS (2:30–4:00)

**[HOST, ticking off fingers]**

"There are three easy ways to be more specific. I call them the Three S's:

**1. Setting** — where, when, what world?
Instead of 'a story about a robot' → 'a story about a small robot living in a library in the future'

**2. Stakes** — what's the problem or challenge?
Instead of 'a story about a robot' → 'a story about a small robot who is worried about becoming outdated and being turned off'

**3. Style** — what should it feel like?
Instead of 'a story about a robot' → 'a funny story' or 'a scary story' or 'a story told in the robot's diary'

Mix all three: 'Write a funny diary entry from a small robot who lives in a future library and is very worried about becoming outdated before he can finish cataloguing all the books.'"

**[TYPE THAT PROMPT LIVE — show the delightful output]**

"See? SO much better."

---

## THE ITERATION SECRET (4:00–5:00)

**[HOST]**

"Here's a secret that even adults don't always know: you don't have to get it right the first time.

When AI gives you something that's almost right — you can just say: 'Make it funnier.' Or 'Make the robot smaller and more nervous.' Or 'Add a part where a human tries to help but makes things worse.'

You're having a conversation! It's not one-and-done. The best outputs come from going back and forth — trying, adjusting, trying again.

This is called **iterating** — and it's one of the most important skills in this whole course."

---

## CHALLENGE (5:00–5:40)

**[HOST, game show energy]**

"Your challenge — the Vague vs Specific experiment!

Pick ANY topic you love. Could be your favourite game, sport, animal, book character — anything.

First, write the vaguest possible prompt about it. Like: 'Write about Minecraft.'
Run it. See what you get.

Then make it as specific as possible — use Setting, Stakes, and Style.
Run THAT. See what you get.

Screenshot or write down both and compare. I bet you'll never go back to vague prompts again.

Tell me in the comments: what was your topic, and what was the biggest difference between the two?"
