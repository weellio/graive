# Lesson Plan: Real vs Fake
### Explorer Level (Ages 10–11) | Module 11

---

## About This Lesson

For most of human history, a photograph was considered proof. If someone showed you a photo, you could be fairly confident that whatever was in it had really happened, because creating a fake photograph required expensive, specialised equipment and a great deal of skill. That certainty is now gone. AI image generators can create a photorealistic image of absolutely anything in seconds — a historical event that never happened, a person doing something they never did, a place that does not exist — and the best ones are nearly indistinguishable from real photographs. This is one of the most significant changes in the history of communication, and it is happening right now, during your lifetime.

The same transformation is happening with text. AI can now write news articles, social media posts, personal messages, and academic essays in styles that are almost impossible to distinguish from human writing — and it can produce them at enormous speed and scale. This means that a single person could, if they wanted to, flood the internet with thousands of realistic-seeming fake stories in a single afternoon. The potential for this to be used to spread false information, manipulate opinions, or cause harm to real people's reputations is genuinely serious.

Here is the important counterpoint: knowing these risks does not have to make you suspicious of everything or frightened of the internet. The vast majority of what you see online is still real. Most AI-generated images are created for entirely innocent purposes — art, entertainment, illustration. The skill you are developing in this lesson is not paranoia; it is discernment. Discernment means the ability to tell the difference, to look carefully, to apply a few simple checks before you react, believe, or share something. It is a skill that is more valuable now than it has ever been in human history.

Think of yourself as a detective. A good detective does not assume everything is a crime — they just have a trained eye that notices things that other people walk past. They ask questions: Does this make sense? Who benefits from this being believed? Where did this actually come from? What do I actually know for certain? By the end of this lesson, you will have a detective's eye for AI-generated content — and that skill will protect you and the people you share information with for the rest of your life.

---

## Key Concepts

- **AI-generated image:** A photograph or illustration created entirely by AI, without any real camera, real person, or real event involved.
- **Deepfake:** An AI-generated video or audio recording that realistically shows a real person saying or doing something they never actually did.
- **Verification habit:** A regular practice of checking sources and evidence before believing or sharing content.
- **Misinformation:** False or inaccurate information spread without necessarily intending to deceive.
- **Disinformation:** False information spread deliberately, with the intent to deceive or cause harm.

---

## Fun Facts

- Researchers at MIT found that false news stories spread six times faster on social media than true ones — and AI tools that can generate convincing fake content are making this problem significantly worse.
- The word "deepfake" was coined in 2017, and within just a few years the technology became good enough that major governments around the world began passing laws specifically about AI-generated fake content.
- Some universities have started training medical students to spot AI-generated fake scientific images — because fake research images are starting to appear in real academic journals, threatening the trustworthiness of scientific knowledge.

## Try It — Become a Fake Detector

### Activity 1: Learn the Signs in AI Images (10 mins)

AI-generated images have telltale signs — if you know what to look for. Let us build your detective eye.

**The seven things to look for in a potentially AI-generated image:**

1. **Hands and fingers** — AI often gets hands wrong. Count the fingers. Are there too many? Do any look melted or twisted?
2. **Text in the image** — AI struggles with letters. Signs, labels, and writing in AI images often have garbled, nonsense letters.
3. **Eyes** — AI eyes can look slightly glazed, too perfect, or strangely shiny.
4. **Ears, teeth, and jewellery** — These often look odd, asymmetrical, or blurry at the edges.
5. **Hair** — Sometimes too perfect, sometimes weirdly merged with the background.
6. **Backgrounds** — Look at the edges of objects. Do they blend strangely into the background? Are there odd blurs or warped shapes?
7. **The "too perfect" feeling** — Real photos have flaws. Skin has pores and blemishes. Lighting is uneven. If a photo looks too flawless, that is a clue.

Now do an image search. Try searching for: **"AI generated portrait"** or **"AI generated landscape"** and look at several images.

Pick two images and write down which of the seven signs you can spot in each one. Be specific — for example: "In image 1, the woman has six fingers on her right hand and the sign behind her says 'STOPR' instead of a real word."

---

### Activity 2: Real or AI? The Text Challenge (10 mins)

AI-generated text can also have patterns that give it away. Here are some things to notice:

**Signs a piece of writing might be AI-generated:**
- It is very polished but somehow feels a bit flat or generic
- It uses phrases like "it is important to note that" or "in today's world" or "in conclusion" very often
- Every paragraph is about the same length
- It does not have personal opinions, hesitations, or specific personal examples
- It never makes small human errors (a small typo or informal phrasing that a real person might use)
- It covers a topic very broadly without ever going deep on one interesting detail

**Your challenge:**

Read these two short paragraphs and decide which one feels more human and which feels more AI. Write your reasoning.

---

**Paragraph A:**
"School lunches are an important part of a student's day. A nutritious meal can help improve concentration and support healthy development. Many schools are working to offer balanced options that include proteins, vegetables, and whole grains. It is important to note that students who eat well tend to perform better academically. In conclusion, school lunch programmes play a vital role in student wellbeing."

**Paragraph B:**
"Honestly, our school canteen is a bit of a lottery. Some days they do this amazing pasta bake and the queue goes all the way to the door. Other days it is just watery soup that smells like feet. I once complained about it to a teacher and she said 'well at least it is hot food' which did not really help. I think they should let students suggest the menu — we would definitely do better than feet soup."

---

Write down:
- Which paragraph do you think is AI-generated? What clues gave it away?
- Which paragraph sounds more like a real person? What makes it feel human?

Now open your AI tool and ask it to write a short paragraph about your favourite food. Read it back. Can you spot the AI patterns? What would you add to make it sound more like you specifically wrote it?

### Activity 3: Build Your Verification Habit (10 mins)

Knowing the signs is only half the skill. The other half is having a habit of checking before you believe or share something.

Here is a simple **3-step check** you can do before sharing anything you see online:

**Step 1: PAUSE**
Before reacting to an image or story, stop for 5 seconds. Ask yourself: "Does this seem too surprising, too perfect, or too outrageous to be true?"

**Step 2: LOOK CLOSER**
Check the image for the seven signs from Activity 1. Check the text for the AI patterns from Activity 2. Look for the original source — where did this first appear?

**Step 3: SEARCH IT**
Type the claim or a description of the image into a search engine. If it is a real news event, multiple trusted sources will have covered it. If you can only find it on one random website or social post, be suspicious.

Now, practise this routine with a scenario:

Imagine you see this post on a social media feed:

> *[IMAGE: A photograph of a purple elephant standing in the middle of a city street in London. The image looks like a real news photo.]*
> Caption: "Escaped zoo animal spotted near Big Ben today! Share if you saw it!"

Walk through the 3-step check:
1. PAUSE — Does this seem too surprising to be true?
2. LOOK CLOSER — What would you check in this image?
3. SEARCH IT — What search would you do to verify this?

Write down your full check for this scenario. What is your conclusion — real or fake?

---

### Activity 4: The Reverse Test (10 mins)

Now you are going to create something — and then try to detect it.

Ask the AI to write a short paragraph (5–6 sentences) about a recent school event or local news story as if it were a real news report. The AI does not know your local area, so it will make something up. That is the point.

After you get the paragraph, read it back and make a list of:
- Specific claims that could be checked (names, places, events, statistics)
- Phrases that sound like AI-generated text patterns
- Things that feel vague or too generic to be a real local news report

This activity flips the exercise: instead of detecting AI, you produce AI content and then investigate your own output. It will sharpen your eye for the exact things a real detective would look for.

**Write a one-sentence verdict:** "This paragraph reads like AI because..."

5. What is one thing you are going to do differently the next time you see a surprising or shocking image online?

## Level Up Challenge

Conduct a real-world fake detection investigation.

Over the next 24 hours (or during one online session with a trusted adult present), collect three examples of content you see online that you are not 100% sure about — images, claims, or stories that make you wonder "is this real?"

For each one, run through the full 3-step verification check. Write up your findings in a "case file" format:

```
Case #1
What I saw: _______________
Step 1 — Pause: My initial suspicion: _______________
Step 2 — Look closer: Signs I noticed: _______________
Step 3 — Searched: What I found: _______________
Verdict: Real / Fake / Unverified — because: _______________
```

At the end, write one paragraph: "Before this lesson, I would have treated this content by... Now I..."
