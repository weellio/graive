# Lesson Plan: AI in the News
### Builder Level (Ages 12–13) | Module 11 — Expanded

---

## About This Lesson

If you have spent any time reading news headlines in the past few years, you will have noticed that artificial intelligence appears constantly — and the coverage is rarely calm. One week AI is going to cure all disease and solve climate change. The next week it is going to destroy jobs, destabilise democracy, or develop consciousness and turn on humanity. The week after that, a tech company announces a "revolutionary breakthrough" that changes everything. These cycles of hype and panic repeat so frequently that it becomes almost impossible to know what is actually happening with AI.

Learning to read AI news critically is not a niche skill. It is essential for anyone who wants to participate in informed conversations about one of the most significant technological developments in human history. AI policy decisions — about regulation, education, healthcare, defence, and employment — are being made right now by governments and companies, and those decisions affect your generation more than any other. If you cannot evaluate AI claims critically, you cannot meaningfully participate in discussions about AI's direction.

The challenge is structural. Most AI journalism is written by generalist reporters who may not have deep technical knowledge of the subject. It is published by organisations with commercial incentives to attract clicks, which rewards dramatic headlines over nuanced reporting. It often relies on company press releases as primary sources — the equivalent of taking a company's word for how good its own products are. And the underlying technology changes quickly enough that context from three months ago may already be outdated.

Against this, there are excellent science and technology journalists doing rigorous work. There are academic researchers publishing peer-reviewed studies. There are independent analysts and think tanks producing measured assessments. The skill you are building in this lesson is knowing how to tell the difference — and knowing which questions to ask regardless of the source.

There is also an important meta-level here: some of the most influential people shaping public perception of AI are those with the most to gain from specific narratives. Tech companies benefit from "AI is revolutionary" stories because they drive investment and adoption. Certain researchers benefit from "AI is existentially dangerous" stories because they drive funding and regulatory attention toward their specific concerns. This does not mean these claims are wrong — it means you should know who is making them and what they stand to gain before deciding how much weight to give them.

## Key Concepts

- **Source credibility:** A way of evaluating how trustworthy a piece of information is based on who produced it, their expertise, their track record, and their potential conflicts of interest.
- **Hype cycle:** A predictable pattern in technology coverage where initial excitement leads to exaggerated claims, followed by disappointment, followed (sometimes) by more realistic assessment of actual capability.
- **Peer-reviewed research:** Scientific findings that have been evaluated and verified by independent experts in the field before publication — a higher standard of evidence than a press release or a news article.
- **Vague attribution:** A journalistic practice of citing "experts" or "scientists" without naming specific people or studies — a red flag that the claim may not be well-evidenced.
- **Conflict of interest:** A situation where a source of information has a financial or strategic reason to want people to believe something — which should be disclosed and considered when evaluating their claims.
- **Red/Amber/Green rating:** A simple framework for quickly categorising the credibility and balance of a news article about AI.

---

## How It Works

AI news is produced within an ecosystem with specific incentive structures that you need to understand to read it critically.

**Companies** issue press releases describing their products and research in the most favourable terms possible. These are not neutral — they are marketing. When a news article says "AI Company X claims its model can...", the claim originated with a company that has a financial interest in you believing it.

**Journalists** at general news outlets face pressure to produce clickable content. A headline reading "New AI model achieves marginal improvement on benchmark" will get far fewer clicks than "AI surpasses human performance for the first time." This creates structural pressure toward exaggeration.

**Academic researchers** publish peer-reviewed work that is more carefully evaluated but moves slowly, is technical and hard to translate for general audiences, and often requires a subscription to access.

**Tech commentators and influencers** produce fast, accessible takes but often lack deep technical knowledge and have their own audiences and brand interests to maintain.

Understanding who produced a piece of AI content — and what their incentive structure is — is the single most important habit in reading AI news critically.

## Real World Examples

**1. The GPT-4 announcement and capability claims (2023)**
When OpenAI released GPT-4, they published a technical report including benchmark scores. News coverage translated these scores into dramatic claims about AI "passing the bar exam" and "scoring in the 90th percentile" of human performance. Researchers pointed out that these comparisons were misleading — the AI was tested only on multiple-choice questions, not on the actual professional judgment tasks lawyers perform. This is a clear example of a "breakthrough announcement" story (Type 1) requiring critical analysis.

**2. AI job prediction headlines**
Numerous studies and reports in 2023–2024 predicted that AI would "replace" 20–40% of jobs within various timeframes. These headlines generated significant anxiety. Critical readers noted that the studies came mostly from consulting firms selling AI transformation services (conflict of interest), that "replace" often meant "change some tasks in" rather than eliminate entirely, and that historical predictions about automation replacing jobs have consistently overestimated the speed of change while underestimating how new jobs emerge. This is a Type 2 story requiring careful reading.

**3. The Google "AI sentience" story (2022)**
A Google engineer named Blake Lemoine publicly claimed that LaMDA, Google's AI system, had become sentient and had feelings. The story generated enormous media coverage. Almost all AI researchers and ethicists disagreed strongly — they argued Lemoine had anthropomorphised the AI's outputs, confusing fluent human-like language with actual consciousness. Google placed Lemoine on leave and later dismissed him. This is a Type 3 story — a capability exaggeration that requires understanding what AI actually does versus what it seems to do.

## Try It — Read Like a Journalist

### Activity 1: The Hype-O-Meter (15 mins)

AI news tends to cluster around a few repeating story types. Learning to recognise these types helps you instantly calibrate how seriously to take them.

Here are the five most common AI story types:

**Type 1 — The Breakthrough Announcement**
"AI beats human experts at X for the first time!"
*What to ask:* What is the actual test it passed? Is that test a fair measure of real-world ability? Has the result been independently verified?

**Type 2 — The Job Fear Story**
"AI will replace 40% of jobs within 10 years"
*What to ask:* Who produced this prediction — a tech company, a consultancy, a researcher? What is their track record on previous predictions? What does "replace" mean — fully, or change the role?

**Type 3 — The Capability Exaggeration**
"AI develops feelings / becomes conscious / understands humans"
*What to ask:* Is this peer-reviewed science or a press release? What does "understands" actually mean in technical terms? Who gains from this claim?

**Type 4 — The Doom Story**
"AI will destroy democracy / cause mass unemployment / end civilisation"
*What to ask:* Is this a specific, near-term risk with evidence, or a distant hypothetical? Who is making the claim — what is their background and agenda?

**Type 5 — The Miracle Solution**
"AI will cure cancer / solve climate change / end poverty"
*What to ask:* What specific problem is it solving, and what stage is the research at? Is this peer-reviewed? Has it been tested in the real world?

**Your task:** Go to Google News and search "artificial intelligence" or "AI". Find five recent headlines. For each one:
1. Write the headline
2. Identify which of the five story types it is (or create a new type if none fit)
3. Write one question you would want answered before you believed the story

### Activity 2: Anatomy of an AI Article (15 mins)

Now let us go deeper on one article. Pick the most interesting headline from your list in Activity 1 and open the full article. Read it carefully.

Then fill in this analysis framework:

**Source Analysis:**
- Who published this? (Newspaper, tech blog, company PR, academic journal)
- When was it published?
- Who wrote it — is the author named? Do they have expertise in AI?

**Claim Analysis:**
- What is the central claim of the article? (One sentence)
- What evidence is given to support it?
- Is the evidence from a primary source (original research) or secondary (someone reporting on someone else's claim)?

**Language Analysis:**
Look for these "hype words" and count how many appear:
- Revolutionary / game-changing / breakthrough
- Could / might / may / potentially (hedge words that sound dramatic but commit to nothing)
- Experts say / scientists claim (vague attribution — which experts?)
- According to [company name] (the company is not an independent source)

**Counter-Analysis:**
- What is the strongest argument against the article's claim?
- Is anyone quoted in the article who disagrees or adds caution? (If not, that is itself suspicious.)
- What would this story look like if the opposite were true?

After completing the framework, rate the article:
- **Red:** This is hype, fear-mongering, or PR dressed as news
- **Amber:** Some substance, but significant parts are exaggerated or one-sided
- **Green:** Solid reporting with real evidence and multiple perspectives

---

### Activity 3: Write the Balanced Version (10 mins)

Pick one of the articles you have analysed (or the headline from Activity 2 if you only did one deep-read).

Your task: Write a **balanced, 150-word summary** of what the article is actually saying — stripping out the hype and adding the caveats the journalist left out.

Your summary must include:
- The central claim (what happened or what was found)
- The actual evidence (what was tested, by whom, at what scale)
- At least one limitation or reason to be cautious
- One question that remains unanswered

You are not writing a negative or positive spin — you are writing a neutral, accurate summary. Read it back: could a thoughtful adult trust this as a fair account of the story?

Bonus: Use your AI tool to help. Ask it:
> "Here is a news article about AI. [Paste the article or a summary.] What are the limitations or caveats that this article downplays or leaves out? What questions should a critical reader ask?"

Compare what the AI identifies with what you found yourself. Did it spot anything you missed?

### Activity 4: Follow the Money (10 mins) — NEW

This activity asks you to investigate the financial interests behind an AI story.

Pick any AI company that appears in the news this week — any announcement, product launch, or study. Then research:

1. **Who funded it?** — Who paid for the research or built the product? What are their financial interests?
2. **Who benefits from you believing this?** — If this story is true and widely believed, who gains financially or in reputation?
3. **Who loses?** — Are there competitors, workers, or industries who would be worse off if this story is true? Are they quoted in the article?
4. **Is there an independent voice?** — Is anyone quoted who has no financial stake in the outcome?

Write a short "conflict of interest audit" for the story: a paragraph describing the financial landscape around this claim and what a critical reader should know about it.

This skill transfers far beyond AI news — it applies to pharmaceutical research, nutritional studies, energy policy, and almost any area where economic interests intersect with scientific claims.

---

### Activity 5: Write Two Headlines for the Same Story (10 mins) — NEW

This activity demonstrates how framing shapes perception.

Find one substantive AI news story this week. Read it carefully and understand what it is actually reporting.

Now write two different headlines for it:
- **Version A:** The most sensationalist, clickbait version — designed to maximise emotional reaction and clicks. (You can be dramatic, slightly misleading, or provocative — but based on something in the actual story.)
- **Version B:** The most neutral, accurate version — designed to tell a reader exactly what happened without triggering any emotional reaction.

Compare the two headlines. Write a reflection:
1. How different are they? Which is closer to the actual headline the article used?
2. If you only read headline Version A, what would you think happened? How does that compare to reality?
3. Why might a news organisation choose Version A over Version B?
4. What does this exercise tell you about the relationship between headlines and truth?

## Design It!

Create a one-page **"AI News Checklist"** poster — something you could stick next to your desk as a reminder for every time you read an AI news story.

Include:
- A title ("How to Read AI News Without Being Fooled" or your own title)
- The five story types from Activity 1 (in brief)
- Five quick questions to ask about any AI headline
- A simple 3-colour rating system (Red/Amber/Green like the one above)
- A short reminder statement at the bottom (your own words)

Make it visually clear and easy to scan — someone should be able to use it in under 60 seconds.

## Reflect
Answer these questions out loud or write them down:

1. After this lesson, do you think you will read AI news differently? What specifically will you do that you did not do before?
2. Is there a difference between a journalist writing an exaggerated headline and an AI company publishing a press release claiming their product is "revolutionary"? Who has more responsibility to be accurate?
3. You used AI to help analyse a news article about AI. Is there anything ironic or problematic about that? What are the limits of using AI to fact-check claims about AI?
4. If most people cannot critically evaluate AI news, who does that benefit? Does the inability to read AI news critically give power to specific groups?
5. If you wanted to stay well-informed about AI without being misled by hype, what specific sources, habits, or practices would you develop? What would your ideal "AI news diet" look like?
