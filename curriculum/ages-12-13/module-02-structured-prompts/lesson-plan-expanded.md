# Lesson Plan: Your First Structured Prompt
### Builder Level (Ages 12–13) | Module 02 — Expanded

---

## About This Lesson

If you have ever typed a question into an AI tool and been disappointed by a vague, generic, or completely wrong answer, the problem almost certainly was not the AI — it was the prompt. The quality of what you get out of an AI is almost entirely determined by what you put in. This is both frustrating (it means you have to think harder upfront) and empowering (it means you are in control of the result).

Think of it this way: imagine you need help from a brilliant expert who knows a huge amount about almost everything. But this expert has never met you, has no idea what you already know, does not know your situation, and does not know in what format you want the information. If you walk up to that expert and say "tell me about the French Revolution," you will get a lecture pitched at some generic imagined audience that may not help you at all. But if you say "I am a 12-year-old studying the causes of the French Revolution for a history essay. I already know about the financial crisis and food shortages. I am confused about why the king was so unpopular. Can you explain that in plain language and give me two points I could use in my essay?" — now you have given the expert everything they need to actually help you.

That is what a structured prompt does. The RCTFC framework you will learn in this module — Role, Context, Task, Format, Constraints — is essentially a checklist for giving the AI the five pieces of information it most often lacks. Each element narrows the space of possible responses, guiding the AI from "anything that might fit" to "exactly what this person actually needs."

Prompt engineering — the skill of writing effective AI prompts — is already a valued professional skill. Businesses pay for people who can reliably get useful outputs from AI tools. Researchers use it to accelerate their work. Writers use it to push through creative blocks. But more importantly for you right now, it is a thinking skill. Writing a good prompt forces you to be clear about what you actually need, what you already know, and who you are communicating with — skills that transfer directly into writing, communication, and problem-solving in every area of life.

You will do a before-and-after experiment in this lesson that should make the difference completely visible. Most students are genuinely surprised by how large the gap is between a casual prompt and a structured one. After this lesson, you will never look at an AI conversation quite the same way.

---

## Key Concepts

- **RCTFC framework:** A five-element structure for AI prompts — Role, Context, Task, Format, Constraints — that significantly improves output quality.
- **Prompt engineering:** The skill of crafting inputs to AI systems in ways that reliably produce useful, accurate, and well-formatted outputs.
- **Iteration:** The process of refining an AI response through follow-up messages, treating the conversation as a back-and-forth rather than a single exchange.
- **Role prompting:** Telling the AI which persona or expert role to adopt, which changes the vocabulary, assumptions, and tone of its responses.
- **Constraints:** Instructions about what to avoid, what limits to stay within, or what requirements must be met — often the most overlooked part of a prompt.
- **Template:** A reusable prompt with placeholder brackets, allowing the same structure to be applied quickly to different topics or situations.

---

## How It Works

When you send a message to an AI, it processes your entire input and generates a probability distribution over possible responses. Your prompt acts as a steering signal — every word nudges the probabilities in a particular direction.

When you say "explain the French Revolution," the probability distribution is very wide. The AI must guess: Am I explaining to a child or an adult? Should I cover everything or focus on one aspect? Should this be casual or formal? A long essay or a short summary? Without information, it defaults to a kind of average — and averages are rarely exactly what anyone needs.

Adding role information ("you are a patient history tutor for a 12-year-old") immediately narrows the tone and vocabulary distribution. Adding context ("I already understand the financial crisis") tells it what to skip. Adding format ("give me bullet points") specifies the output structure. Each RCTFC element removes ambiguity, bringing the generated response closer to what you actually want.

The iteration step is equally important. A single prompt is rarely perfect. Real prompt engineering is a conversation — you push the AI in a direction, see what you get, and then make a targeted adjustment. Professionals who work with AI daily describe this as a collaboration: you bring the goal and the judgment, the AI brings the pattern-matching horsepower.

```

│  R — ROLE        Who should the AI be?       │
│  C — CONTEXT     What's the background?      │
│  T — TASK        What exactly do you need?   │
```

## Real World Examples

**1. GitHub Copilot and developer prompts**
Professional software developers use tools like GitHub Copilot that generate code based on natural language descriptions. The developers who get the best results write structured descriptions — specifying the language, the function's purpose, the inputs and outputs, and any constraints (like "do not use external libraries"). This is RCTFC applied to code.

**2. Content teams at social media companies**
Social media managers at brands use AI to draft posts, responses, and captions. The ones that work best include role ("write as a friendly brand voice"), context ("this is responding to a complaint about a delayed order"), task ("write an apology"), format ("under 280 characters"), and constraints ("do not offer refunds in this message, only acknowledge the issue").

**3. AI tutoring platforms like Khan Academy's Khanmigo**
Khan Academy's AI tutor is designed around structured prompts in the background — it is given a role (tutor who asks questions rather than gives answers), constraints (do not just give the student the answer), and context (the student's age and subject). The structured prompt runs silently so that students get a more useful interaction than they would from a raw AI conversation.

## Try It — The Before/After Experiment

### Step 1: Pick Your Topic (5 mins)

Choose ONE thing you actually want help with. Real is better than made-up. Ideas:
- Explaining a school topic you're studying
- Writing a message to someone
- Getting ideas for a creative project
- Understanding something from the news
- Planning something (an event, a project, a video)

Write your topic down: _______________________

---

### Step 2: Write the "Bad" Prompt (5 mins)

Write the simplest, most direct version of your request — just instinct, no structure.

Example: *"Explain the French Revolution"* or *"Help me apologise to my mum"*

Your bad prompt: _______________________

Paste it into the AI. Copy the output into your notebook and label it **Output A**.

---

### Step 3: Build Your RCTFC Prompt (15 mins)

Now build a structured version using the framework. Fill in each section:

| Element | Your Answer |
|---------|------------|
| **R** — Role | The AI should act as a... |
| **C** — Context | The situation is... / This is for... / My background is... |
| **T** — Task | I need it to... specifically... |
| **F** — Format | Present it as... (bullet points / short paragraphs / numbered steps / table) |
| **C** — Constraints | Don't use... / Keep it under... / Avoid... |

Now write it as one connected prompt. You can use all the elements in any order that flows naturally — you don't have to label them.

**Your RCTFC prompt:**

```
[write yours here]
```

Paste it into the AI. Copy the output and label it **Output B**.

---

### Step 4: Compare (10 mins)

Look at Output A vs Output B side by side.

Answer these questions:
1. Which is more useful? Why?
2. Which sounds more like what you actually wanted?
3. What single element of RCTFC made the biggest difference?
4. What would you change in your RCTFC prompt to make it even better?

---

### Step 5: Iterate (5 mins)

Take Output B and improve it with a follow-up message. Don't start over — just respond to the AI with one refinement.

Examples:
- "Make it shorter and more casual"
- "Add an example to the second point"
- "Change the tone — it sounds too formal"
- "Give me a version for someone who already knows the basics"

Copy this final output and label it **Output C**.

**Output C should be the best one. If it's not, try one more iteration.**

---

### Step 6: The Role Swap Test (10 mins) — NEW

This activity isolates the power of the Role element specifically.

Take one of your prompts from earlier (Output B works well). Now run it three more times, changing ONLY the role — keeping everything else identical:

- Version 1: Role = "a strict and formal academic professor"
- Version 2: Role = "a fun and energetic teacher who uses lots of analogies"
- Version 3: Role = "a peer your own age explaining it to a friend"

Compare all three outputs. Write down:
1. How different were the three versions? Rate the difference 1–10.
2. Which version was most useful for your actual purpose?
3. What does this tell you about how much the role instruction shapes the entire response?

---

### Step 7: Constraint Stress-Test (10 mins) — NEW

Most people underuse constraints. This activity shows you how powerful they are.

Take your original topic and write a prompt that deliberately has a very strange or strict constraint — something that challenges the AI to work differently than it normally would. Examples:

- "Explain [your topic] without using any words over two syllables"
- "Explain [your topic] as if it happened in medieval times"
- "Give me a summary of [your topic] in exactly 50 words, no more, no less"
- "Explain [your topic] using only questions — no statements allowed"

Try two constraint variations. Write down:
- Did the AI follow the constraint accurately?
- Did the unusual constraint produce anything surprising or interesting?
- What does this tell you about how constraints steer the AI's output?

## Level Up — Build a Reusable Prompt Template

Create a prompt template you could use again and again for a task you do regularly. Ideas:
- A template for summarising something you've read
- A template for getting feedback on your writing
- A template for brainstorming ideas on any topic
- A template for explaining a concept you're struggling with

Use this structure:

```
You are [ROLE].
I need help with [GENERAL TASK TYPE].
Here is the specific situation: [CONTEXT PLACEHOLDER — you'll fill this in each time].
Please [SPECIFIC ACTION] and present it as [FORMAT].
Avoid [CONSTRAINTS].
```

Test your template on two different topics to make sure it works generally, not just for one thing. Then write a short note explaining what makes a good template general-purpose versus too specific to one situation.

## Reflect

2. **When I compared Output A and Output B, the most surprising difference was...** What does that tell you about how AI works?

3. **Iteration felt [easy/hard/weird] because...** How do you think you'll use back-and-forth prompting in real life?

4. **If prompting is a skill, can it be learned and improved?** What would deliberate practice look like for this skill?

5. **Is there a risk that very structured prompts make AI responses feel less natural or creative?** How would you balance structure with openness?
