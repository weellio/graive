## CONCEPT: The RCTFC Framework (0:45–3:30)

**[HOST, whiteboard-style animation or graphics]**

"Okay so why does the second prompt work so much better?

Because AI is a pattern machine — we learned that last time. And the more clearly you describe the pattern you want, the better the output.

Think of writing a prompt like giving directions to someone who has never been to your house. If you say 'just come over' — good luck to them. If you give them the address, the street, what the house looks like, and where to park — they'll get there.

A good prompt has five ingredients. I call it the RCTFC framework:

**[ANIMATED CARD — reveal one letter at a time]**

```
R — Role       → Who should the AI be?
C — Context    → What's the situation / background?
T — Task       → What do you actually want it to do?
F — Format     → How should it present the answer?
C — Constraints → What should it avoid or stay within?
```

Let me break each one down."

---

**[HOST, talking through each element with on-screen examples]**

**R — Role**
"Tell the AI who to *be*. This sounds weird but it works, because it activates different patterns.

There's a big difference between:
- 'Explain photosynthesis'
- 'You are a friendly science teacher. Explain photosynthesis to a 12-year-old who has never heard of it before.'

The second one tells AI what voice to use, what level to pitch at, and what style to write in."

**C — Context**
"Give it background. What's the situation? Who is this for? Why do you need it?

Example: 'I have a presentation tomorrow about climate change for my class. They're 13 and they already know what CO2 is but they don't know how the greenhouse effect works.'

Now the AI knows: audience, existing knowledge, urgency. It can actually help."

**T — Task**
"Be specific about what you want. Not 'help me' — what *exactly* do you need?

- Too vague: 'Write something about climate change'
- Better: 'Write a 3-paragraph explanation of the greenhouse effect'
- Best: 'Write a 3-paragraph explanation of the greenhouse effect. Start with an analogy, then explain the science, then give one real-world impact.'"

**F — Format**
"Tell it how you want the answer presented. Bullet points? Numbered list? Short paragraphs? A table?

This is massively underused. If you want something you can read quickly, say 'use bullet points.' If you want to copy it into a document, say 'write it in full sentences.'"

**C — Constraints**
"Tell it what NOT to do. This is powerful.
- 'Don't use jargon'
- 'Keep it under 200 words'
- 'Don't include statistics'
- 'Write in a casual, friendly tone, not formal'"

## DEMO (3:30–6:00)

**[HOST, screen share, live typing into AI]**

"Let's build a real prompt together. I want AI to help me write a message to a friend explaining why I can't make their birthday party.

**Bad version:**
> 'Help me cancel on my friend's birthday'

**[TYPE IT IN — show mediocre output]**

Okay, it gives something. But it's generic. It doesn't sound like me. It's a bit formal. Not great.

Now let's use RCTFC.

**Good version:**
> You are a helpful writing assistant (R). I'm a 13-year-old and I need to cancel on my friend's birthday party because I have a family thing I forgot about. We've been friends for 3 years. (C). Write a text message I can send to apologise and reschedule (T). Keep it short — 3-4 sentences max, like a real text message (F). Don't make it sound formal or robotic. Don't use the word 'regrettably' or 'apologise' — I'd never say those in a text (C).

**[TYPE IT IN — show great output]**

Same question. Completely different result. That sounds like something a real person would actually send.

Now here's the key move: **iterate**. The first response is almost never perfect.

Watch:"

**[TYPE: 'Make it sound a little more casual and add that you're going to bring them something next week']**

"See how I'm just having a conversation with it? Refining it? That's the real skill — not writing one perfect prompt, but knowing how to *guide* it."

## CHALLENGE (6:00–7:00)

**[HOST, direct to camera]**

"Your challenge: pick something you actually need help with right now. Doesn't have to be schoolwork — could be:
- Writing a message to someone
- Getting ideas for a project
- Explaining something you're learning
- Getting feedback on something you made

Write your prompt using RCTFC. All five ingredients.

Then try the 'bad' version first — just ask without structure. Then try your RCTFC version. Screenshot or copy both outputs and compare them.

I promise you will never go back to vague prompts after you see the difference."
