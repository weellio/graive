# Lesson Plan: Algorithms Around You
### Builder Level (Ages 12–13) | Module 03

---

## Objective
By the end of this lesson, you'll be able to identify when an algorithm is influencing what you see, and use AI tools to deliberately search outside your default "bubble."

---

## What You'll Need
- Your main social media / video platform (YouTube, TikTok, Instagram, etc.)
- Access to AI tool (ChatGPT or Claude)
- Notebook or Google Doc
- 48-hour observation window + 30 mins for activities

---

## Watch First
Watch **Module 03: Algorithms Around You**.

Key ideas:
- Algorithms optimise for engagement, not truth or balance
- Filter bubbles narrow what you see over time
- Outrage and strong emotion get more engagement than calm info

---

## Part 1: The 48-Hour Algorithm Watch (ongoing)

Before doing the rest of this lesson, start your observation first.

Choose ONE platform you use regularly. For 48 hours, log your observations in a notebook. You don't have to use the platform more than normal — just pay attention when you do.

Each time you notice something, note:

| Observation | Why do you think it was shown to you? | Did it make you feel something? |
|------------|--------------------------------------|--------------------------------|
| Example: Video about [topic] | I watched 3 similar videos yesterday | Yes — excited / annoyed / sad |
| | | |
| | | |
| | | |

Try to log at least **5 observations** over the 48 hours.

---

## Part 2: The Other Side Experiment (20 mins)

After your 48-hour observation, do this activity.

Think about a topic your feed shows you a lot — a sport, a type of music, a topic from the news, a hobby, a political issue (if relevant).

Now ask an AI to show you the *other side* or a *different angle*:

**Prompt template:**
```
I usually see a lot of content about [TOPIC] that focuses on [YOUR USUAL ANGLE].
Show me 5 interesting perspectives, arguments, or facts about [TOPIC] that I probably
haven't come across before. Include at least one that would challenge my current view.
Present each as a 2-3 sentence summary.
```

Write down:
1. Did any of the perspectives genuinely surprise you?
2. Did any of them make you uncomfortable? Why?
3. Does knowing these exist change how you think about your feed?

---

## Part 3: Build a Better Search (15 mins)

Algorithms give you what's popular. But sometimes you need what's *accurate* or *balanced*.

Use AI as a research starting point instead of social media. Pick a topic from the news (something you've seen recently) and run this prompt:

```
I want to understand [NEWS TOPIC] properly.
Give me:
1. A 3-sentence neutral summary of what's happening
2. Two different perspectives people have on it (one per side)
3. Two things that both sides agree on
4. One question that remains genuinely unresolved

Don't take sides. I'm trying to understand, not confirm what I already think.
```

Compare this to what you've seen in your social media feed about the same topic. Are they the same? Different? What's missing from each?

---

## Level Up — Design a "Bubble-Breaker" Habit

Design a personal routine for getting outside your filter bubble. This should be realistic — something you'd actually do once a week.

Answer these:
1. What platform am I most at risk of living in a bubble on?
2. What's one thing I could do once a week to see a different perspective?
3. How could I use AI to help with this?

Write it as a short personal commitment:
> "Once a week, I will [action] to make sure I'm not only seeing [type of content]. I'll use AI to [specific use]."

---

## Reflect

1. Before this module, did you think about why you were being shown specific content? Has that changed?

2. Is a filter bubble always bad? Can it ever be useful? Give an example of each.

3. What's the difference between an algorithm choosing what you see and *you* choosing what you see? Does the difference matter?
