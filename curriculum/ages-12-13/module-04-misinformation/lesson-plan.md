# Lesson Plan: Misinformation in the AI Age
### Builder Level (Ages 12–13) | Module 04

---

## Objective
By the end of this lesson, you can apply the STOP framework to evaluate suspicious content and use AI as a verification tool.

---

## What You'll Need
- Access to AI tool (ChatGPT or Claude)
- Access to a search engine
- Notebook or Google Doc
- 40–50 minutes

---

## Watch First
Watch **Module 04: Misinformation in the AI Age**.

Key framework: **STOP** — Source, Tone, Other sources, Proof

---

## Try It — Fact Check a Real Claim

### Step 1: Find a Claim to Check (10 mins)

Go to a social media platform, news aggregator, or Google News. Find ONE claim that seems interesting or surprising. It could be:

- A statistic ("Did you know 90% of people...")
- A news headline about something dramatic or unexpected
- A quote attributed to a famous person
- A health/science claim ("Study finds...")

Copy the claim into your notebook exactly as you found it. Also note where you found it (which website/platform).

**Do not choose something obviously fake or satirical.** Pick something that genuinely made you think "hm, is that true?"

---

### Step 2: Run the STOP Check (15 mins)

Go through each letter:

**S — Source**
- Who published this originally?
- Is this a known publication with a track record?
- When was the site/account created?
- Does it have an About page? Who runs it?

Notes: _______________________

**T — Tone**
- Does it make you feel angry, scared, or outraged?
- Does it use emotionally charged language?
- Is it trying to persuade you strongly rather than inform you?
- How is that making you feel right now?

Notes: _______________________

**O — Other Sources**
- Can you find this story or claim on at least 2 other sources?
- Are those sources independent (different organisations, not just re-posting each other)?
- What do the most trusted news sources you know say about this topic?

Notes: _______________________

**P — Proof**
- Is there a direct link to the original study, video, document, or event?
- If it's a quote — can you find the original video or transcript?
- If it's a statistic — where did that number come from?

Notes: _______________________

**Your STOP verdict:** TRUE / PROBABLY TRUE / UNCERTAIN / PROBABLY FALSE / FALSE

---

### Step 3: Use AI to Verify (10 mins)

Now use an AI tool to help you dig deeper. Use this prompt:

```
I'm fact-checking a claim. The claim is:
"[PASTE YOUR CLAIM HERE]"

Please help me:
1. Identify what I would need to verify this claim
2. List any red flags in how the claim is worded
3. Tell me what reliable sources would say about this topic (general knowledge — you may not know this specific story)
4. Suggest 2–3 search queries I could use to investigate further
```

Write down what the AI tells you. Then try the suggested search queries.

**Did the AI help you reach a better conclusion? Where was it useful? Where did it fall short?**

---

### Step 4: Write Your Verdict (5 mins)

Write a short fact-check summary (4–6 sentences):
- What the claim was
- Where it came from
- What you found when you checked it
- Your conclusion (true, false, misleading, unverifiable)
- What someone should know before sharing this

---

## Level Up — Spot the Fake

This is a creative challenge. Use an AI tool to generate three short news-style paragraphs about made-up events. Make them *plausible-sounding* (not obviously ridiculous).

Then share them with a family member or friend and ask them to guess which is real.

After the experiment, discuss: what made the fake ones seem believable? What gave them away?

**Reflection: What did building fake content teach you about detecting it?**

Note: The point of this exercise is understanding *how* misinformation works so you can spot it — not to deceive anyone for real.

---

## Reflect

1. Before this module, did you ever share something online without checking if it was true? No judgement — just honest reflection.

2. The STOP framework takes maybe 5 minutes. What stops people from using it before sharing? What would make it easier?

3. Is it worse to accidentally share something false, or to never share anything in case it's wrong? Where's the line?
