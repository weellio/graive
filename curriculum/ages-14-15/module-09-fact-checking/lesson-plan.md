## Think Deeper — Trust, But Verify

### Activity 1: Understanding Why AI Hallucinates (10 mins)

Before you can catch AI errors, you need to understand why they happen.

AI language models generate text by predicting what word is most likely to come next, based on patterns in training data. They do not look things up in a database of verified facts. They predict plausible-sounding text.

This means:
- AI can generate entirely fake citations (books, papers, journal articles) that sound completely real but do not exist
- AI can get statistics slightly wrong — inverting numbers, confusing similar facts, or combining two real facts into one incorrect composite
- AI can state outdated information confidently because its training data has a cutoff date
- AI can fabricate quotes from real people that they never actually said
- AI can get names and dates slightly wrong — often close enough to be hard to catch without checking

**The "Confident Wrong" test:**

Open your AI tool and ask it these questions. For each answer, try to verify it using a quick search:

1. "What is the population of [your country]?" — Check against an official government census or statistics website
2. "Who won [a specific award, e.g. the Booker Prize] in [year approximately 5–10 years ago]?" — Check the official award website
3. "What did [a historical figure] say about [a topic they actually wrote about]?" — Look for the original source

In at least one of these cases, the AI may give you a subtly wrong answer, a rounded figure, or an invention. Document what you found.

Write down: What type of error did you find (wrong number, fake quote, outdated information, something else)? How would you have known it was wrong without checking?

### Activity 2: Build the Verification Workflow (20 mins)

Here is a systematic verification workflow. You are going to learn it by using it.

**The 5-Step AI Verification Workflow:**

**Step 1 — Identify the claim type.**
Is it a statistic? A historical fact? A quote? A scientific claim? A legal claim? Different claim types require different verification sources.

**Step 2 — Ask for the source.**
Ask the AI: "What is the source for that claim?" or "Can you give me the original paper/book/report this comes from?"
Warning: AI may invent a source. The source it gives you is itself unverified — treat it as a lead to follow, not a confirmation.

**Step 3 — Go to primary sources.**
Primary sources are the original: the original research paper, the official government report, the historical document, the published book. Secondary sources (news articles, Wikipedia, explainer sites) can point you toward primary sources but are not final verification themselves.

For statistics: Look for the original dataset or report (World Bank, ONS, WHO, official national statistics)
For scientific claims: Look for the original peer-reviewed paper (Google Scholar, PubMed)
For historical facts: Look for the primary historical document or a major academic reference work
For quotes: Find the original speech, letter, book, or interview where the quote appears

**Step 4 — Cross-reference with two independent sources.**
One source confirming something is not enough — especially for important claims. Two independent, reputable sources that agree (and whose agreement is not because one simply copied the other) gives you much stronger confidence.

**Step 5 — Note the date.**
Even verified information may be outdated. Always note when the source was published and whether the claim might have changed since then.

**Now practise:**

Choose one of these topic areas and ask AI to give you a detailed summary with at least 5 specific facts (statistics, names, dates, claims):
- Climate change statistics
- The history of the internet
- The science of sleep
- The effects of social media on teenagers
- Space exploration milestones

Take those 5 specific facts and run them through the 5-step workflow. Document each step for each fact.

At the end, create a fact-check table:

| Claim from AI | Verified? | Source used | Accuracy |
|--------------|-----------|-------------|---------|
| Claim 1 | Yes/No/Partial | [Source name & URL] | Correct / Wrong / Outdated |
| Claim 2 | | | |
...

### Activity 3: The High-Stakes Check (15 mins)

Some situations require more rigorous fact-checking than others. Let us understand the stakes.

**Low stakes:** Using AI to suggest birthday party ideas, write a funny caption, help brainstorm a creative project. If something is wrong, the consequence is minor.

**Medium stakes:** Using AI to help with a school essay, summarise a historical event, explain a scientific concept. If something is wrong, you might submit incorrect work or form a false belief.

**High stakes:** Using AI to understand medical information, legal rights, financial decisions, personal safety. If something is wrong, the consequences could be serious.

**Your task — Map your AI use to the stakes:**

Think about the last week or two. Write down five times you have used AI (or would realistically use AI). For each one:
1. What was the task?
2. What is the stakes level (Low / Medium / High)?
3. Did you (or would you) fact-check? With what level of rigour?

Then write a short personal policy: "I will always fully verify AI outputs when [conditions]. I will do a quick spot-check when [conditions]. I will use AI with minimal checking only when [conditions]."

**Bonus challenge:** Ask AI to intentionally give you a mix of correct and incorrect facts about a topic you know well. (Ask: "Tell me five facts about [topic you know], but include one false fact mixed in — I want to practise spotting AI errors. Do not tell me which one is false.") Then try to identify the false fact using your workflow.

## Reflect
Answer these questions out loud or write them down:

1. If AI sometimes confidently states wrong information and you cannot always tell, does that make AI a reliable tool for serious research? How should students and journalists adjust their practice to account for this?
2. Wikipedia is often dismissed as unreliable — but well-maintained Wikipedia articles cite primary sources throughout. How might Wikipedia be useful in your verification workflow even if it is not itself a final source?
3. The skill of going to primary sources is valuable regardless of AI. How would this workflow change how you approach research for a school essay, even if you were not using AI at all?
