# Video Script: Jobs & Automation
### Thinker Level (Ages 14–15) | Module 04 | ~10 minutes

---

## HOOK (0:00–0:50)

**[HOST on camera]**

"People keep asking: will AI take my job?

And here's the honest answer: for many jobs, yes, some tasks will be automated. But the question is more complicated than 'will AI replace humans.'

The better questions are: *which parts* of which jobs are automatable? What does that mean for the people doing those jobs? And what do you actually learn now — given that the job market in 2030, 2035, 2040 will look very different from today?

Let's actually answer those questions instead of just being vague about it."

**[TITLE CARD: "Jobs & Automation"]**

---

## THE TASK FRAMEWORK (0:50–3:30)

**[HOST, animated breakdown]**

"The key insight that most takes on this topic miss: **it's not about jobs, it's about tasks.**

Most jobs are a bundle of different tasks. Some of those tasks are highly automatable. Others are much harder for AI to replicate.

Economists use a simple 2x2 framework:

**[ANIMATED 2x2 matrix]**

- **Routine cognitive tasks** — following set rules, processing information, filling forms, basic analysis → HIGH automation risk
- **Routine physical tasks** — assembly line work, basic data entry, standardised procedures → HIGH automation risk
- **Non-routine cognitive tasks** — complex judgment, creative thinking, novel problem-solving, emotional intelligence → LOWER automation risk (for now)
- **Non-routine physical tasks** — plumbing, electrical work, caregiving, childcare → LOWER automation risk (requires dexterity and presence)

So a radiologist (doctor who reads medical scans) — AI can now read certain types of scans with high accuracy. But the radiologist also does: complex case interpretation, communicating results to frightened patients, managing edge cases. The routine part gets automated. The high-judgment part becomes more important.

This pattern repeats across almost every profession."

---

## WHICH JOBS ARE MOST AFFECTED (3:30–5:30)

**[HOST, examples]**

"Let's look at specific sectors honestly.

**High exposure to automation:**
- Data entry and basic administrative roles
- Basic paralegal and accounting work
- Customer service scripts and tier-1 support
- Basic journalism (sports results, financial summaries, weather)
- Entry-level coding (junior developer tasks)

**Moderate exposure — some tasks automated, humans remain essential:**
- Teaching (AI can tutor, but relationships and emotional support matter hugely)
- Medicine (AI assists diagnosis, but judgment, ethics, patient relationships remain human)
- Law (AI handles research and drafting, but courtroom judgment and client relationships remain)
- Architecture and design (AI generates options, but client vision and contextual judgment matter)

**Lower exposure — or genuinely growing:**
- Skilled trades: plumbers, electricians, construction — require presence, dexterity, contextual judgment
- Caregiving: nursing, social work, childcare — the human relationship *is* the service
- Creative direction and curation — knowing what's *good* and why is still human
- AI oversight and ethics — someone needs to check the AI

**The honest reality:** Almost every knowledge-work job will change significantly. The question is whether you're the person *using* the AI tools effectively — or competing against them."

---

## THE SKILLS THAT MATTER (5:30–7:30)

**[HOST, building a list]**

"So what should you be developing now?

**1. AI fluency** — not programming, but understanding how to use AI tools effectively. You're doing that by taking this course.

**2. Judgment** — knowing which AI output is good and which is wrong. AI doesn't know when it's wrong. You need to.

**3. Communication** — written, verbal, across cultures and contexts. AI can generate text, but it can't build a real relationship or read a room. Strong communication remains a moat.

**4. Domain expertise** — being deeply knowledgeable in a specific area makes you much better at using AI tools in that area, and much better at catching its mistakes.

**5. Emotional intelligence** — empathy, conflict resolution, motivation, trust-building. These are genuinely hard to automate because they require reading people, not data.

**6. Creativity and direction** — not just executing, but deciding what to create, what the goal is, what 'good' looks like. AI needs a brief. You write the brief.

**7. Learning agility** — the ability to learn new things quickly. The tools will change. The person who can adapt will always have an edge.

The common thread: **things AI cannot easily do** — exercise judgment, build relationships, take moral responsibility, set direction, and understand context deeply."

---

## THE REALISTIC PICTURE (7:30–8:30)

**[HOST, balanced tone]**

"I want to be honest with you: this transition will be disruptive for many people. Jobs will change or disappear in some sectors. That's real and it's worth taking seriously.

But history shows that major technological transitions also create new jobs — just not always for the same people in the same places at the same time. The transition can be painful for specific workers and communities even if the long-term picture is fine.

The young people best positioned aren't the ones who ignore this, and they're not the ones who are paralysed by it. They're the ones who look at it clearly, develop durable skills, and stay adaptable."

---

## CHALLENGE (8:30–9:15)

**[HOST]**

"Your challenge: pick a career you're curious about. It doesn't have to be what you want to do — just something interesting.

Use this prompt:
```
I'm exploring [CAREER] as a case study in automation.
Please break down:
1. The main tasks involved in this job
2. Which tasks are highly automatable and why
3. Which tasks are harder to automate and why
4. How this profession might look in 10 years
5. What skills a person entering this field should develop
```

Then: does this change your view of that career? What would you tell someone who wanted to go into it?"
