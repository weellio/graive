## Key Concepts

### How Algorithmic Feeds Work
Social media platforms use AI recommendation systems to decide what you see. The AI is trained to maximise a metric — usually "engagement" (likes, shares, time on platform, comments).

The problem: the content that generates the most engagement is often:
- Emotionally provocative (outrage, fear, envy, desire)
- Extreme or sensational
- Personally validating (content that confirms your existing views)

> "The algorithm wasn't designed to make you unhappy. It was designed to make you engaged. But those two things turn out to be deeply connected."

### The Dopamine Loop
Social media platforms exploit the brain's reward system:

1. You post something → variable reward (will people like it or not?)
2. Notifications arrive unexpectedly → variable reinforcement schedule (same mechanism as gambling)
3. Scrolling delivers intermittent rewards → keeps you seeking the next one
4. Comparison with others activates social anxiety → you check back to see where you stand

This is not an accident. Platform designers have described engineering these features intentionally. Former employees of major platforms have spoken publicly about choosing mechanics they knew were addictive.

### The Research on Social Media and Mental Health
The evidence is complex and debated:

**Evidence suggesting harm (especially for adolescent girls):**
- Studies link heavy social media use with increased rates of anxiety, depression, and loneliness
- A major 2022 Surgeon General's advisory in the US warned about social media's effects on youth mental health
- Internal documents from Meta reportedly showed they knew Instagram harmed teenage girls' body image
- Social comparison effects are amplified when feeds are curated to show peak moments

**Evidence suggesting it's complicated:**
- Correlation isn't causation — unhappy people may use more social media
- Some uses of social media are clearly positive (community, support, connection)
- Effects vary hugely by individual, platform, and how it's used
- Passive scrolling appears more harmful than active engagement with friends

### AI Therapy Tools: Promise and Peril
Several AI-powered mental health apps exist (Woebot, Wysa, others). They offer:
- Cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT) techniques
- Always-available support with no judgement
- Accessible and free/cheap compared to human therapy

Concerns:
- They are not a substitute for clinical care
- If someone is in crisis, AI may not recognise it or respond appropriately
- User data is commercially valuable — privacy questions arise
- The "relationship" with AI therapy feels real to users but is not genuinely reciprocal
- They might reduce the urgency for real investment in human mental health services

## The AI Therapy Question

Read this scenario:

> *Fifteen-year-old Jordan has been struggling with anxiety for six months. They haven't told their parents. They've started using an AI mental health app instead of talking to anyone. The app gives them breathing exercises and CBT techniques. Jordan says it helps.*

Questions to consider:

1. Is Jordan getting real help? What are the benefits of this situation?

2. What are the risks of Jordan relying on AI rather than talking to a human?

3. If Jordan's anxiety became a serious crisis, would the AI be adequate? What should happen?

4. Should AI mental health apps be available to under-18s? If so, with what safeguards?

5. Is there something ethically different about an AI appearing to care versus a human actually caring?

My analysis: ___

> "An AI that says 'I understand how you're feeling' doesn't understand anything. But if it helps someone feel heard and take steps to feel better — does that matter? And does it matter that it isn't real?"

## Challenge
**The Digital Wellbeing Experiment:**

For one week, make one specific change to how you use social media or streaming platforms. Pick something meaningful, not trivial.

Ideas:
- Turn off all notifications for one platform for a week
- Set a hard 30-minute daily limit using screen time tools
- Unfollow 10 accounts that make you feel bad and replace with accounts that make you feel good
- Use a platform's "view my timeline" feature (if available) instead of the algorithmic feed
- No social media after 9pm for a week

Before: Rate your current digital wellbeing: ___/10
After the week: Rate your digital wellbeing: ___/10

Document:
- What was harder than expected: ___
- What improved: ___
- What you'd keep doing permanently: ___
