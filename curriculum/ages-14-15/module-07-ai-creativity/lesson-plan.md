## Key Concepts

### What Do We Mean by "Creativity"?
Creativity is actually a contested concept even among humans. Consider these different definitions:

1. **Originality definition** — creating something that didn't exist before in quite that form
2. **Intentionality definition** — deliberately bringing something into being because you wanted to express something
3. **Emotional definition** — creating from genuine feeling, experience, and inner life
4. **Social definition** — producing something that is recognised by others as creative and valuable
5. **Combinatorial definition** — recombining existing elements in novel ways

**Key question:** By which of these definitions can AI be "creative"?

> "AI can satisfy some definitions of creativity and fail others completely. The real debate is about which definition matters most — and that reveals what we think creativity is fundamentally FOR."

### The Case That AI Is Creative
- AI generates outputs that are genuinely novel — not copies
- AI can surprise its creators with unexpected combinations
- Some AI outputs have been judged as beautiful or profound by human critics who didn't know they were AI-generated
- Human creativity is also "just" remixing prior influences — we don't invent from nothing either
- If the output has creative value, why does the process matter?

### The Case That AI Is Not Creative
- AI has no inner experience, no desire to express anything, no emotions driving its choices
- AI doesn't choose what to create — it responds to human prompts
- AI optimises for pattern prediction, not for expression or meaning
- When AI "surprises" us, it's a side effect of statistics, not genuine imagination
- Creativity requires intentionality — the artist made choices with purpose

### The Philosophical Stakes
Why does this debate matter? Because:
- **Copyright law** — in most countries, AI alone cannot hold copyright, because copyright requires a human creator
- **Cultural value** — if AI floods culture with "creative" content, what happens to human artists?
- **Identity** — creativity is central to how humans understand themselves as unique; what changes if machines share that?

## Try It — Analysing AI Creative Output

### Activity 1: The Turing Test for Art (15 mins)

Find examples of AI-generated creative work to analyse. You can:
- Ask AI to write a poem or short story
- Search for "AI-generated art gallery" or look at Midjourney examples online
- Ask AI to compose song lyrics

For each example you look at, apply these questions:

**Analysis questions:**
1. Is this original? (Have you seen something exactly like this before?)
2. Does it have emotional impact on you as a viewer/reader? What is that impact?
3. Can you tell it was made by AI? What clues, if any, are there?
4. Would you describe this as "creative"? Using which definition from above?
5. If a human made this, would your assessment change? Why or why not?

### Activity 2: The Intentionality Test (15 mins)

This activity probes whether AI makes intentional creative choices.

Ask your AI tool to write a short poem about loneliness. Then ask it:

1. "Why did you choose the specific words and images you used in this poem?"
2. "Have you ever felt lonely yourself?"
3. "What were you trying to make the reader feel?"
4. "Would you write this poem differently if you had a different mood today?"

## The Human Artist's Perspective

Many professional artists feel genuinely threatened by AI creative tools. Their concerns aren't just about jobs — they're about meaning.

A painter who spent years developing a style, drawing on life experiences, processing emotion through colour and form — they see AI produce something superficially similar in seconds, trained partly on their own work.

Questions to sit with:
- Is there something valuable in the *process* of creating, independent of the output?
- If AI generates "better" music than most humans, should humans stop making music?
- What does creating something mean to YOU — why do people make things?

> "The value of human creativity might not be in the output alone. It might be in the maker's journey, the meaning behind the work, and the connection between creator and audience. AI cannot replicate that — even if it replicates the product."

## Reflect

1. Has engaging with actual AI creative outputs changed your view of the debate? Did you find the outputs more or less impressive than you expected?

2. Is there a type of creativity (art, music, writing, humour, science) where you think AI is closest to genuine creativity — and one where it seems furthest? Why?

3. If the best music in 50 years is generated by AI — what does that mean for musicians? Should we care? What would be lost?
