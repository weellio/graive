## Background

The concept of a "personal brand" feels modern and slightly commercial — it is the language of marketing applied to individuals. But the underlying idea is ancient: how you present yourself to the world, how others perceive you, and how you manage the gap between who you are and who you appear to be are questions that philosophers, social theorists, and individuals have grappled with for centuries. What is genuinely new is the permanence and searchability of online information, the speed at which reputations can be built or destroyed, and the scale at which your words and images can travel.

Before the internet, your reputation existed in the memories of people who knew you. It was inherently local, inherently fallible, and inherently impermanent — over time, people forgot, moved on, forgave. A teenage mistake embarrassed you at school but was unlikely to follow you into adulthood. The internet fundamentally changed this. Content you create or that others create about you can persist indefinitely, be indexed by search engines, surface in background checks, and reach audiences you never intended. The concept of "context collapse" — coined by scholar danah boyd — describes what happens when content created for one audience (your friends, in a private moment) reaches a completely different audience (an employer, a university admissions officer, a stranger) who interprets it without the original context.

The response to context collapse cannot be to disengage from digital life — that is neither practical nor desirable. The internet is where careers are built, communities are formed, opportunities are found, and ideas travel. The response is intentionality: being thoughtful about what you create, where you create it, and who you want to reach. This does not mean being inauthentic or corporate. It means understanding that you have agency over your digital narrative and making deliberate choices rather than accidental ones.

Personal branding at its best is not performance — it is documentation. It is creating a record of what you actually care about, what you have done, and what you are working toward. A student who writes about things they are genuinely interested in, shares projects they are actually working on, and engages thoughtfully in communities they are part of is building something real and durable. This is different from the manufactured personal brand — the curated perfection designed to impress — which tends to be fragile, unsustainable, and often counterproductive because sophisticated audiences can detect inauthenticity.

There is also the question of privacy and safety. Building any online presence involves a careful calculation about what to share and what to protect. For young people especially, this calculation involves real risks: unwanted attention, harassment, exploitation of personal information, and the long-term consequences of sharing things that feel important now but may be embarrassing or harmful later. The skills of understanding platform privacy settings, knowing what not to share, and maintaining the ability to disengage when something goes wrong are as important as the skills of building a positive presence.

AI adds a new dimension to digital identity questions. AI-generated deepfakes and synthetic media can put words in your mouth you never said or images of your face in contexts you never consented to. AI tools can synthesise information about you from multiple sources, creating profiles more detailed than you would ever construct yourself. And AI-assisted communication can make it harder to know whether you are talking to a real person or an automated system impersonating one. Navigating digital identity in the age of AI requires being sophisticated about all of these possibilities.

## The Big Picture

Your digital presence will matter in almost every path you might take: applying to universities, finding jobs, building a business, establishing yourself as a professional or creative in any field. Increasingly, the people evaluating you — admissions officers, hiring managers, clients, collaborators — will search for you online as a matter of course. What they find will shape their perception of you. More positively: a well-built digital presence can open doors that traditional credentials would not. A young person who has documented genuine expertise, contributed to communities, and demonstrated real thinking over time has an advantage over someone with equivalent formal credentials but no digital presence at all.

## Key Terms

> **Digital footprint:** The collection of data that exists about you online — what you have created, what others have created about you, and what platforms have recorded about your behaviour.
>
> **Context collapse:** The situation where content created for one audience reaches a very different audience with different norms, expectations, and interpretations.
>
> **Personal brand:** The impression you create and the reputation you build through your online presence, work, and communication — whether intentional or not.
>
> **Parasocial relationship:** A one-sided emotional connection — the feeling of knowing someone (a creator, celebrity, or influencer) who does not know you exist. Relevant to how audiences relate to online personas.
>
> **Deepfake:** Synthetic media — typically video or audio — generated by AI to realistically depict someone saying or doing things they never did.
>
> **Digital legacy:** The online presence, data, and content that persists after someone changes, moves on, or dies.
>
> **Authenticity:** In online contexts, the quality of genuinely representing yourself rather than performing a constructed persona — though the relationship between the "authentic self" and the "presented self" is philosophically complex.

### Part 2: The Audit of Your Social Presence (10 mins)

Pick your most active social platform. Scroll back through your last 30 posts/interactions.

Ask yourself honestly:
- Is there anything here I would be embarrassed by in 5 years?
- Am I proud of anything here?
- Does this represent who I actually am or a version I was performing?

No need to share this — it is just for you. But note: is there anything you want to clean up?

### Part 3: The Digital Identity Vision (20 mins)

Use AI as a thinking partner to help you articulate what you actually want:

**Prompt:**
```
I want to think about my digital identity and online presence.
I'm 14-15 years old and interested in [YOUR INTERESTS — be specific].
Help me think through:
1. What would a genuine, useful online presence look like for someone with my interests?
2. What platforms would make most sense and why?
3. What could I create or share that would be genuinely useful to others?
4. What kind of reputation would I want to have built by the time I'm 18?
```

## Deep Dive — Case Studies in Digital Identity

This activity asks you to study how real people have navigated digital identity — both successfully and catastrophically.

**Choose ONE of the following research tasks:**

**Option A — The Comeback Story:**
Research a public figure (creator, athlete, politician, entrepreneur) who had a major public online controversy and either recovered or did not recover their reputation. Try to find someone whose situation you can study in reasonable depth.

Questions to answer:
1. What happened? What did they say or do, and how did it spread?
2. How did they respond (if they did)? Was the response effective?
3. How did different audiences react — sympathetically, harshly, or with complexity?
4. What does this case teach you about the permanence of online content and the possibility of reputation recovery?

**Option B — The Builder:**
Research someone who built a meaningful online presence starting from nothing in their teens or early twenties — a creator, entrepreneur, or activist who documented their journey from early on. (Examples: many YouTubers who started as teenagers, Greta Thunberg's use of social media in activism, young entrepreneurs who built audiences around specific skills.)

Questions to answer:
1. When and how did they start?
2. What did their early content look like versus their later work?
3. What was the turning point — when did they get serious traction?
4. What decisions did they make that seem important in retrospect?
5. What can you learn from their approach?

After your research, write a 200-word reflection: What is the most important insight from this case study for your own digital identity planning?

## Level Up — The 5-Year LinkedIn Thought Experiment

Imagine you are 19 or 20, applying for a job, university place, or grant in something related to your current interests.

Write the "About" section of a LinkedIn profile or portfolio introduction that you would want to have by then.

Use AI to help you:
```
I'm 15. I'm interested in [INTERESTS/GOALS]. Help me write an "About Me" draft for what I'd want my profile to say when I'm 19, as if I'd had 4 years to build a real presence and body of work in this area. What would the ideal version of my journey look like? Write it in first person as if I'm 19 looking back.
```

This is a vision document. Now: what would you need to do in the next 4 years to make it true?

**Extension:** Research the concept of a "portfolio career" — where instead of a single employer or single job title, you build a body of work across multiple areas. Is this a model that appeals to you? What would your portfolio career look like?

## Debate This

1. **Is "personal branding" inherently inauthentic?** Critics argue that treating yourself as a brand requires performing rather than being — that you inevitably present a curated version of yourself rather than the real one. Defenders argue that we always present different aspects of ourselves in different contexts, and that being thoughtful about it is just self-awareness. Who is right?

2. **Should employers and universities be allowed to check applicants' social media?** On one hand, what you post publicly is public. On the other, social media content from your teenage years may not accurately represent who you are as an adult — and systematic screening may disadvantage people from cultures that use social media differently. Where should the line be?

3. **Should platforms be required to give users the "right to be forgotten" — the ability to delete their data and have it removed from search engines?** The EU has this as a legal right; many other countries do not. Is this a privacy protection or a way for people to escape accountability for things they actually did?

## Reflect

1. Before this module, how consciously were you thinking about your digital footprint? What has changed?

2. What is the difference between "performing" a personal brand and genuinely building one? Can you give an example of each?

3. Is it possible to be authentic *and* strategic at the same time? Or does strategy undermine authenticity?

4. Context collapse means your posts can reach audiences you never intended. Does knowing this change what you post, or how you post it? Should it?

5. If you met your 25-year-old self, what would they wish you had started doing online at 15? What would they wish you had never done?
