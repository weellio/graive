# Video Script: Personal Brand & Digital Identity
### Thinker Level (Ages 14–15) | Module 06 | ~8 minutes

---

## HOOK (0:00–0:45)

**[HOST on camera]**

"Your digital footprint is already being built — whether you're intentional about it or not.

Everything you post, comment on, share, and create online is leaving a trace. And that trace will exist for years, possibly permanently.

But here's the thing: that's not necessarily a problem. It's actually an opportunity — if you approach it with some intention.

Today we're talking about digital identity. Who you are online. How you can shape it. And what you should think about before you post."

**[TITLE CARD: "Personal Brand & Digital Identity"]**

---

## YOUR DIGITAL FOOTPRINT (0:45–2:30)

**[HOST]**

"Your digital footprint is the trail of data you leave online. It has two parts:

**Active footprint:** things you deliberately create — posts, comments, accounts, content you publish.

**Passive footprint:** data collected without you posting anything — browsing history, location data, purchase patterns, searches.

The passive footprint we discussed in Module 6 of Builder Level. Today we're focusing on the active one — because that's the part you can most intentionally shape.

The key thing to understand: **content doesn't disappear.** Screenshots exist. Archives exist. Employer background check tools exist. University admissions processes sometimes include social media review.

This doesn't mean you should live in fear of what you post. It means: post as if it might be seen by the most important person you'll meet in ten years. Because it might be."

---

## WHAT IS A PERSONAL BRAND? (2:30–4:30)

**[HOST]**

"Personal brand sounds like something for influencers or CEOs. But at its core, it's simple: it's what you're known for and what people think of when they hear your name.

Online, you have more control over this than ever. You can build a reputation, a body of work, a network — before you've even left school.

Some examples of what a genuine personal brand can look like at your age:

- A YouTube channel where you document learning a skill
- A written thread or newsletter where you share what you're reading or thinking about
- A portfolio of projects — art, code, writing, music
- Thoughtful participation in online communities around things you care about

None of these require millions of followers. A small, genuine reputation in a specific area is worth far more than a large, generic presence.

The question to ask yourself: **What do I want to be known for? What value do I want to offer?**"

---

## THE AUTHENTICITY QUESTION (4:30–6:00)

**[HOST, more conversational]**

"Here's the uncomfortable bit about personal branding: done badly, it becomes performance. You start performing who you want people to think you are instead of being who you actually are.

This is exhausting and ultimately self-defeating. Because the most durable reputations are built on genuine expertise, genuine curiosity, and genuine character — not curated aesthetics.

The people who do this well typically:
- Share what they're genuinely learning, not what they think will get engagement
- Are honest when they don't know something
- Take real positions rather than staying vague to avoid criticism
- Let their actual personality show — the weird interests, the genuine humour, the real opinions

Authenticity isn't a strategy. But it happens to also be good strategy."

---

## USING AI FOR YOUR DIGITAL PRESENCE (6:00–7:00)

**[HOST]**

"A quick note on AI and personal expression. You can use AI to:
- Help you articulate ideas you have but can't quite express yet
- Draft and edit writing to make your actual thoughts clearer
- Brainstorm what to create or write about
- Research topics you want to share perspectives on

What you shouldn't do is use AI to generate an online persona that isn't really you — AI-written posts that sound like someone you're not. Not because it's dishonest (though it is), but because it doesn't build the skills, relationships, or reputation that actually matter.

The point of building an online presence is to develop *yourself* — your thinking, your communication, your expertise. If AI is doing all of that, you're outsourcing your own development."

---

## CHALLENGE (7:00–7:40)

**[HOST]**

"Your challenge: Google yourself. Actually do it.

Then answer:
1. What comes up? What story does that tell about you?
2. Is there anything there you'd want to change or remove?
3. If someone who could open a door for you in 5 years searched your name — what would you want them to find?

Then: write one paragraph describing the digital presence you want to intentionally build over the next year. What would you post about? Where? What would the point be?

You don't have to act on it yet. Just articulate it."

---
