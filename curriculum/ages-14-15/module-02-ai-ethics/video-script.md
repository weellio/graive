"In 2018, Amazon scrapped an AI hiring tool they'd spent years building. It had been trained on CVs submitted to the company over ten years — and it had learned to downgrade CVs that included the word 'women's.' As in 'women's chess club' or 'women's college.'

Nobody programmed it to do that. The AI learned it from patterns in the data — patterns that reflected a decade of human hiring decisions.

The tool wasn't evil. It wasn't designed to discriminate. It just faithfully learned from data that already had discrimination baked into it.

That's the core of today's topic. And it's one of the most important things you can understand about AI."

## CONCEPT: Where Bias Comes From (0:50–4:00)

**[HOST, animated examples]**

"AI bias isn't a bug or a glitch. It's a consequence of how AI learns.

Remember: AI learns patterns from data. If the data reflects existing inequalities, the AI will learn those inequalities. If certain groups are underrepresented in the training data, the AI will perform worse for those groups.

Three main sources of bias:

**1. Training data bias**
If the data doesn't represent all groups equally, the model will be better at serving some groups than others.

Classic example: early facial recognition systems were trained mostly on lighter-skinned faces. Result: they were significantly less accurate at identifying darker-skinned people — especially women of colour. This led to real-world mistakes — including false arrests when used by police.

**2. Label bias**
Even with diverse data, if humans *label* it incorrectly, those errors get baked in. If human reviewers have unconscious biases, those biases become the training signal.

**3. Feedback loop bias**
This is subtle and dangerous. If a biased system affects outcomes (like hiring or loan approval), those outcomes become new data — which trains the next version of the system — which continues the pattern.

Example: if a lending algorithm approves loans at lower rates for a certain neighbourhood (maybe for historical reasons), fewer people there get loans. Less borrowing history → seen as higher risk → fewer approved loans. The bias compounds.

## REAL CASES (4:00–6:30)

**[HOST, case study graphics]**

"Let me walk you through three documented cases so this isn't just abstract.

**Case 1: Healthcare prediction (US)**
A widely-used algorithm for predicting which patients need extra medical care was found to systematically underestimate the needs of Black patients. Why? The algorithm used 'healthcare costs' as a proxy for 'health needs' — but Black patients in the US spend less on healthcare due to a range of historical and structural reasons, even when equally ill. The model learned to predict lower need where there was actually equal or greater need.

**Case 2: Image generation**
Ask AI image generators to create 'a CEO' or 'a judge' or 'a nurse.' What do you get? For years, the defaults consistently reflected stereotypes — mostly older white men for leadership roles, mostly women for caregiving roles. The AI had learned those associations from millions of internet images. It wasn't inventing the bias — it was *amplifying* what already existed.

**Case 3: Language translation**
Some AI translation systems, when translating from gender-neutral languages like Turkish, would default to 'he' for 'doctor' and 'she' for 'nurse.' Not because anyone told it to — because that's what the training data reflected.

**The pattern:** AI doesn't create bias. It learns it. And then it scales it."

## WHO IS RESPONSIBLE? (6:30–8:00)

**[HOST, camera — more conversational]**

"This is the question that gets genuinely complicated. When an AI system causes harm because of bias — who's at fault?

**The data collectors?** Who decided what data to use?

**The engineers?** Who designed the system and chose what to optimise for?

**The company?** Who deployed it without enough testing on diverse groups?

**The governments?** Who allowed it to be used without regulation?

**The users?** Who chose to rely on it?

The honest answer is: usually all of them, to varying degrees. AI systems don't exist in a vacuum. They're built by people, deployed by organisations, and embedded in social structures.

This matters because it means the solution isn't just 'fix the AI.' It's also: fix the data practices, the testing standards, the deployment oversight, the regulatory frameworks.

And it matters for you, because as you become someone who uses, builds, or makes decisions with AI — you'll be part of this chain."

---

## WHAT YOU CAN DO (8:00–9:00)

**[HOST]**

"So what do you actually do with this knowledge?

**As a user:** Be aware that AI tools may perform differently for different groups. Don't assume the AI's output is neutral. Ask yourself: whose perspective is reflected in this? Who might this be worse for?

**As a future builder:** If you ever create AI tools — even just custom prompts or automations — think about who might be disadvantaged by your design. Test on edge cases. Ask who might be harmed.

**As a citizen:** This is increasingly a policy question. AI in hiring, lending, healthcare, criminal justice — all of these have real stakes for real people. Being an informed, critical voice in these conversations matters."

## CHALLENGE (9:00–9:30)

**[HOST]**

"Your challenge: pick one AI tool you use and run an audit prompt.

> 'I want to audit this AI tool for potential bias. It's used for [PURPOSE]. What groups of people might it perform differently for, and why? What would I need to test to find out?'

You're not expected to fully answer this — but the thinking process of asking the question is the point.

Write down: what would you need to test? What data would tell you if it's biased?"
