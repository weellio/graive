# Lesson Plan: AI for Learning (Without Cheating Yourself)
### Thinker Level (Ages 14–15) | Module 05

---

## Objective
By the end of this lesson, you can apply at least three Socratic AI learning techniques to a real subject you're studying, and explain why each technique builds understanding better than passive reading.

---

## What You'll Need
- AI tool
- A subject you're currently studying (school subject, personal interest — something real)
- Notebook or Google Doc
- 60 minutes

---

## Watch First
Watch **Module 05: AI for Learning (Without Cheating Yourself)**.

Key principle: Struggle is where learning happens. Use AI to ask you questions, not just answer them.

---

## Try It — Socratic Learning Session

### Part 1: The Baseline Test (10 mins)

Pick a concept from a subject you're currently studying. Something you've covered but might be a bit fuzzy on.

**Step 1:** Without looking anything up, write everything you know about it. Just free-write for 3 minutes.

**Step 2:** Now ask AI: "How would you explain [CONCEPT] to a 15-year-old? Give me the clearest possible explanation."

Read it. Did you already know most of this? What was new? What did you have wrong?

This is your baseline. Keep it — you'll compare later.

---

### Part 2: The Feynman Technique (15 mins)

Now try to explain the concept back in your own words:

**Prompt:**
```
I'm going to explain [CONCEPT] in my own words. I want you to:
1. Listen to my explanation
2. Identify any gaps, mistakes, or things I'm oversimplifying
3. Ask me a question about each gap rather than filling it in for me

Here's my explanation: [WRITE YOUR EXPLANATION]
```

Go through 3–4 rounds of questions and revised explanations.

**After:** Is your understanding clearer than when you started? What was the biggest gap the AI identified?

---

### Part 3: The Socratic Quiz (15 mins)

Use this prompt:
```
I want to be quizzed on [TOPIC/CHAPTER].
Rules:
- Start with easier questions and get progressively harder
- After I answer, don't tell me if I'm right immediately
- Ask me to justify or expand on my answer
- If I'm wrong, ask a question that points me toward the right thinking
- Only confirm or correct after I've made a genuine attempt
```

Do at least 8 question-answer exchanges.

**Score yourself:** How many did you get right on first attempt? On second attempt after the follow-up question?

---

### Part 4: The Essay Scaffold (15 mins)

You have a topic you'll need to write about (pick a real one — upcoming assignment, exam topic, something from this course).

Use AI as a thinking partner, not a writer:

```
I need to write an essay/piece on [TOPIC]. Don't write anything for me.
Instead, help me build my argument by asking me:
1. What is my specific thesis or position?
2. What are my three strongest supporting points?
3. What is the strongest counterargument to my position?
4. How would I respond to that counterargument?
5. What's my conclusion?

Ask me one question at a time. Push back if my answers are vague.
```

By the end, you should have a complete argument structure — in your own words, developed by your own thinking.

**Now write an outline or draft based on what you built. Don't use the AI chat to write it.**

---

## Level Up — Design a Learning Sprint

Design a 1-week study plan using these AI techniques for a subject you find hard. Specify:

- Subject and specific topic
- Which technique you'd use each day (Feynman, Socratic quiz, What-If, analogy challenge, essay scaffold)
- How you'd measure whether you actually understood it by the end of the week (not AI-generated — what would *you* be able to do or say?)

---

## Reflect

1. How did the Socratic quiz feel compared to passively reading an explanation? Which was harder? Which helped more?

2. Where did the AI find gaps in your Feynman explanation? Did those gaps surprise you?

3. The module argues that writing your own essays matters because "you keep the thinking, not the text." Do you agree? What's your own view on AI and academic work?
