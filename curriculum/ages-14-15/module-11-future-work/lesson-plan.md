## Key Concepts

### What AI Is Automating
AI is most capable of automating tasks that are:
- Repetitive and rule-based
- Pattern-matching (language, images, data)
- High-volume and low-variation
- Physically dangerous or difficult
- Information synthesis and retrieval

Jobs most at risk include:
- Data entry and processing
- Customer service and call centres
- Routine legal and financial analysis
- Basic writing, summarising, translating
- Some types of medical diagnosis
- Truck driving and delivery (with robotics)
- Manufacturing assembly

Jobs less at risk (for now) include:
- Caring professions (nursing, social work, therapy)
- Skilled trades (plumber, electrician — physical problem-solving in varied environments)
- High-creativity roles (novelist, conceptual artist, scientist)
- Leadership and complex organisational decision-making
- Jobs requiring deep human trust and relationship (teaching, pastoral care)

> "The question is not 'will AI take my job?' but 'which parts of my job will AI take?' — and then: 'what's left, and is it the better part or the worse part?'"

### The Historical Parallel (and Why It Might Be Different)
Every major technology wave — the industrial revolution, the computer age — disrupted existing jobs but created new ones. Economists argue this will happen again.

But some economists are less sure this time, because:
- AI is general-purpose (it can replace cognitive work across many sectors simultaneously)
- The speed of transition may be too fast for reskilling to keep up
- New jobs created may require skills that displaced workers don't have
- Inequality may widen if AI productivity benefits accrue to capital owners rather than workers

### Universal Basic Income (UBI)
UBI is a proposal where the government gives every citizen a regular unconditional payment — enough to meet basic needs, regardless of employment.

**Arguments for UBI:**
- If AI displaces large numbers of workers, people need income to survive
- Removes the poverty trap of current welfare systems
- Gives people freedom to do unpaid but valuable work (care, community, art)
- Stimulates the economy through consumer spending
- Reduces the stigma and bureaucracy of means-tested welfare

**Arguments against UBI:**
- Enormously expensive — where does the money come from?
- May reduce the incentive to work, leading to labour shortages
- Inflation risk — more money without more goods could raise prices
- Current small-scale pilots don't tell us what nationwide implementation would do
- Better to invest in targeted education and reskilling than give everyone money

### Human-AI Collaboration Models
Rather than "AI replaces humans" or "humans stay the same," the most likely future is hybrid:

- **Human-in-the-loop** — AI does the work, human reviews and approves
- **AI augmentation** — AI handles routine aspects, humans handle complex judgment
- **Human as supervisor** — humans oversee multiple AI systems rather than doing tasks
- **New role creation** — AI creates entirely new categories of work humans do alongside it

## The Reskilling Challenge

Even if new jobs emerge, there's a reskilling problem: the workers displaced are often not the workers who can easily move into new roles.

Consider: a 50-year-old factory worker whose job is automated cannot easily become a software developer or AI trainer. The skills, the cost of retraining, and the time required are real barriers.

> "Technology optimists often assume that 'the market will sort it out.' History shows that when it does sort it out, the transition involves real human suffering — unemployment, poverty, community collapse — that takes a generation to resolve."
