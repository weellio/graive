# Lesson Plan: Building a SaaS with AI
### Innovator Level (Ages 16–18) | Module 10

---

## Objective
By the end of this lesson, you have applied product thinking to a real AI business idea — you have a clear problem, a defined customer, a minimum viable product specification, a basic monetisation model, and a customer discovery plan you could actually execute.

---

## What You'll Need
- A device with internet access
- A free AI tool (ChatGPT free or Claude free)
- A document tool for your work (Google Docs, Notion, etc.)
- Paper and coloured pens OR a digital document
- About 55 minutes

## Go Deep — From Idea to Product

### Activity 1: The Problem Before the Product (15 mins)

The biggest mistake new product builders make is falling in love with their solution before they truly understand the problem. Let us start the right way.

**The "Pain Spectrum" Exercise:**

Think about different groups of people you know, interact with, or have access to. Consider:
- Small business owners (a local shop, a freelancer, a consultant)
- Students preparing for exams or applications
- Professionals in a specific industry (teachers, nurses, lawyers, accountants)
- Creators (YouTubers, writers, photographers, podcasters)
- Community organisations or non-profits

For each group you consider, ask: "What is one thing they do repeatedly that is slow, tedious, error-prone, or expensive — and that might be automatable with AI?"

The best problems have these properties:
- The person does this task **regularly** (not just once a year)
- The current solution is **painful** — it takes too long, costs too much, requires skills they do not have
- They would **notice and care** if it were solved — they would pay for a solution
- AI **actually helps** — it is not just adding a chatbot to a problem that needs a different fix

**Your task:** Generate 10 candidate problems using the above process. Write them as "Problem Statements" in this format:
> "[Specific person] spends too much time/money/effort on [specific task] because [root cause], which costs them [consequence]."

Example:
> "Freelance graphic designers spend too much time writing client proposals because every one is custom, which costs them up to 3 hours per proposal and reduces the number of projects they can pitch for."

Once you have 10, score each one on:
- **Frequency:** How often does the person face this problem? (1 = rarely, 5 = daily)
- **Pain:** How much does it bother them? (1 = minor annoyance, 5 = major blocker)
- **AI fit:** How well could AI actually solve this? (1 = AI barely helps, 5 = AI is perfect for this)

Multiply the three scores together. Your highest-scoring problem is your best starting point.

### Activity 2: Design the MVP (25 mins)

MVP stands for Minimum Viable Product — the simplest version of your product that a real customer could actually use and that you could actually learn from. The key word is **minimum** — you are not building the full vision, you are building the smallest thing that tests your core assumption.

Take the problem you scored highest in Activity 1 and work through this MVP design framework.

**Step 1 — The Core Value Proposition:**
Write one sentence that completes this: "My product helps [specific customer] to [accomplish goal] by [how it works], so they [outcome]."

Example:
> "My product helps freelance designers to produce client proposals in 20 minutes instead of 3 hours by using AI to generate personalised proposal drafts from a brief, so they can pitch for more work without burning out on admin."

**Step 2 — The Minimum Feature Set:**
What are the absolute minimum features that deliver the core value? For an MVP, aim for 1–3 features maximum. Everything else is a future version.

For each feature:
- Name and description (1–2 sentences)
- Why this feature must be in the MVP (without it, the core value is not delivered)
- How AI specifically enables it

**Step 3 — The Tech Stack:**
You do not need to code everything from scratch. Think about what existing tools you could combine to build an MVP quickly:
- AI models: OpenAI API, Claude API, or an open-source model
- No-code builders: Bubble, Glide, Softr
- Automation: n8n, Make (Zapier)
- Forms and databases: Airtable, Notion, Google Sheets
- Payments: Stripe (free to set up)
- Frontend: A simple webpage, a Notion site, a Typeform

Map out a minimal tech stack for your MVP. You may not be able to build all of it yourself — that is fine. Identify what skills or tools you would need.

**Step 4 — The Pricing Question:**
What would you charge? Common SaaS pricing models:
- **Free trial + paid:** 14-day free trial, then a monthly subscription
- **Freemium:** Core features free, advanced features paid
- **Per-use:** Charge per document generated, per analysis run, per user
- **One-time:** Pay once for access (simpler but less recurring revenue)

Research: What do similar products charge? (Spend 10 minutes searching for existing tools in your problem space — knowing the market price is essential.) What is the maximum amount your target customer would pay per month?

Write your proposed pricing model and price point, with your justification.

**Step 5 — The Unfair Advantage:**
Why would someone use your product instead of an existing one? Common valid answers:
- There is no direct competitor (rare)
- You serve a more specific niche better
- You are significantly cheaper
- You have direct access to the target customers
- Your AI integration is meaningfully better

Write your honest answer. If you cannot identify an unfair advantage, that is important information — it means you need to either rethink the idea or find a more specific niche.

### Activity 3: Customer Discovery — The Five Conversations (15 mins)

The most valuable thing you can do before building anything is talk to real potential customers. This is called customer discovery, and it is the step most student builders skip — and the reason most student projects fail.

**The Customer Discovery Script:**

Write a short script for a 15-minute conversation you could have with a real potential customer. Include:

1. An opening that explains who you are and what you are exploring (not pitching — exploring)
2. Questions about their current experience with the problem (not leading questions — open questions)
3. Questions about what they have tried before and why it did not fully solve the problem
4. Questions about what a perfect solution would look like to them
5. A closing that asks if they would like to stay in touch

Example opening: "Hi — I am exploring whether there is an opportunity to make [task] easier for [type of person] using AI. I am not selling anything yet — I am trying to understand how people currently deal with this. Do you have 15 minutes to share your experience?"

**Your task:**

1. Write your full customer discovery script tailored to your specific problem and customer
2. Identify five real people you could approach to have this conversation (they do not need to be your close friends — they need to be people who actually experience the problem)
3. Write down what you would need to learn from these conversations to know whether your product idea is worth pursuing or not

If you can, actually have one of these conversations this week. Real customer feedback is worth more than any amount of market research.

## Design It!

Create a **"Product One-Pager"** — a single-page document that explains your product idea clearly enough for a potential investor, co-founder, or early customer to understand it immediately.

Include:
- **Product name** (even if provisional)
- **The problem** (one sentence, specific and vivid)
- **The customer** (who exactly)
- **The solution** (what your product does — one paragraph, plain language)
- **Why AI?** (one sentence — what AI specifically enables)
- **The MVP features** (bulleted list — maximum 5 items)
- **Pricing** (your proposed model and price)
- **The ask** (what are you asking the reader to do? Try it? Invest? Give feedback?)
- **Your name and contact**

This one-pager should be something you could hand to a real person at a startup event or email to a potential early customer. Make it sharp, honest, and visually clean.

---

## Reflect
Answer these questions out loud or write them down:

1. The "Jobs to Be Done" framework says customers do not buy products — they "hire" them to do a job. What "job" does your product get hired to do, and what are customers currently "hiring" to do that job instead? What does that tell you about the bar your product needs to clear?
2. Many AI SaaS products are essentially a thin wrapper around an OpenAI or Anthropic API — they add a user interface and charge a margin. Is that a valid business? What are the risks of building a business entirely dependent on another company's AI model?
3. If your product succeeded beyond your expectations — 10,000 paying customers — what would be the biggest operational, ethical, or technical challenges you would face that you have not thought about yet?

---

## Share (Optional)
Send your product one-pager to at least one person outside your immediate circle — a teacher, a local small business owner, a family friend who works in business. Ask them: "Does this problem resonate with you or anyone you know? Would you try this product?" Their honest, non-polite answer will be more valuable than encouragement from friends.
