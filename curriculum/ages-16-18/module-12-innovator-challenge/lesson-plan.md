# Lesson Plan: Innovator Challenge
### Innovator Level (Ages 16–18) | Module 12

---

## Objective
By the end of this lesson, you have shipped something real — an automation, a no-code AI tool, a written work of substance, or a complete product prototype — that exists in the world and that a real person can use, read, or experience.

---

## What You'll Need
- A device with internet access
- A free AI tool (ChatGPT free or Claude free)
- Whatever tools are needed for your chosen output track (see below)
- A quiet space and dedicated time — this is a capstone project
- About 90–120 minutes minimum (many Innovators will invest more — that is the point)

---

## Watch First
Watch **Module 12: Innovator Challenge** with a parent or on your own.

Remember: The hardest thing about building is shipping. Not planning, not designing, not learning — shipping. Putting something in front of real people is where you find out what is actually true about your idea. This module is about doing that, however imperfectly, and learning from the reality.

---

## The Challenge — Ship Something Real

Unlike previous challenges, this one does not have a single structure. You choose your track based on your skills, interests, and what you have been building toward in the Innovator curriculum.

**Choose one track:**

- **Track A — The Automation:** Build a working multi-step automation using n8n, Make, or Zapier
- **Track B — The No-Code AI Tool:** Build a working product prototype using Bubble, Glide, or a similar no-code platform with AI integration
- **Track C — The Written Product:** Produce a substantial written work (guide, report, analysis, essay) that demonstrates genuine original thinking about an AI topic
- **Track D — Open Track:** Propose and execute a different kind of output — a presentation, a video explainer, a research database, a curriculum contribution, a community resource. Must be approved with a parent or teacher.

Read all four tracks below, choose the one that fits you, and complete the full requirements for your track.

---

### Track A: The Automation

**What you are building:** A working multi-step automation that uses AI as one of its components and solves a real, recurring problem for yourself or someone else.

**Examples of valid automations:**
- A weekly news digest: monitor RSS feeds or websites, summarise new items with AI, email the summary to yourself every Monday morning
- A content repurposing tool: take a long-form article, use AI to generate three social media posts from it, save them to a Google Sheet
- A study question generator: take notes from a Google Doc, use AI to generate quiz questions from them, save them to a flashcard app
- An email triage helper: categorise incoming emails by type using AI, move them to the right folder, send a daily summary
- A job application tracker: monitor a job board, extract key details from new listings with AI, add them to a tracking spreadsheet

**Requirements:**
- [ ] The automation has at least 3 steps, at least one of which uses an AI model
- [ ] It has run successfully at least 3 times on real data (not just test data)
- [ ] You can demonstrate it working to another person
- [ ] You have documented: what it does, how to set it up, and what can go wrong
- [ ] You have identified at least one failure case (what happens when a step fails?) and handled it

**Submission:** A working automation link or export + a 300-word write-up explaining what it does, who it is for, and what you learned building it.

---

### Track B: The No-Code AI Tool

**What you are building:** A working product that a real person could sign up for and use, built without writing production code.

**Recommended tools:**
- Bubble (bubble.io) — visual web app builder with AI plugin integrations
- Glide (glideapps.com) — app builder from spreadsheets, includes AI components
- Softr (softr.io) — client portals and apps from Airtable/Google Sheets
- Bolt.new or v0.dev — AI-assisted app generation (more advanced)

**Requirements:**
- [ ] The product solves a real problem identified through the Module 10 customer discovery process (or a new problem you have validated)
- [ ] A real person who is not you has used it and given you feedback
- [ ] The product is publicly accessible (shareable link, not just on your local machine)
- [ ] It includes at least one AI-powered feature (not just AI-generated content pasted in, but live AI integration)
- [ ] You have a basic usage instruction document

**Submission:** A live product link + a 400-word write-up: what problem it solves, who your user is, what you learned from their feedback, and what you would improve in Version 2.

---

### Track C: The Written Product

**What you are building:** A substantial, original piece of writing on an AI topic — one that goes beyond summarising, and that reflects your genuine thinking, research, and voice.

**Examples of valid written products:**
- A 1500-word opinion piece (suitable for a student newspaper, blog, or public forum) arguing a specific position on an AI policy question
- A 2000-word research essay on a topic from the Innovator curriculum — must include proper citations and engage with primary sources
- A comprehensive "Field Guide" to a specific AI tool or technique — written for a specific audience (younger students, non-technical parents, teachers)
- An original interview-based piece: you interview 3–5 people with relevant expertise or lived experience about an AI topic and synthesise their perspectives into a coherent piece
- A speculative design piece: a detailed, researched scenario for how AI will affect a specific industry or aspect of life in 10 years — must be grounded in current evidence, not pure speculation

**Requirements:**
- [ ] Minimum 1500 words of original writing (not AI-generated)
- [ ] AI may be used as a research assistant, outline tool, or writing coach — but the ideas, arguments, and voice must be yours
- [ ] At least 5 citations from credible, non-AI sources
- [ ] A clear intended audience and publication context (where would this be published? Who reads it?)
- [ ] A reflection section (300 words) on what you found and what you believe

**Submission:** The complete written piece + a 200-word "behind the scenes" note describing your process, what AI tools you used and how, and what you would do differently.

---

### Track D: Open Track

If you have a strong idea that does not fit the three tracks above, write a short proposal (200–300 words) explaining:
- What you are building
- Who it is for
- What "done" looks like
- How you will know if it succeeded

Share this proposal with a parent, teacher, or trusted adult and get their sign-off before proceeding. Once approved, execute it to the same standard of quality expected in the other tracks.

---

## Completion Criteria

Regardless of track, your submission is complete when you can honestly say yes to all four:

**1. Is it real?**
Does it exist outside your head and your notes? Can someone else access it, read it, or use it?

**2. Did a real person experience it?**
Did at least one person (who is not you) see, use, or read your work and give you genuine feedback?

**3. Did you learn something unexpected?**
What surprised you — about the technology, about the problem, about your users, about yourself as a builder?

**4. What would Version 2 look like?**
What would you change, improve, or add if you kept going? (This question is how you know you have built something real — real products always have a Version 2 in mind.)

---

## Design It!

Create your **Innovator Retrospective** — a one-page document capturing your capstone experience.

Include:
- **What I built:** One paragraph describing your output
- **The hardest part:** What nearly stopped you, and how you got through it
- **What I would do differently:** Three specific things
- **What I am most proud of:** One thing (be specific — not "the whole thing")
- **What this taught me about building:** Your honest reflection
- **What I want to build next:** Even if you have no idea how yet

Sign it and date it. This is a record of your first ship.

## Final Self-Assessment

Rate your capstone on these dimensions:

| Dimension | 1 | 2 | 3 |
|-----------|---|---|---|
| **Shipped:** Does it exist in the real world? | In notes only | Draft/prototype | Live and accessible |
| **Real user:** Did a non-you person experience it? | No | Shown to someone | Used by someone |
| **Unexpected learning:** Did you learn something you did not expect? | Not really | One small thing | A genuine surprise |
| **Quality:** Are you proud of this? | Not really | It's okay | Yes, genuinely |
| **Reflection:** Did you capture what you learned? | No | Brief notes | Full retrospective |

## Reflect
Answer these questions out loud or write them down:

1. Most people who say they want to build something never ship it. You did. What was the specific moment when you almost gave up — and what kept you going?
2. You have now completed 12 modules across the Innovator curriculum. You have studied AI agents, open-source models, security, product building, and customisation. Looking back, what was the single most important idea — the one that most changed how you think about AI?
3. The world genuinely needs young people who understand AI deeply enough to build with it, critique it, improve it, and shape the policies that govern it. Based on everything you have done in this curriculum, where do you want to go next? Not just with AI — with your life as a thinker, builder, and engaged citizen?

---

## Share

This one is not optional.

Share what you built — the automation, the tool, the essay, the product — with at least two people outside your immediate circle. This could be:
- A teacher who might find it useful for their class
- A local business owner who might benefit from the automation
- A student magazine or blog that might publish your essay
- A community organisation that could use your tool
- A portfolio or personal site where you share your work publicly

Sharing is part of shipping. A product that no one uses is not a product. An essay no one reads is a private journal. The last step of building is putting it where people can find it.
