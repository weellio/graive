## The Landscape: The Global Scramble to Govern AI

The year 2023 marked a turning point in AI governance: for the first time, legislators in major jurisdictions moved from studying AI regulation to actually legislating it. The EU AI Act, passed by the European Parliament in March 2024, became the world's first comprehensive AI regulation — a milestone comparable in significance to the GDPR for data privacy. But the EU's approach was just one of several competing governance models emerging simultaneously, and the divergence between approaches has become one of the defining geopolitical stories of the AI era.

The EU AI Act operates on a risk-tiered framework: AI applications are classified by risk level — unacceptable risk (banned outright), high risk (permitted with significant obligations), limited risk (transparency requirements only), and minimal risk (no specific obligations). The "unacceptable risk" category includes AI used for real-time biometric surveillance in public spaces, social scoring systems, and AI that exploits psychological vulnerabilities to manipulate behaviour. "High risk" includes AI in critical infrastructure, education, employment, and law enforcement — where extensive documentation, testing, and human oversight are required. The Act applies to any AI system used in the EU, regardless of where it was developed — meaning global companies building AI products must account for EU rules.

The United States took a markedly different approach. Rather than comprehensive legislation, the Biden administration's 2023 Executive Order on AI established a framework of voluntary commitments from major AI companies, directives to federal agencies to develop sector-specific guidance, and a requirement for safety testing of frontier models before deployment. The US approach reflects a consistent preference for lighter-touch, innovation-friendly regulation — combined with the political reality that comprehensive federal AI legislation faces significant hurdles in a divided Congress. In parallel, individual US states have been legislating on AI, creating a patchwork of laws on topics from AI in hiring to deepfake disclosure requirements.

China has taken a third path: highly specific regulations targeting particular AI applications (deepfakes, recommendation algorithms, generative AI) combined with requirements that AI systems align with "socialist core values" and maintain state security. China's approach is notable for moving faster on specific applications than any other major jurisdiction, while maintaining strong state control over what AI can and cannot say. The implications for global AI development are significant: Chinese AI companies must develop models that satisfy both domestic Chinese requirements and, if they want international markets, the very different requirements of EU and US regulations.

The UK, post-Brexit, has positioned itself as a pro-innovation AI hub with a deliberately non-prescriptive approach: rather than new legislation, the UK initially announced that existing regulators (in healthcare, financial services, competition, etc.) should develop AI guidance relevant to their sectors, with a central AI Safety Institute coordinating cross-cutting safety research. The UK hosted the first major international AI Safety Summit at Bletchley Park in November 2023, producing the "Bletchley Declaration" in which 28 countries agreed on the risks of frontier AI — a significant diplomatic achievement even if light on binding commitments.

Sitting beneath these national approaches are several genuinely hard technical and political problems that no jurisdiction has convincingly solved. First, the definitional challenge: "AI" is not a single technology but a broad family of techniques, and regulations that are too broad risk capturing everything from spam filters to autonomous weapons systems under the same rules. Second, the pace problem: legislative processes move in years; AI capability advances in months. Any specific technical requirement in regulation may be obsolete before the regulation takes effect. Third, the global coordination problem: AI systems are developed globally, deployed globally, and their harms often occur globally. A regulation passed in one jurisdiction can be circumvented by developing the AI in another. Fourth, the enforcement problem: verifying that an AI system is compliant with requirements about its training data, its safety testing, or its risk assessment is technically demanding — regulators currently lack the tools and expertise to do this reliably.

The people who will shape how these problems are solved are not yet in the rooms where decisions are being made — but they will be within a decade. The students engaging seriously with AI policy today are building exactly the kind of informed civic leadership that governance of this technology will require. Young people who understand both the technical capabilities and the societal implications of AI are the most needed participants in these debates.

## Technical Deep Dive: How Policy Gets Made

Understanding the mechanics of policy-making is as important as understanding the substance of AI policy debates.

**The legislative process.** In most democracies, laws go through a process of: proposal (by government, a legislator, or following a public consultation), committee review (where experts are heard and the bill is analysed in detail), parliamentary or congressional debate and amendment, and finally passage and implementation. The EU AI Act took approximately three years from proposal to passage. The practical implication: by the time a regulation passes, the technology it addresses may have changed substantially. Regulations that specify technical requirements tend to age poorly; regulations that specify principles and outcomes tend to be more durable.

**The role of public consultation.** Before drafting major regulations, governments typically conduct public consultations — seeking input from stakeholders. These consultations are open to anyone, including individuals and student groups. Submissions from well-informed individuals can influence the final text of regulations — not usually by themselves, but as part of a body of public opinion that legislators and civil servants genuinely read. The EU received tens of thousands of consultation responses before finalising the AI Act.

**Standards bodies.** Technical standards — like the technical requirements that define what constitutes adequate safety testing for a high-risk AI system — are often developed by standards bodies rather than in legislation. Organisations like ISO (International Organization for Standardization), IEEE (Institute of Electrical and Electronics Engineers), and NIST (National Institute of Standards and Technology in the US) develop technical standards that can be referenced by regulation. These bodies have formal processes for public input and involve significant technical expertise.

**The revolving door and influence dynamics.** AI policy is also shaped by lobbying and influence. Major AI companies spend significant sums on policy advocacy, employing former government officials and funding think tanks that promote views favourable to their interests. Understanding who is funding a policy position and what interests they represent is essential for critically evaluating policy arguments — including arguments made in this module.

## Case Studies

**Case Study 1: The EU AI Act — Three Years of Negotiation**
The EU AI Act was first proposed in April 2021. The final version passed in March 2024 — three years later, after extensive negotiation between the European Parliament, the European Council, and the European Commission. During those three years, the Act went through significant changes, particularly regarding foundation models and general-purpose AI (including GPT-4 and Claude), which were not addressed in the original proposal because they didn't yet exist in their current form. Tech companies lobbied extensively against provisions they found burdensome, particularly transparency requirements for foundation models and the liability framework. Civil society organisations pushed for stronger protections, particularly on biometric surveillance. The final Act represents a genuine political compromise — stricter than industry wanted, less strict than civil society campaigners sought.

*Analysis questions: What does the three-year timeline of the EU AI Act reveal about the relationship between technological development and governance? What provisions do you think were most important, and what do you think was left out? Who "won" the negotiation in your assessment?*

**Case Study 2: The US Copyright Office and AI Training Data**
In the United States, the Copyright Office has been grappling with whether using copyrighted works to train AI models constitutes copyright infringement. Artists, writers, and musicians filed numerous lawsuits against AI companies, arguing that training on their work without permission or compensation was theft. AI companies argued that training constitutes "fair use" — a legal doctrine that permits some uses of copyrighted material without permission, particularly for transformative purposes. As of 2024–2025, courts were still working through these cases, with no definitive ruling. The outcome will have profound implications for the creative industries, for AI development costs, and for the question of whether artists can effectively withdraw their work from AI training.

*Analysis questions: What are the competing interests at stake in the AI copyright debate? Who bears the costs of different possible outcomes? What would a fair resolution look like to you, and why?*

**Case Study 3: San Francisco's Deepfake Politics Law**
California passed legislation in 2019 requiring disclosure of AI-generated images or videos used in political advertising. By 2024, several states had passed stronger laws, and the federal Electoral Count Act was under debate. The deepfake problem in politics is particularly acute because the harm occurs before the content is verified — a realistic deepfake of a candidate saying something false can be viewed millions of times before fact-checkers respond. The policy challenge is that technically effective detection of deepfakes is still imperfect, and requiring only disclosure (as opposed to prohibition) of AI-generated content leaves significant room for misuse.

*Analysis questions: Is disclosure of AI-generated political content sufficient, or should some AI-generated content in political contexts be prohibited? How would you enforce such a prohibition? What are the risks of over-regulating AI in political speech contexts?*

## Career Paths

**Technology Policy Advisor (Government)**
Government departments dealing with digital, technology, and economic policy increasingly need advisors who can understand AI technical capabilities and their societal implications. Entry pathways typically go through graduate school (law, public policy, computer science with policy interest) followed by positions at regulatory agencies, ministries, or parliamentary research offices. The most valuable combination is technical understanding plus legal/regulatory literacy plus the ability to communicate clearly to non-specialist ministers or legislators.

**AI Policy Lead (Industry)**
Large AI companies and major technology companies employ policy teams — sometimes called "public policy," "regulatory affairs," or "government relations" — whose work includes engaging with regulators, shaping industry positions on legislation, and ensuring company compliance with emerging regulations. These roles require deep understanding of both the regulatory process and the technical systems being regulated. They sit at the intersection of law, technology, and politics.

**Think Tank Researcher / Analyst**
Think tanks like the AI Now Institute, the Ada Lovelace Institute, the Center for AI Safety, and many others produce research that directly influences policy debates. Researchers here combine rigorous analysis with the ability to translate findings for policy audiences. This is a strong entry point for people who want to influence policy without working directly in government or industry.

**Civic Technology / AI Advocacy**
Non-profit organisations focused on digital rights, AI ethics, and tech accountability are significant players in AI governance — organisations like the Electronic Frontier Foundation, AlgorithmWatch, Big Brother Watch, and many others. These organisations do research, campaign, and advocate on specific issues, often serving as counterweights to industry lobbying. Entry pathways are diverse: law, computer science, journalism, social science, and direct community organising are all relevant backgrounds.

## Level Up — The Mock Consultation Response

Many governments issue "calls for evidence" before making AI policy. Find one (search: "[your country] AI consultation" or use a previous one from the EU AI Act process).

Write a 200-word response as if you were submitting to the consultation. Use your policy brief as the basis.

This is exactly what advocacy organisations do — and individual submissions are read.

**Specific deliverable:** A complete 250–350 word formal consultation response, structured as you'd actually submit it: an introduction stating who you are and your interest in the question, a substantive response to the consultation's specific questions or themes drawing on your policy brief, and a clear recommendation. Format it appropriately — consultation responses are formal documents, not essays. Use specific language ("The consultation should consider...," "We recommend that...," "Evidence suggests...") rather than informal language.

## Further Reading

- **"Atlas of AI" by Kate Crawford** — a rigorous, critical examination of the material, political, and social implications of AI, including extensive analysis of how AI is being governed (and not governed). Crawford is one of the sharpest critics of both the AI industry and inadequate regulation. Essential for understanding the perspectives not well-represented in industry-funded policy discussions.
- **"The Alignment Problem" by Brian Christian** — while primarily about AI safety and alignment research, this book provides essential context for understanding why AI governance is genuinely difficult. The technical challenges of making AI do what we want are directly related to the political challenges of writing regulations that achieve intended effects.
- **AI governance newsletters and tracking resources** — the AI governance space moves quickly, and the best way to stay current is through dedicated resources. The Future of Life Institute, the Center for AI Safety, and the MIT Technology Review all produce regular policy-focused content. Several dedicated policy newsletters (Politico's Digital Bridge, Protocol's AI coverage before it closed) track regulatory developments in real time.
- **Primary source access** — the actual text of the EU AI Act, US Executive Orders on AI, and major government consultation responses are all publicly available. Reading primary sources rather than summaries is essential for serious policy engagement. The ability to locate, read, and cite primary regulatory documents is a baseline skill for policy work.

## Deep Reflection Questions

1. Of the five debates (safety, liability, copyright, deepfakes, access), which do you think is most underappreciated or underreported in mainstream media? Why might that be, and what are the consequences of that gap in public attention?

2. The module says "if you build, you govern." What does that mean for the responsibility of developers and entrepreneurs who build AI products that may affect people they've never met? Where does that responsibility end, and where does collective governance begin?

3. What's the tension between moving fast to develop AI — potentially gaining economic advantages, winning geopolitical competition, or solving problems sooner — and moving carefully to reduce risks? How would you resolve that tension if you were a government minister?

4. AI regulation is being made primarily by people who did not grow up with these technologies and who often have limited technical understanding. What is lost when governance of a powerful technology is designed by people without deep knowledge of that technology? What would better representation in AI governance look like?

5. You have now encountered multiple policy positions on AI governance — from the EU's precautionary approach to the US's innovation-first approach to China's state-control approach. Is there a position here that cannot be reduced to "more regulation good" or "less regulation good"? What does a genuinely sophisticated position on AI governance look like?

6. If you designed the AI governance framework for your country from scratch — no existing laws, no existing institutional constraints — what would be your top three priorities? What would you prohibit, what would you mandate, and what would you leave to self-regulation? How confident are you that your framework would actually work as intended?
