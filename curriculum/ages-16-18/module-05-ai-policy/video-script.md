## THE MAJOR POLICY DEBATES (0:45–5:00)

**[HOST, walking through each debate clearly]**

"There are five major AI policy debates everyone should understand:

**1. AI Safety and Alignment**
The central concern: as AI systems become more powerful, do we know how to ensure they do what we intend?

This ranges from near-term concerns (AI that behaves unpredictably, AI being used for fraud or manipulation) to longer-term concerns about very advanced AI systems that might pursue goals misaligned with human values.

Key question: how much do you pause or slow development to ensure safety, versus how much does slowing down cause other harms?

**2. Liability and Accountability**
When an AI system makes a mistake — a medical misdiagnosis, a biased hiring decision, a car crash — who is responsible?

Current law is largely unprepared for this. The EU AI Act (2024) is the most developed attempt to address it, categorising AI by risk level and requiring different levels of oversight.

Key question: should liability fall on the developer, the deployer, or the user?

**3. Copyright and Intellectual Property**
AI is trained on vast amounts of human-created content. Did the creators consent to this? Do they deserve compensation?

Artists, writers, and musicians are suing AI companies. Courts are still working this out.

Key question: when does training on data constitute theft of value, versus legitimate learning?

**4. Deepfakes and Synthetic Media**
AI can generate realistic videos, audio, and images of real people saying and doing things they never did.

This is already being used for political manipulation and non-consensual sexual imagery.

Key question: should synthetic media be required to be labelled? What constitutes harm? How do you enforce this globally?

**5. Access and Inequality**
The most powerful AI systems are expensive to develop and run. Who has access?

If AI dramatically increases productivity, but access is concentrated — does inequality widen? What obligations do governments and companies have?

Key question: should advanced AI be a public good, like roads, or a private good, like cars?"

## THE EU AI ACT (5:00–6:30)

**[HOST]**

"The EU AI Act is the world's most comprehensive AI regulation as of 2024. It's worth understanding because it will affect AI globally — any company that wants to operate in the EU must comply, which means global products get shaped by it.

The core framework: **risk-based regulation.**

- **Unacceptable risk** (banned): AI that manipulates people, social scoring by governments, real-time facial recognition in public spaces (with narrow exceptions)
- **High risk** (strict requirements): AI in healthcare, critical infrastructure, hiring, education, law enforcement — must be transparent, auditable, and human-overseen
- **Limited risk** (transparency obligations): chatbots must disclose they're AI
- **Minimal risk**: most AI applications — no specific obligations

This is a genuine governance achievement. It's also not without critics — some argue it goes too far and will harm European competitiveness; others argue it doesn't go far enough on safety."

## HOW YOUNG PEOPLE CAN ENGAGE (6:30–8:30)

**[HOST]**

"This isn't just for politicians and lawyers. Here's how young people can meaningfully engage with AI governance.

**1. Get informed.** Read the EU AI Act summary. Follow what organisations like the Center for AI Safety, the AI Now Institute, and the Future of Life Institute are publishing. Follow regulators on social media.

**2. Write.** Public consultations happen — governments ask for feedback on proposed regulations. Young people almost never respond. Your perspective is different and valuable.

**3. Get involved in organisations.** There are student AI governance groups, fellowships, and competitions. These often lead to real roles.

**4. Vote.** AI policy is increasingly part of political platforms. Know where candidates stand.

**5. Build responsibly.** If you build AI products, you're making governance decisions. What data will you use? Who could your product harm? What oversight mechanisms will you build in?

**6. Talk about it.** Most people in your life don't know much about AI governance. Being the person who explains it clearly — in school, at home, in community settings — is genuinely valuable."

## CHALLENGE (8:30–9:15)

**[HOST]**

"Your challenge: pick one of the five policy debates and write a 400-word policy brief.

Structure:
- The issue (2–3 sentences)
- The strongest argument for strong regulation
- The strongest argument against strong regulation
- What you think the right approach is and why
- One specific policy recommendation

Use AI to research both sides. Write the recommendation yourself.

This is a real format used in policy — concise, structured, taking a position."
